## 1. General information (PDF p.176) <a id="p176"></a>

All vehicles come with either a five-speed manual or a three- or four-speed automatic
transaxle — all automatic transaxle information is in this Part (manual transaxle
information is in Part A, `07a-manual-transaxle.md`).

A shift lock system is used on **1990 and later models**: it prevents shifting from Park to
any other gear unless the brake pedal is held down, and prevents shifting with the ignition
key in Lock or Acc position. The key interlock portion prevents removing the ignition key
unless the shift lever is in Park.

Due to the automatic transaxle's complexity and the special equipment needed for most
service operations, this Chapter contains only general diagnosis, routine maintenance,
adjustment, and removal/installation procedures. Major repair work should be left to a
dealer service department or an automotive/transmission repair shop — but removing and
installing the transaxle yourself is feasible and saves expense, even if the actual repair
is done by a transmission shop.

## 2. Diagnosis - general (PDF p.176-177) <a id="p176"></a>

*Note (as printed): automatic transaxle malfunctions may be caused by five general
conditions — poor engine performance, improper adjustments, hydraulic malfunctions,
mechanical malfunctions, or malfunctions in the computer or its signal network. Diagnosis
should always begin with the easily-checked items: fluid level and condition (Chapter 1),
shift linkage adjustment, and throttle linkage adjustment. Next, road test to confirm the
diagnosis before continuing. Once preliminary tests/corrections are complete, further
diagnosis should go to a dealer or transmission repair shop. See the Troubleshooting section
at the front of the manual (`00-introduction.md`) for symptom information.*

**Preliminary checks:** drive the vehicle to warm the transaxle to normal operating
temperature. Check the fluid level (Chapter 1): (a) if unusually low, add fluid to the
dipstick's designated area, then check for external leaks (see below); (b) if abnormally
high, drain the excess and check the drained fluid for coolant contamination — coolant in
the ATF indicates an internal radiator-wall failure separating coolant from transmission
fluid (Chapter 3); (c) if the fluid is foaming, drain and refill, then check for coolant
contamination or an overfilled level.

Check the engine idle speed. *Note: if the engine is malfunctioning, don't proceed with
preliminary checks until it's repaired and runs normally.* Check the TV cable for freedom of
movement, adjust if necessary (Section 4). *Note: the TV cable may function properly when
the engine is off/cold but malfunction once hot — check both cold and at normal operating
temperature.* Inspect the shift control linkage (Section 3) for proper adjustment and smooth
operation.

**Fluid leak diagnosis:** most leaks are easy to locate visually — repair usually means
replacing a seal or gasket. If a leak is hard to find: identify the fluid (confirm it's ATF,
not engine oil or brake fluid — ATF is deep red). Try to pinpoint the source — drive several
miles, then park over a large sheet of cardboard; after a minute or two, locate the leak from
where fluid drips onto the cardboard. Carefully visually inspect the suspected
component/area, paying attention to gasket mating surfaces — a mirror helps in hard-to-see
areas. If still not found, clean the suspected area thoroughly with degreaser/solvent, dry
it, then drive several miles at normal operating temperature and varying speeds — visually
re-inspect afterward.

Once located, the *cause* must be determined before proper repair — a replaced gasket with a
bent sealing flange won't stop the leak; the flange must be straightened. Before repairing a
leak, confirm the following conditions are corrected (some require highly specialized
tools/expertise and must go to a transmission shop or dealer):

**Gasket leaks:** check the pan periodically — bolts tight, none missing, gasket in good
condition, pan flat (dents may indicate valve body damage inside). A leaking pan gasket may
mean: fluid level/pressure too high, plugged vent, overtightened pan bolts, warped pan
sealing flange, damaged transaxle housing sealing surface, damaged gasket, or a
cracked/porous transaxle casting. If sealant was used instead of gasket material between the
pan and housing, it may be the wrong sealant.

**Seal leaks:** a leaking transaxle seal may mean: fluid level/pressure too high, plugged
vent, damaged seal bore, damaged/improperly installed seal, damaged shaft surface protruding
through the seal, or a loose bearing causing excessive shaft movement. Confirm the dipstick
tube seal is in good condition and the tube properly seated. Periodically check around the
speedometer gear/sensor for leakage — if fluid is evident, check the O-ring for damage.

**Case leaks:** if the case itself appears to leak, the casting is porous and must be
repaired or replaced. Confirm the oil cooler hose fittings are tight and in good condition.

**Fluid comes out vent pipe or fill tube:** indicates the transaxle is overfilled, coolant is
in the fluid, the case is porous, the dipstick is incorrect, the vent is plugged, or the
drain-back holes are plugged.

## 3. Shift linkage - adjustment (PDF p.177) <a id="p177"></a>

Raise the vehicle and support securely on jackstands. On 1990 and later models, remove the
left-hand engine splash shield. Loosen the swivel nut on the manual shift lever at the
transaxle. Push the lever toward the right side of the vehicle, then return it two notches to
the Neutral position. Move the shift lever inside the vehicle to Neutral. While holding the
lever with slight pressure toward the Reverse position, tighten the swivel nut securely
(illustrations 3.5a — 1987 and earlier models, 3.5b — 1988 and later models; *the lever shape
may differ slightly on some early models*). Check transaxle operation in each shift lever
position — try to start the engine in each gear; the starter should operate only in Park and
Neutral.

## 4. Throttle valve (TV) cable - check and adjustment (PDF p.177-178) <a id="p177"></a>

Remove the air cleaner duct assembly. Have an assistant hold the throttle pedal down while
you watch the TV link in the engine compartment to confirm it opens fully. If the link
doesn't open all the way, have the assistant continue holding the pedal while you loosen the
adjusting nuts and adjust the cable housing position until the cable stopper or painted mark
is the specified distance from the boot end (illustration 4.3 — printed distance: 0-1 mm).
Tighten the adjusting nuts securely, recheck the clearance, and confirm the link opens fully
when the throttle is depressed.

## 5. Neutral start switch - replacement and adjustment (PDF p.178) <a id="p178"></a>

**Replacement** (illustration 5.5): disconnect the negative battery cable. Shift the
transaxle into Neutral. Remove the nut and lift off the shift lever. Unplug the electrical
connector. Remove the bolts and detach the switch from the shift shaft. To install: line up
the flats on the shift shaft with the flats in the switch and push the switch onto the
shaft. Install the bolts, but leave them loose and follow the adjustment procedure below —
the remainder of installation is the reverse of removal.

**Adjustment** (illustrations 5.9a, 5.9b): confirm the switch connector is unplugged.
Connect an ohmmeter to the switch terminals and rotate the switch until there's continuity
between the terminals, indicating the Neutral position — tighten the switch bolts. Connect
the negative battery cable and verify the engine starts only in Neutral or Park.

## 6. Shift cable - replacement (PDF p.178-179) <a id="p178"></a>

Detach the cable from the negative battery terminal. Remove the front and rear consoles
(Chapter 12). Remove the hood latch lever. Remove the instrument panel left side lower trim.
Remove the steering column covers. Remove the center console (Chapter 12) — remove shift
indicator components as needed for access to the upper end of the shift cable. Detach the
shift cable from the shift control assembly. Remove the ECU if necessary for removal access.
Trace the shift cable to the grommet retainer where it passes through the lower firewall and
detach the retainer. Raise the vehicle and support securely on jackstands. Detach the shift
cable from the Neutral start switch (illustrations 6.7a — 1984-1987 models, 6.7b — 1986 and
later models; *the lever shape on some early models may differ slightly*). Detach the shift
cable from its support brackets, then pull it through the firewall from inside the vehicle.
Installation is the reverse of removal.

## 7. Parking lock cable - replacement (1990 and later models) (PDF p.179-181) <a id="p179"></a>

Disconnect the negative battery cable. Remove the front and rear consoles (Chapter 12).
Remove the hood latch lever. Remove the instrument panel left side lower trim. Remove the
steering column covers. Pry out the parking lock cable slide key with a small screwdriver
(illustrations 7.6a — shift lock system details diagram: parking lock cable, stop light
switch, shift lock override button, shift lock control switch, shift lock solenoid, shift
lock control computer; 7.6b). Remove the parking lock cable clips, then detach the cable from
the shift lever (illustration 7.7). Detach the parking lock cable from the key lock cylinder
with a screwdriver (illustration 7.8 — pry up the cable clip). Detach the cable from its
clamps (illustration 7.9 — open the cable clamps), then remove it.

Secure the new cable in its clamps — the cable stopper must be above clamp B. Line up the
lock cylinder protrusion with the hole in the end of the cable, then attach the cable to the
lock cylinder (illustration 7.11). Place the ignition key in the Acc position and the shift
lever in Park. Push the set cable lever up into the correct position (illustration 7.13).
Hold down the shift lock override button and place the shift lever in Neutral — confirm the
set cable lever moves to the correct position, then connect the cable and install the clips.
Pull gently on the cable to remove any slack, then secure it with the slide key (illustration
7.6). Lightly push the set cable lever toward the center (illustration 7.16) and confirm it
drops into the shown position. Place the shift lever in Park. Hold down the shift lock
override button and push and release the shift lever knob — confirm the set cable lever
releases to the correct position (illustration 7.18). The remainder of installation is the
reverse of removal.

## 8. Oil seal - replacement (PDF p.181) <a id="p181"></a>

Oil leaks frequently occur due to worn driveaxle oil seals (or side gear shaft seals on
models with flanged inner CV joints) and/or the speedometer drive gear O-ring. Replacement
is relatively easy — usually possible without removing the transaxle from the vehicle. The
driveaxle/side gear shaft oil seals are located at the sides of the transaxle where the
driveaxles attach — if leakage is suspected, raise and support the vehicle securely on
jackstands; lubricant on the sides of the transaxle indicates a leaking seal.

To replace: refer to Chapter 8 and remove the driveaxles. Using a screwdriver or pry bar,
carefully pry the oil seal out of the transaxle bore (illustration 8.4 — careful not to
scratch the bore). If it can't be removed this way, a special oil seal removal tool (auto
parts stores) is needed. Using a large section of pipe or a large deep socket as a drift,
install the new oil seal (illustration 8.6) — drive it in squarely, completely seated.
Install the driveaxle(s) — careful not to damage the new seal's lip.

The speedometer cable and driven gear housing is on the transaxle housing — look for
lubricant around the cable housing to determine if the O-ring is leaking. Disconnect the
speedometer cable from the transaxle. Install a new O-ring on the driven gear housing and
reinstall the speedometer cable assembly.

## 9. Automatic transaxle - removal and installation (PDF p.181-183) <a id="p181"></a>

**1984 through 1987 models** (illustrations 9.3, 9.4, 9.7, 9.10, 9.11, 9.17, 9.19,
9.24a-9.24e, 9.26a, 9.26b):

**Removal:** disconnect the negative battery cable. Remove the air cleaner and inlet tube
(Chapter 4). Detach the speedometer cable (illustration 9.3, at the speedometer driven gear
housing on the transaxle). Detach the thermostat housing from the transaxle. Detach the
ground cable from the transaxle. Unplug the electrical connector from the Neutral start
switch (Section 5). Remove the upper transaxle mount-to-bracket bolt. Disconnect any
electrical connectors from the transaxle. Detach the TV cable from the carburetor or
throttle body (Section 4). Remove the two upper mounting bolts (illustration 9.10). Support
the engine with an engine hoist — without one, place a 4x4 post between the firewall and the
upper crossmember and hang the engine from it with a heavy chain (illustration 9.11). Loosen
(don't remove) the front wheel lug nuts. Raise the vehicle and support securely on
jackstands. Remove the front wheels. Remove the left, right, and center splash shields.
Disconnect the shift cable from the Neutral start switch and shift cable clips (Section 5).
Disconnect the cooler line fittings from the transaxle (illustration 9.17) and cap the lines
to prevent leakage; detach the bracket attaching the lines to the transaxle. Remove the
torque converter cover. Mark the torque converter to the driveplate for reinstallation in the
same position (illustration 9.19). Remove the torque converter-to-driveplate bolts (turn the
crankshaft pulley bolt for access to each). Drain the transaxle fluid (Chapter 1).
Disconnect the driveaxles from the transaxle (Chapter 8). Remove the starter motor (Chapter
5). Remove the center beam (illustrations 9.24a-9.24e: pry off the protective plastic covers
from the front/rear recesses, remove the front mount bolts, remove the rear mount bolts,
remove the center beam front mounting bolts, remove the rear mounting bolts and detach the
center beam).

Support the transaxle with a jack — preferably a transmission jack, with safety chains to
steady it; a floor jack works too, with a wood block between the jack and transaxle. Remove
the rest of the mounting bolts (illustrations 9.26a, 9.26b — front and rear lower bellhousing
bolts). Grasp the transaxle firmly and disengage it from the engine block dowel pins,
confirming the torque converter has detached from the driveplate. Slowly lower the transaxle
until it clears the vehicle — secure the torque converter to the transaxle so it doesn't fall
out during removal.

**Installation:** confirm the torque converter hub is securely engaged in the pump before
installing. With the transaxle secured to the jack, raise it into position, keeping it level
so the torque converter doesn't slide out — connect the fluid cooler lines. Turn the torque
converter to line up its holes with the driveplate holes — the marks must line up. Move the
transaxle forward carefully until the dowel pins and torque converter engage. Install the
torque converter-to-driveplate bolts and tighten to the Specifications torque. Install the
transaxle-to-engine bolts, tighten securely. The remainder of installation is the reverse of
removal.

**1988 and later models** (illustrations 9.34a — 1988-1989 models, 9.34b — 1990 and later
models): the procedure is basically the same as for earlier vehicles. For transaxle
disconnection points, refer to the accompanying illustrations — labeled components include:
transaxle control cable, ground cable, speedometer cable, dust cover, engine mounting
insulator, exhaust pipe, engine under cover, center member, lower arm with crossmember,
transaxle under cover (1988-1989 diagram); and engine mounting center insulator, snap ring,
engine under cover No. 1, lower crossmember, center member, engine under cover No. 2 (1990
and later diagram).

> *Be sure to support the crossmembers, engine, and transaxle with jacks during the removal
> procedure.*

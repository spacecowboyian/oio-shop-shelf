## 1. General information (PDF p.128) <a id="p128"></a>

Fuel system: rear-mounted tank, combination metal/rubber fuel hoses, an engine-mounted
mechanical pump or an electric pump, and either a two-stage/two-venturi carburetor or an
electronic fuel injection system. Exhaust system: exhaust manifold, catalytic converter,
and a combination muffler/tailpipe assembly. Emission control systems modify both exhaust
and fuel system functions — cross-references to Chapter 6 appear throughout, since
emissions systems are integral with the induction and exhaust systems.

Extreme caution when dealing with either the fuel or exhaust system — fuel is a primary
combustion element; the exhaust system operates at very high temperatures and can cause
serious burns from even momentary contact, and fire potential is ever-present.

## 2. Fuel pressure relief procedure (PDF p.128-129) <a id="p128"></a>

> **WARNING** — gasoline is extremely flammable; take extra precautions when working on
> any part of the fuel system. Don't smoke or allow open flames or bare light bulbs near
> the work area, and don't work in a garage where a natural gas-type appliance (water
> heater, clothes dryer) with a pilot light is present. If you spill fuel on your skin,
> rinse it off immediately with soap and water. Wear safety glasses and have a Class B
> fire extinguisher on hand.

1. Always relieve the fuel pressure before disconnecting any fuel system component, to
   minimize fire/injury risk.

**All vehicles:** (2) Unscrew the fuel filler cap to release fuel-vapor pressure.

**Fuel-injected vehicles only:** (3) Disconnect the negative battery cable. (4) Place a
container beneath the fitting to catch dripping gasoline; wrap a rag around the fitting,
slowly loosen it, and let the pressurized fuel drip into the container. (5) Even though
pressure should now be relieved, place a shop rag over any fuel fitting while loosening it
— loosen fittings slowly to allow residual pressure to escape. (6) Cap or plug all
disconnected lines to keep contaminants out.

## 3. Fuel lines and fittings - inspection and replacement (PDF p.129-130) <a id="p129"></a>

> **WARNING** — same fuel-system precautions as Section 2.

**Inspection:** (1) Whenever working under the vehicle, inspect fuel lines and fittings
for damage/deterioration. (2) Check hoses/tubing for cracks, kinks, deformation,
obstructions. (3) Make sure hoses/clips/clamps attach securely to the underside. (4)
Verify hose clamps attaching rubber hoses to metal fuel lines are snug enough for a tight
fit.

**Replacement:** (5) Use original-equipment replacement hoses/tubing made from exactly the
same material as the section being replaced — substitutes of inferior/inappropriate
material risk a fuel leak or fire. (6) Before detaching/disassembling, note the routing of
hoses/pipes and orientation of clamps/clips so replacements go in exactly the same way.
When attaching hoses to metal lines, overlap them as shown (illustration 3.6). (7) Before
detaching any part of the fuel system, relieve the fuel line/tank pressure (Section 2). (8)
While under the vehicle, check the condition of the fuel filter (Chapter 1).

Dimensions noted on the hose-overlap illustration: hose overlap 2-7 mm (0.08-0.28 in.);
gap/clip clearance 0-3 mm (0-0.12 in.).

## 4. Fuel pump - check (PDF p.130-131) <a id="p130"></a>

> **WARNING** — same fuel-system precautions as Section 2.

*Note: the following checks assume the fuel filter is in good condition — if in doubt,
install a new one (Chapter 1).* (1) Check that there's adequate fuel in the tank; if
doubting the gauge, insert a long wooden dowel at the filler opening as a dipstick.

**Mechanical pump (carbureted models)** — refer to illustration 4.3: (2) Raise and support
the vehicle securely on jackstands. With the engine running, examine all fuel lines
between tank and pump for leaks, loose connections, kinks, or hose distortion — do this
quickly before the engine gets hot (air leaks upstream of the pump can seriously affect
pump output). (3) Check the pump body for leaks (illustration); shut off the engine. (4)
Remove the fuel filler cap to relieve tank pressure. Disconnect the fuel line at the
carburetor. Disconnect the primary electrical connectors to the distributor so the engine
can be cranked without firing. Place an approved gasoline container at the end of the
detached fuel line and have an assistant crank the engine for several seconds — there
should be a strong spurt of gasoline from the line on every second revolution. (5) If
little or no gasoline emerges during cranking, either the fuel line is clogged or the pump
isn't working — disconnect the fuel feed line from the pump and blow air through it to
confirm it's clear; if not clogged, replace the pump.

**Electric pump (fuel-injected models)** — refer to illustrations 4.8a and 4.8b: *Note:
checking with a fuel pressure gauge requires special adapters not normally available to
the home mechanic; it's still possible to determine if the pump is receiving power and
rotating, a good indication it's pumping fuel.* (6) Turn the ignition key Off and remove
the fuel filler cap. With an assistant, put your ear next to the opening, crank the engine
and listen for the pump's whirring sound. (7) As an additional check, pinch the line
coming from the fuel filter (Chapter 1) with your fingers — you should feel pressure in
the line; after listening, turn the ignition switch Off. (8) To confirm the Step 7 result,
find the fuel pump circuit test connector (illustrations); using a jumper wire, bridge the
indicated terminals; turn the ignition switch On — you should hear the pump running and
feel pressure at the fuel filter line. **1987 models:** turn on the ignition switch (don't
start the engine) and bridge both terminals of the fuel pump check connector with a jumper
wire. **1988 and later models:** turn on the ignition switch (don't start the engine) and
bridge terminals +B and Fp of the service connector. (9) If the pump is still not working,
the problem could be: (a) fusible link, (b) fuse, (c) wiring connections, (d) EFI main
relay, (e) circuit opening relay, (f) fuel pump. (10) The fusible link, fuse, and wiring
connections can be checked by the home mechanic (Chapter 12); further checks should be
done by a dealer or repair shop.

## 5. Fuel pump - removal and installation (PDF p.131-132) <a id="p131"></a>

> **WARNING** — same fuel-system precautions as Section 2.

(1) Disconnect the negative battery cable. (2) Relieve the fuel system pressure (Section
2).

**Mechanical pump (carbureted models):** (3) Relieve the fuel tank pressure by removing
the filler cap. (4) Locate the pump on the left rear corner of the cylinder head
(illustration 4.3); place rags underneath to catch spilled fuel. (5) Loosen hose clamps
and slide them down the hoses past the fittings; disconnect the hoses using a twisting
motion; immediately plug the hoses to prevent fuel leakage and dirt entry. (6) Unscrew the
fasteners retaining the pump to the cylinder head, then detach the pump. Inspect the pump
arm for wear; coat it with clean engine oil before installing. (7) Using a gasket scraper
or putty knife, remove all traces of old gasket material from the cylinder head (and pump,
if reused) mating surfaces — careful not to gouge the soft aluminum surfaces. (8)
Installation is the reverse of removal; use a new gasket and tighten mounting fasteners
securely.

**Electric pump (fuel-injected models)** — refer to illustrations 5.10, 5.11, 5.13 and
5.14: (9) Remove the fuel tank per Section 6. (10) Remove the retaining screws, then lift
the assembly out of the fuel tank. (11) Pull the lower end of the fuel pump loose from the
bracket. (12) Remove the rubber cushion from the lower end of the pump. (13) Remove the
clip securing the filter to the pump; pull out the filter and inspect for contamination —
replace if dirty. (14) Loosen the hose clamp at the upper end of the pump, disconnect the
pump from the hose, then disconnect the wires from the pump terminals. (15) Installation
is the reverse of removal — replace the pump unit gasket if it shows any signs of
deterioration.

## 6. Fuel tank - removal and installation (PDF p.132-134) <a id="p132"></a>

> **WARNING** — same fuel-system precautions as Section 2.

*Note: much easier with an empty tank — some tanks have a drain plug; otherwise drive
until the level is very low (don't run it empty — could damage the fuel pump) or siphon
with a siphoning kit.*

(1) Remove the fuel filler cap to relieve pressure. (2) Detach the cable from the negative
battery terminal. (3) Relieve the fuel pressure (Section 2). (4) If the tank has a drain
plug, remove it and drain fuel into an approved gasoline container. (5) Raise the vehicle
and support securely on jackstands. (6) Remove the fuel tank protectors (illustration
6.6). (7) Detach the parking brake cable from the fuel tank strap (illustration 6.7). (8)
Disconnect the fuel lines, vapor return lines, and fuel filler neck (illustrations 6.8a,
6.8b). *Note: fuel lines are usually different diameters so reattachment is simplified —
if in doubt, clearly label the lines and fittings; be sure to plug the hoses to prevent
leakage/contamination.* (9) Support the fuel tank with a floor jack, with a piece of wood
between the jack head and tank to protect it. (10) Disconnect both fuel tank straps
(illustration 6.10) and pivot them down out of the way. (11) Lower the tank enough to
disconnect the electrical wires and ground strap from the fuel pump/fuel gauge sending
unit, if not already done. (12) Remove the tank from the vehicle. (13) Installation is the
reverse of removal.

## 7. Fuel tank cleaning and repair - general information (PDF p.134) <a id="p134"></a>

(1) Any repairs to the fuel tank or filler neck should be done by a professional
experienced in this critical, potentially dangerous work — even after cleaning/flushing,
explosive fumes can remain and ignite during tank repair. (2) If the tank is removed, it
should not be placed where sparks or open flames could ignite fumes coming from it — be
especially careful inside garages with a natural gas-type pilot-light appliance, which
could cause an explosion.

## 8. Throttle cable - removal, installation and adjustment (PDF p.134-135) <a id="p134"></a>

**Removal** — refer to illustrations 8.2, 8.3, 8.5: (1) Detach the cable from the negative
battery terminal. (2) Unscrew the locknut on the threaded portion of the throttle cable at
the carburetor or throttle body. (3) Grasp the throttle lever arm, rotate it to put slack
in the cable, then slip the cable end out of its slot in the arm. (4) Trace the cable to
the firewall, detaching it from all brackets. (5) The cable is secured to the firewall
with a flange and two mounting bolts, removed from inside the vehicle. (6) Detach the
throttle cable from the accelerator pedal. (7) From inside the vehicle, pull the cable
through the firewall.

**Installation and adjustment:** (8) Installation is the reverse of removal. (9) To adjust
the cable, fully depress the accelerator pedal and check that the throttle is fully
opened. (10) If not fully opened, loosen the locknuts, depress the accelerator pedal, and
adjust the cable. (11) Tighten the locknuts and recheck — make sure the throttle closes
fully when the pedal isn't depressed.

## 9. Electronic Fuel Injection system - general information (PDF p.135-136) <a id="p135"></a>

Non-carbureted engines use an Electronic Fuel Injection (EFI) system. The 4A-FE and 4A-GE
systems differ slightly but are very similar in operation and service procedures. Three
sub-systems: fuel system, air system, electronic control system.

**Fuel system:** an electric fuel pump inside the fuel tank supplies fuel under constant
pressure to the fuel rail, which distributes it evenly to all injectors. Fuel is injected
into the intake ports, just above the intake valves, by four fuel injectors. Injector fuel
amount is precisely controlled by the Electronic Control Unit (ECU). An additional
injector, the cold start injector, supplies extra fuel into the intake manifold for
starting. A pressure regulator controls system pressure relative to intake manifold
vacuum. A fuel filter between the pump and rail protects the system's components.

**Air system:** air filter housing, throttle body, auxiliary air valve, intake manifold.
Some models have an air flow meter — an information-gathering device for the ECU. A
potentiometer measures intake air flow, and a temperature sensor measures intake air
temperature, helping the ECU determine injector duration. The throttle plate inside the
throttle body is driver-controlled; as it opens, more air passes through, the
potentiometer opens further, and the ECU signals the injectors to increase fuel delivery.

**Electronic control system:** the ECU (a microcomputer) controls EFI and other systems.
It receives signals from information sensors monitoring intake air volume, intake air
temperature, coolant temperature, engine rpm, acceleration/deceleration, and exhaust
oxygen content — these help the ECU determine injection duration for the optimum air/fuel
ratio. Some sensors and their corresponding ECU-controlled relays aren't contained within
EFI components but are located throughout the engine compartment. For more on the ECU and
its relationship to the engine electrical/ignition system, see Chapter 6.

## 10. Electronic Fuel Injection system - check (PDF p.136-137) <a id="p136"></a>

> **WARNING** — same fuel-system precautions as Section 2.

Refer to illustrations 10.6 and 10.7. (1) Check tightness of all electrical connectors
related to the system — loose connectors and poor grounds can mimic more serious
malfunctions. (2) Check that the battery is fully charged, since the control unit and
sensors depend on accurate supply voltage to properly meter fuel. (3) Check the air filter
element — a dirty/partially blocked filter severely impedes performance and economy
(Chapter 1). (4) If a blown fuse is found, replace it and see if it blows again — if so,
search for a grounded wire in the related harness. (5) Check the air intake duct from the
air mass sensor to the intake manifold for leaks (causes an excessively lean mixture);
also check the condition of vacuum hoses connected to the intake manifold. (6) Remove the
air intake duct from the throttle body and check for dirt/carbon/residue buildup — clean
with carburetor cleaner and a toothbrush if dirty. (7) With the engine running, place a
screwdriver or stethoscope against each injector, one at a time, and listen for a clicking
sound indicating operation. (8) The remainder of system checks should be left to a dealer
service department or other qualified shop, since there's a chance the control unit could
be damaged if not performed properly.

## 11. Electronic Fuel Injection system - component removal and installation (PDF p.137-139) <a id="p137"></a>

> **WARNING** — same fuel-system precautions as Section 2.

**Throttle body** — refer to illustrations 11.6, 11.7, 11.8: (1) Detach the cable from the
negative battery terminal. (2) If coolant hoses are attached to the throttle body, drain
about a gallon of engine coolant from the radiator (Chapter 1). *Warning: wait until the
engine is completely cool before doing this.* (3) Loosen the hose clamps and remove the
air intake duct. (4) Detach the throttle cable from the throttle lever arm (Section 8),
then detach the throttle cable bracket and set it aside (no need to detach the cable from
the bracket itself). (5) On automatic-transaxle vehicles, detach the throttle valve (TV)
cable from the throttle linkage (Chapter 7B), detach the TV cable brackets from the engine,
set aside. (6) Clearly label, then detach, all vacuum and coolant hoses from the throttle
body. (7) Unplug the electrical connector from the throttle position sensor (TPS) — pry
the spring clip up far enough to release the connector plug. (8) Remove the throttle body
mounting nuts/bolts and detach the throttle body and gasket from the air intake chamber.
(9) Using a soft brush and carburetor cleaner, thoroughly clean the throttle body casting,
then blow out all passages with compressed air. *Caution: do not clean the throttle
position sensor with anything — just wipe it off carefully with a clean soft cloth.* (10)
Installation is the reverse of removal — tighten the throttle body mounting bolts to the
torque listed in this Chapter's Specifications (16 ft-lbs).

**Fuel pressure regulator** — refer to illustrations 11.16 and 11.17: (11) Relieve the
fuel pressure (Section 2). (12) Detach the cable from the negative battery terminal. (13)

mounting bolts and detach the regulator from the fuel rail. (17) Installation is the
reverse of removal — use new sealing washers and make sure the regulator is installed
properly on the fuel rail (a cocked regulator will not seal properly).

**Fuel rail and fuel injectors** — refer to illustrations 11.23, 11.24, 11.25, 11.28,
11.30a, 11.30b, 11.32: (18) Remove the fuel filler cap to relieve tank pressure. (19)
Relieve the system fuel pressure (Section 2). (20) Detach the cable from the negative
battery terminal. (21) Detach the accelerator cable from the throttle linkage and its
bracket on the air intake (Section 8). (22) Detach the vacuum sensing hose from the fuel
pressure regulator. (23) Unplug the cold start injector connector and remove the cold
start injector fitting bolt. (24) Unplug the four fuel injector electrical connectors and
set the injector wire harness aside. (25) Remove the pulsation damper and detach the fuel
feed line. (26) Remove the two fuel rail mounting bolts and detach the fuel rail/injector
assembly from the cylinder head by pulling while wiggling it back and forth. (27) Remove
the fuel injectors from the fuel rail and set them aside in a clean container. (28) Remove
the fuel rail. (29) Remove the four fuel rail insulators from the cylinder head and set
aside. (30) If replacing the injector(s), discard the old injector, grommet, and O-ring.
If just replacing leaking injector O-rings and reusing the same injectors, remove and
discard the old grommet and O-ring. (31) Further injector testing is beyond the scope of
the home mechanic — if in doubt about an injector's status, it can be bench-tested for
flow volume and leakage at a dealer or repair shop. (32) Installation is the reverse of
removal — position the fuel rail insulators and spacers correctly (4A-FE engines use two
spacers; 4A-GE engines use three spacers). Use new grommets and O-rings on the
injector(s), lightly lubricated with engine oil. Twist the injectors after installing —
they should rotate freely; if not, the O-rings or grommets may be installed incorrectly.

## 12. Carburetor - diagnosis and overhaul (PDF p.139-140) <a id="p139"></a>

> **WARNING** — same fuel-system precautions as Section 2.

**General diagnosis:** (1) A thorough road test and check of carburetor adjustments should
precede any major carburetor service work; specs for some adjustments are on the Vehicle
Emissions Control Information (VECI) label in the engine compartment. (2) Carburetor
problems usually show up as flooding, hard starting, stalling, severe backfiring, and poor
acceleration — a carburetor leaking fuel and/or covered with wet-looking deposits
definitely needs attention.

(3) Some performance complaints attributed to the carburetor actually stem from loose,
out-of-adjustment, or malfunctioning engine/electrical components, or leaking/disconnected/
misrouted vacuum hoses. Proper analysis approach: (a) inspect all vacuum hoses/actuators
for leaks and correct installation (Ch. 1, 6); (b) tighten intake manifold and carburetor
mounting nuts/bolts evenly and securely; (c) perform a cylinder compression test (Ch. 2);
(d) clean or replace spark plugs as necessary (Ch. 1); (e) check the spark plug wires (Ch.
1); (f) inspect the ignition primary wires; (g) check ignition timing (per the Emissions
Control Information label); (h) check the fuel pump (Section 4); (i) check the heat
control valve in the air cleaner for proper operation (Ch. 1); (j) check/replace the air
filter element (Ch. 1); (k) check the PCV system (Ch. 1, 6); (l) check/replace the fuel
filter (Ch. 1) — the strainer in the tank could also be restricted; (m) check for a
plugged exhaust system; (n) check EGR valve operation (Ch. 6); (o) check the choke — should
be completely open at normal engine operating temperature (Ch. 1); (p) check for fuel
leaks and kinked/dented fuel lines (Ch. 1, 4); (q) check accelerator pump operation with
the engine off (remove the air cleaner cover, operate the throttle while looking into the
carburetor throat — you should see a stream of gasoline enter); (r) check for incorrect
fuel or bad gasoline; (s) check valve clearances (if applicable) and camshaft lobe lift
(Ch. 1, 2); (t) have a dealer or repair shop check the electronic engine and carburetor
controls. (4) Diagnosing carburetor problems may require starting/running the engine with
the air cleaner off — backfires are possible in this state, especially if the carburetor
is malfunctioning, since removing the air cleaner alone can lean the mixture enough to
produce a backfire.

> **WARNING** — don't position any part of your body, especially your face, directly over
> the carburetor during inspection/servicing. Wear eye protection.

**Feedback carburetor inspection** — refer to illustrations 12.5a, 12.5b, 12.6a, 12.6b:
(5) Check the Thermostatic Vacuum Switching Valve (TVSV) with a cold engine: (a) coolant
temperature should be below 45°F; (b) disconnect the vacuum hose from the vacuum switch;
(c) start the engine and check that no vacuum is felt at the hose; (d) reconnect the
vacuum hose. (6) Check the Electronic Air Bleed Control Valve (EBCV): (a) warm the engine
to normal operating temperature; (b) disconnect the EBCV connector; (c) maintain engine
speed at 2,500 rpm; (d) reconnect the EBCV connector — engine rpm should momentarily drop
about 300 rpm; (e) with the engine idling, repeat steps (b) and (d); (f) check that engine
speed does not change; (g) disconnect the vacuum hose from the vacuum switch (illustration
12.5a or 12.5b); (h) repeat steps (b), (c), (d) — check that engine speed does not change.
(7) If no problems are found, the system is okay; any further checks should be done by a
dealer service department.

obtain a new or rebuilt carburetor — readily available from dealer parts departments and
auto parts stores; make absolutely sure the exchange carburetor is identical to the
original (a tag on top of the carburetor or a number stamped on the float bowl helps
identify the exact type). When obtaining a rebuilt carburetor or kit, make sure it matches
the application exactly — seemingly insignificant differences can make a large difference
in engine performance. (10) If overhauling your own carburetor, allow enough time to
disassemble carefully, soak necessary parts in cleaning solvent (usually at least a
half-day, or per the carburetor cleaner's instructions), and reassemble — reassembly
usually takes much longer than disassembly. Match each part with the kit's illustration
and lay parts out in order on a clean work surface. Overhauls by inexperienced mechanics
can result in a poorly-running or non-running engine — use care and patience.

## 13. Carburetor - removal and installation (PDF p.140-141) <a id="p140"></a>

> **WARNING** — same fuel-system precautions as Section 2.

**Removal** — refer to illustrations 13.5 and 13.7: (1) Remove the fuel tank cap to
relieve tank pressure. (2) Remove the air cleaner from the carburetor — label all vacuum
hoses attached to the air cleaner housing. (3) Disconnect the throttle cable from the
throttle lever (Section 8). (4) On automatic-transmission vehicles, disconnect the TV
cable from the throttle lever. (5) Clearly label all vacuum hoses and fittings, then
disconnect the hoses. (6) Disconnect the fuel line from the carburetor. (7) Label and
unplug the electrical connectors. (8) Remove the mounting fasteners, remove the EGR
modulator bracket out of the way, and lift the carburetor from the intake manifold; remove
the carburetor mounting gasket — do not remove the cold mixture heater; stuff a shop rag
into the intake manifold openings.

<!-- DIAGRAM CANDIDATE: p.140 — illustration 13.5 "Clearly label all vacuum hoses before disconnecting them" and 13.7 (unplug the electrical connectors from the carburetor), kind: sequence, depth: gray. -->

**Installation:** (9) Use a gasket scraper to remove all traces of gasket material and
sealant from the cold mixture heater (careful not to damage the heater, and the
carburetor if reinstalled), then remove the shop rag from the manifold openings; clean the
mating surfaces with lacquer thinner or acetone. (10) Place a new gasket on the cold
mixture heater. (11) Position the carburetor on the gasket, attach the EGR modulator
bracket, then install the mounting fasteners. (12) To prevent carburetor distortion or
damage, tighten the fasteners in a criss-cross pattern, 1/4-turn at a time. (13) The
remaining installation steps are the reverse of removal. (14) Check and, if necessary,
adjust the idle speed (Chapter 1). (15) On automatic-transmission vehicles, refer to
Chapter 7, Part B, for the TV cable adjustment procedure. (16) Start the engine and check
carefully for fuel leaks.

## 14. Exhaust system servicing - general information (PDF p.141-142) <a id="p141"></a>

> **WARNING** — inspection and repair of exhaust system components should be done only
> after enough time has elapsed after driving for the components to cool completely.
> Also, when working under the vehicle, make sure it is securely supported on jackstands.

Refer to illustrations 14.1a, 14.1b, 14.1c and 14.1d. (1) The exhaust system consists of
the exhaust manifold, catalytic converter, muffler, tailpipe, and all connecting pipes,
brackets, hangers and clamps. It's attached to the body with mounting brackets and rubber
hangers — damaged/deteriorated parts transmit excessive noise and vibration to the body.
(2) Conduct regular inspections to keep the system safe and quiet — look for damaged/bent
parts, open seams, holes, loose connections, excessive corrosion, or other defects that
could allow exhaust fumes to enter the vehicle. Deteriorated components should not be
repaired — replace with new parts. (3) If exhaust system components are extremely
corroded/rusted together, they'll probably have to be cut from the exhaust system — a
muffler repair shop can remove corroded sections with a cutting torch, or you can cut off
old components with a hacksaw (or pneumatic cutting chisels with compressed air) if doing
it yourself; wear eye protection for metal chips and work gloves.

**Typical exhaust system components** (illustration 14.1d): (1) Gasket, (2) Forward pipe,
(3) Flexible pipe, (4) Catalytic converter, (5) Rear muffler hanger, (6) Muffler/tailpipe
assembly, (7) Front muffler hanger, (8) Intermediate pipe, (9) Hanger bracket, (10)
Integral resonator.

(4) Guidelines when repairing the exhaust system: (a) work from back to front when
removing components; (b) apply penetrating oil to component fasteners to make them easier
to remove; (c) use new gaskets, hangers and clamps when installing components; (d) apply
anti-seize compound to the threads of all exhaust system fasteners during reassembly; (e)
allow sufficient clearance between newly installed parts and all points on the underbody
to avoid overheating the floor pan and possibly damaging interior carpet/insulation — pay
particularly close attention to the catalytic converter and its heat shield.

> **WARNING** — the catalytic converter operates at very high temperatures and takes about
> 30 minutes to cool. Wait half an hour before attempting to remove the converter. Failure
> to do so could result in serious burns.

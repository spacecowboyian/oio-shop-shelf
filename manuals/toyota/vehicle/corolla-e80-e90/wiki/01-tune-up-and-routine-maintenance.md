## Maintenance Operations

### 1 Introduction

(PDF p.28) <a id="p28"></a>

This Chapter is designed to help home mechanics maintain their vehicles with the goals
of maximum performance, economy, safety and reliability in mind.

Included is a master maintenance schedule, followed by procedures dealing specifically
with each item on the schedule. Visual checks, adjustments, component replacement and
other helpful items are included. Refer to the [Component Locations](#component-locations)
diagrams for the locations of various components.

Adhering to the mileage/time maintenance schedule and following the step-by-step
procedures — a comprehensive preventive maintenance program — will result in maximum
reliability and vehicle service life. Maintaining some items but not others at the
specified intervals will not produce the same results.

Many procedures can and should be grouped together because of the nature of the
procedure or the proximity of otherwise unrelated components. For example, if the
vehicle is raised for chassis lubrication, inspect the exhaust, suspension, steering and
fuel systems while under the vehicle at the same time; when rotating the tires, check the
brakes since the wheels are already removed.

The first step in this maintenance program is to prepare yourself before the actual work
begins. Read through all the procedures you're planning to do, then gather up all the
parts and tools needed.

### 2 Toyota Corolla Maintenance Schedule

(PDF p.29) <a id="p29"></a>

The following maintenance intervals assume the vehicle owner is doing the maintenance,
as opposed to a dealer service department. Although loosely based on factory
recommendations, most intervals have been shortened to promote maximum engine/driveline
service life. When the vehicle is new it should be serviced initially by a factory
authorized dealer service department to protect the factory warranty.

**Every 250 miles or weekly, whichever comes first**
- Check the engine oil level (Section 4)
- Check the engine coolant level (Section 4)
- Check the windshield washer fluid level (Section 4)
- Check the brake fluid level (Section 4)
- Check the tires and tire pressures (Section 5)

**Every 3500 miles or 3 months, whichever comes first** — all items listed above, plus:
- Check the power steering fluid level (Section 6)
- Check the automatic transaxle fluid level (Section 7)
- Change the engine oil and oil filter (Section 8)

**Every 7500 miles or 6 months, whichever comes first**
- Inspect/replace the windshield wiper blades (Section 9)
- Check/adjust the clutch pedal freeplay and fluid level (Section 10)
- Check and service the battery (Section 11)
- Check/adjust the engine drivebelts (Section 12)
- Inspect/replace all underhood hoses (Section 13)
- Check the cooling system (Section 14)
- Rotate the tires (Section 15)
- Inspect the brakes (Section 16)
- Check/adjust the engine idle speed (Section 30) ***

**Every 15,000 miles or 12 months, whichever comes first** — all items listed above, plus:
- Check/replace the air filter (Section 17)
- Replace the fuel filter (Section 18)
- Inspect the fuel system (Section 19)
- Check/adjust the engine idle speed (Section 30)
- Inspect the steering and suspension components (Section 20) *
- Check the driveaxle boots (Section 21) *
- Inspect the exhaust system (Section 22)
- Check the manual transaxle lubricant level (Section 23)
- Check/adjust the valve clearances (Section 24)

**Every 30,000 miles or 24 months, whichever comes first**
- Check/replace the spark plugs (Section 25)
- Inspect/replace the spark plug wires, distributor cap and rotor (Section 26) *
- Check the carburetor choke (Section 27)
- Check the thermostatically controlled air cleaner (Section 28)
- Replace the drivebelts (Section 12)
- Drain, flush and refill the cooling system (Section 29)
- If the vehicle is equipped with an automatic transaxle, change the fluid (Section 31) **
- If the vehicle is equipped with a manual transaxle, drain it and refill with new
  lubricant (Section 32)
- Check the throttle positioner system (Section 33)

**Every 50,000 miles or 40 months, whichever comes first**
- Inspect the evaporative emissions control system (Section 34)
- Check/adjust the ignition timing (Chapter 5)
- Check/replace the PCV valve (Section 35)

**Every 60,000 miles or 48 months, whichever comes first**
- Install a new timing belt (Chapter 2, Part A)

**Footnotes:**

\* This item is affected by "severe" operating conditions as described below. If the
vehicle is operated under "severe" conditions, perform all maintenance indicated with an
asterisk (\*) at 7500 mile/6 month intervals. Consider the conditions "severe" if most
driving is done:
- In dusty areas
- When towing a trailer
- At low speeds or with extended periods of engine idling
- When outside temperatures remain below freezing and most trips are less than four miles

\*\* If most driving is done under one or more of the following conditions, change the
automatic transaxle fluid every 15,000 miles:
- In heavy city traffic where the outside temperature regularly reaches 90-degrees F
  (32-degrees C) or higher
- In hilly or mountainous terrain
- Frequent trailer pulling

### 3 Tune-up General Information

(PDF p.30) <a id="p30"></a>

The term tune-up represents a combination of individual operations rather than one
specific procedure. If the routine maintenance schedule is followed closely from the time
the vehicle is new, and frequent checks are made of fluid levels and high-wear items, the
engine will be kept in relatively good running condition and the need for additional work
will be minimized.

The first step in any tune-up or diagnostic procedure to help correct a poor running
engine is a cylinder compression check (see Chapter 2). This check helps determine the
condition of internal engine components and should be used as a guide for tune-up and
repair procedures. If a compression check indicates serious internal engine wear, a
conventional tune-up will not improve engine performance and would be a waste of time and
money.

**Minor tune-up**
- Check all engine related fluids (Section 4)
- Check the air filter (Section 17)
- Clean, inspect and test the battery (Section 11)
- Check and adjust the drivebelts (Section 12)
- Check all underhood hoses (Section 13)
- Check the cooling system (Section 14)
- Replace the spark plugs (Section 25)
- Inspect the spark plug wires, distributor cap and rotor (Section 26)
- Check and adjust the idle speed (Section 30)
- Check the PCV valve (Section 35)

**Major tune-up** — all items listed under Minor tune-up, plus:
- Check the EGR system (Chapter 6)
- Check the ignition system (Chapter 5)
- Check the charging system (Chapter 5)
- Check the fuel system (Chapter 4)
- Replace the air filter (Section 17)
- Replace the distributor cap and rotor (Section 26)
- Replace the spark plug wires (Section 26)

### 4 Fluid Level Checks

(PDF p.30-32) <a id="p30"></a>

> **Warning:** The electric cooling fan can activate at any time, even when the ignition
> is in the Off position. Disconnect the fan motor or negative battery cable when working
> in the vicinity of the fan.

> **Note:** The following fluid level checks should be done on a 250-mile or weekly
> basis. The vehicle must be on level ground when fluid levels are checked.

#### Engine oil

1. The engine oil level is checked with a dipstick located at the drivebelt end of the
   engine. It extends through a tube and into the oil pan at the bottom of the engine.
2. The oil level should be checked before the vehicle has been started, or about 15
   minutes after the engine has been shut off. Checking immediately after driving gives an
   inaccurate reading because oil remains in the upper part of the engine.
3. Pull the dipstick out and wipe all the oil off the end with a clean rag or paper
   towel. Insert the clean dipstick all the way back into the tube, then pull it out
   again. Note the oil at the end of the dipstick. Add oil as necessary to keep the level
   between the L mark and the F mark on the dipstick.
4. Don't overfill the engine — too much oil may result in oil-fouled spark plugs, oil
   leaks or oil seal failures.
5. Oil is added to the engine after removing the cap from the valve cover. An oil can
   spout or funnel may help reduce spills. It takes one quart to raise the level from the
   L mark to the F mark.
6. A consistently low oil level indicates oil leakage through damaged seals, defective
   gaskets or worn rings or valve guides. If the oil looks milky in color or has water
   droplets in it, the cylinder head gasket may be blown or the head or block may be
   cracked — the engine should be checked immediately. If small dirt or metal particles
   are seen clinging to the dipstick, the oil should be changed (see Section 8).

#### Engine coolant

> **Warning:** Do not allow antifreeze to come in contact with your skin or painted
> surfaces of the vehicle. Rinse off spills immediately with plenty of water. Antifreeze
> is highly toxic if ingested. Never leave antifreeze lying around in an open container or
> in puddles on the floor; children and pets are attracted by its sweet smell and may
> drink it. Check with local authorities about disposing of used antifreeze.

1. All vehicles covered by this manual are equipped with a pressurized coolant recovery
   system. A white plastic coolant reservoir located near the battery is connected by a
   hose to the radiator filler neck. If the engine overheats, coolant escapes through a
   valve in the radiator cap and travels through the hose into the reservoir; as the
   engine cools, the coolant is automatically drawn back into the cooling system.
2. The coolant level in the reservoir should be checked regularly.
   > **Warning:** Do not remove the radiator cap to check the coolant level when the
   > engine is warm.
   When the engine is cold, the coolant level should be at or slightly above the LOW mark
   on the reservoir. Once the engine has warmed up, the level should be above the LOW mark
   but below the top of the reservoir. If it isn't, allow the engine to cool, then remove
   the cap from the reservoir and add a 50/50 mixture of ethylene glycol-based antifreeze
   and water.
3. Drive the vehicle and recheck the coolant level. If only a small amount of coolant is
   required, water can be used, but repeated additions of water dilute the antifreeze
   mixture — always top up with the correct mixture to maintain the proper ratio.
4. If the coolant level drops consistently, there may be a leak in the system. Inspect
   the radiator, hoses, filler cap, drain plugs and water pump (see Section 14). If no
   leaks are found, have the radiator cap pressure tested by a service station.
5. If you have to remove the radiator cap, wait until the engine has cooled, then wrap a
   thick cloth around the cap and turn it to the first stop. If coolant or steam escapes,
   let the engine cool down longer, then remove the cap.
6. Check the condition of the coolant — it should be relatively clear. If it's brown or
   rust colored, the system should be drained, flushed and refilled (Section 29). Even if
   the coolant appears normal, the corrosion inhibitors wear out, so it must be replaced
   at the specified intervals.

#### Windshield washer fluid

Fluid for the windshield washer system is located in a plastic reservoir in the engine
compartment. In milder climates, plain water can be used but should be kept no more than
2/3 full to allow for expansion if the water freezes. In colder climates, use windshield
washer system antifreeze mixed with water per the manufacturer's directions.
> **Caution:** Don't use cooling system antifreeze — it will damage the vehicle's paint.

#### Battery electrolyte

1. All vehicles covered by this manual are equipped with a permanently sealed battery
   (except for vent holes) that has no filler caps — water doesn't have to be added to
   these batteries at any time.
2. If an aftermarket maintenance-type battery is installed, the caps on top of the
   battery should be removed periodically to check for a low water level. This check is
   most critical during warm summer months. If the level is low, add distilled water
   only.

#### Brake and clutch fluid

1. The brake master cylinder is mounted on the front of the power booster unit in the
   engine compartment. The clutch cylinder on manual transaxle models is located adjacent
   to the brake master cylinder.
2. The fluid level of the brake master cylinder reservoir should be between the MIN and
   MAX marks. The fluid level in the clutch reservoir should be not less than 0.2 in
   (5 mm) below the maximum level line, or below the minimum level line if one is molded
   on the side of the cylinder.
3. Be sure the specified fluid is used, since mixing different types of brake fluid can
   cause damage to the system.
   > **Warning:** Brake fluid can harm your eyes and damage painted surfaces, so use
   > extreme caution when handling or pouring it. Do not use brake fluid that has been
   > standing open or is more than one year old — it absorbs moisture from the air, and
   > excess moisture can cause a dangerous loss of braking effectiveness.
4. The fluid and master cylinder can be inspected for contamination at this time. The
   system should be drained, refilled and bled if deposits or dirt particles are seen in
   the fluid (see Chapter 9 for brakes and Chapter 8 for clutch).
5. The brake fluid level will drop slightly as the disc brake pads wear down during
   normal operation. If either master cylinder requires repeated additions to keep it at
   the proper level, it's an indication of leakage that should be corrected immediately.
6. If the reservoir is found empty or nearly empty, the hydraulic system should be
   inspected for leaks and bled (see Chapters 8 and 9).

### 5 Tire and Tire Pressure Checks

(PDF p.33-34) <a id="p33"></a>

<!-- DIAGRAM CANDIDATE: p.33 — tire wear diagnosis chart illustrating underinflation, overinflation, cupping, incorrect toe-in/extreme camber, and feathering due to misalignment, with a list of probable causes and corrective action for each pattern. -->

1. Periodic inspection of the tires may spare you the inconvenience of a flat tire and
   can provide vital information about possible steering and suspension problems.
2. The original tires are equipped with 1/2-inch side bands that appear when tread depth
   reaches 1/16-inch, but they don't appear until the tires are worn out. Tread wear can
   be monitored with a tread depth indicator.
3. Note any abnormal tread wear. Tread pattern irregularities such as cupping, flat spots
   and more wear on one side than the other indicate front end alignment and/or balance
   problems.
4. Look closely for cuts, punctures and embedded nails or tacks. A puncture can be
   verified by spraying a soapy water solution onto the suspected area — the solution
   will bubble if there's a leak.
5. Inspect the inner sidewall of each tire for evidence of brake fluid. If you see any,
   inspect the brakes immediately.
6. Correct air pressure adds miles to the lifespan of the tires, improves mileage and
   enhances overall ride quality. A tire pressure gauge is essential.
7. Always check tire pressure when the tires are cold (not driven over a mile in the
   preceding three hours). A pressure rise of four to eight pounds is not uncommon once
   the tires are warm.
8. Unscrew the valve cap and push the gauge firmly onto the valve stem. Compare the
   reading to the recommended tire pressure shown on the label attached to the inside of
   the glove compartment door. Check all four tires and add air as needed.
9. Don't forget to keep the spare tire inflated to the specified pressure.

### 6 Power Steering Fluid Level Check

(PDF p.34) <a id="p34"></a>

> **Warning:** The electric cooling fan can activate at any time. Disconnect the fan
> motor or negative battery cable when working in the vicinity of the fan.

1. Unlike manual steering, the power steering system relies on fluid which may, over
   time, require replenishing. The fluid reservoir is located at the drivebelt side of
   the engine near the firewall.
2. For the check, the front wheels should be pointed straight ahead and the engine should
   be off.
3. Use a clean rag to wipe off the reservoir cap and the area around the cap.
4. Remove the cap and note the dipstick attached to it.
5. Wipe off the fluid with a clean rag, reinsert the dipstick, then withdraw it and read
   the fluid level. The level should be between the HOT and COLD marks. Never allow the
   fluid level to drop below the COLD mark.
6. If additional fluid is required, pour the specified type directly into the reservoir
   using a funnel.
7. If the reservoir requires frequent fluid additions, all power steering hoses, hose
   connections and the power steering pump and rack should be carefully checked for
   leaks.

### 7 Automatic Transaxle Fluid Level Check

(PDF p.34-35) <a id="p34"></a>

> **Warning:** The electric cooling fan can activate at any time. Disconnect the fan
> motor or negative battery cable when working in the vicinity of the fan.

1. The level of the automatic transaxle fluid should be carefully maintained. Low fluid
   level can lead to slipping or loss of drive, while overfilling can cause foaming, loss
   of fluid and transaxle damage.
2. The transaxle fluid level should be checked when the engine is at normal operating
   temperature.
   > **Caution:** If the vehicle has just been driven for a long time at high speed or in
   > city traffic in hot weather, or if it has been pulling a trailer, an accurate fluid
   > level reading cannot be obtained. Allow the fluid to cool for about 30 minutes.
3. Park on level ground, apply the parking brake and start the engine. While the engine
   is idling, depress the brake pedal and move the selector lever through all the gear
   ranges, beginning and ending in Park.
4. With the engine still idling, remove the dipstick.
5. Wipe the fluid off the dipstick with a clean rag and reinsert it until the cap seats.
6. Pull the dipstick out again. The fluid level should be in the HOT range. If the level
   is at the low side of the range, add the specified automatic transmission fluid
   through the dipstick tube with a funnel.
7. Add fluid a little at a time, checking the level until it's correct.
8. Check the condition of the fluid along with the level. If the fluid is black or a dark
   reddish-brown color, or if it smells burned, the fluid should be changed (see
   Section 31).

### 8 Engine Oil and Filter Change

(PDF p.35-37) <a id="p35"></a>

1. Frequent oil changes are the most important preventive maintenance procedure that can
   be done by the home mechanic. As engine oil ages, it becomes diluted and contaminated,
   leading to premature engine wear.
2. A new filter should be installed every time the oil is changed.
3. Gather together all necessary tools and materials before beginning: drain pan, rubber
   gloves, breaker bar, socket (correct size for the drain plug — six-point preferred),
   and a filter wrench (band-type or one that fits the bottom of the filter).
4. Access to the underside of the vehicle is greatly improved if the vehicle can be lifted
   on a hoist, driven onto ramps or supported by jackstands.
   > **Warning:** Do not work under a vehicle which is supported only by a bumper,
   > hydraulic or scissors-type jack.
5. Warm the engine to normal operating temperature (warm oil drains better and carries
   more built-up sludge out with it).
6. Raise and support the vehicle. Move all necessary tools, rags and newspapers under the
   vehicle and set the drain pan under the drain plug — the oil will initially flow with
   some force.
7. Being careful not to touch any hot exhaust components, use a wrench to remove the
   drain plug near the bottom of the oil pan.
8. Allow the old oil to drain into the pan, moving the pan as the flow slows to a trickle.
9. Wipe off the drain plug with a clean rag. Clean the area around the drain plug opening
   and reinstall the plug, tightening it securely (use a torque wrench if available).
10. Move the drain pan into position under the oil filter.
11. Use the filter wrench to loosen the oil filter. Completely unscrew the old filter,
    taking care — it's full of oil — and empty the oil inside it into the drain pan.
12. Compare the old filter with the new one to make sure they're the same type.
13. Use a clean rag to remove all oil, dirt and sludge from the area where the oil filter
    mounts to the engine.
14. Apply a light coat of clean oil to the rubber gasket on the new oil filter.
15. Attach the new filter to the engine, following the tightening directions printed on
    the filter canister or packing box. Most filter manufacturers recommend against using
    a wrench, due to the possibility of overtightening and damaging the seal.
16. Remove all tools, rags, etc. from under the vehicle, being careful not to spill the
    oil in the drain pan, then lower the vehicle.
17. Locate the oil filler cap and pour the fresh oil through the filler opening using a
    funnel to prevent spills. Pour the amount of oil listed in the
    [Specifications](#specifications) into the engine.
18. Wait a few minutes to allow the oil to drain into the pan, then check the level on
    the dipstick. If the oil level is above the L mark, start the engine and allow the new
    oil to circulate.
19. Run the engine for only about a minute and then shut it off. Immediately look under
    the vehicle and check for leaks at the oil pan drain plug and around the oil filter.
    If either is leaking, tighten with a bit more force.
20. With the new oil circulated and the filter now completely full, recheck the level on
    the dipstick and add more oil as necessary.
21. During the first few trips after an oil change, check frequently for leaks and proper
    oil level.
22. The old oil cannot be reused in its present state and should be disposed of at an oil
    reclamation center, auto repair shop or gas station.

### 9 Windshield Wiper Blade Inspection and Replacement

(PDF p.36-37) <a id="p36"></a>

1. The windshield wiper and blade assembly should be inspected periodically for damage,
   loose components and cracked or worn blade elements. Road film can build up on the
   blades and should be washed off regularly with a mild detergent solution.
2. The action of the wiping mechanism can loosen bolts, nuts and fasteners, so they
   should be checked and tightened at the same time.
3. If the wiper blade elements are cracked, worn or warped, or no longer clean
   adequately, they should be replaced with new ones.
4. Lift the arm assembly away from the glass for clearance and detach the blade assembly
   from the arm.
5. Pull the top end of the rubber blade element down until the rubber blade is free of
   the end slot and you can see the replacement hole. Remove the rubber blade from the
   frame and discard it.
6. To install a new rubber wiper element, insert the end with the small protrusions into
   the replacement hole and work the rubber along the slot in the blade frame. Once the
   rubber is in the frame slot, allow it to expand and fill in the end.

### 10 Clutch Pedal Height and Freeplay Check and Adjustment

(PDF p.37) <a id="p37"></a>

1. Measure the clutch pedal height and compare the measurement to that listed in the
   [Specifications](#specifications).
2. If the pedal height is incorrect, remove the lower dash panel and air duct for access.
3. Loosen the locknut and turn the adjustment bolt until the height is correct.
4. Tighten the locknut and recheck the pedal height.
5. Push down on the clutch pedal and use a small steel ruler to measure the distance it
   moves freely before the clutch resistance is felt. The freeplay should be within the
   limits listed in the Specifications. If it isn't, it must be adjusted.
6. Loosen the locknut and turn the push rod until the freeplay is correct.
7. Tighten the locknut and recheck the freeplay. Repeat the adjustment if necessary.
8. Reinstall the air duct and dash panel.

### 11 Battery Check and Maintenance

(PDF p.37-38) <a id="p37"></a>

> **Warning:** Certain precautions must be followed when checking and servicing the
> battery. Hydrogen gas, which is highly flammable, is always present in the battery
> cells, so keep lighted tobacco and all other open flames and sparks away from the
> battery. The electrolyte inside the battery is dilute sulfuric acid, which will cause
> injury if splashed on your skin or in your eyes, and will ruin clothes and painted
> surfaces. When removing the battery cables, always detach the negative cable first and
> connect it last!

1. Several tools are required for this procedure: a face shield/safety goggles, baking
   soda (to neutralize corrosion), petroleum jelly (for the posts), a battery post/cable
   cleaner (wire brush tool), treated felt washers, a puller for stuck cable clamps, and
   rubber gloves.
2. When checking/servicing the battery, always turn the engine and all accessories off.
3. A sealed (maintenance-free) battery is standard equipment on these vehicles — the cell
   caps cannot be removed, no electrolyte checks are required and water cannot be added
   to the cells. If a standard aftermarket battery has been installed, the following
   maintenance procedure can be used.
4. Remove the caps and check the electrolyte level in each cell — it must be above the
   plates. If the level is low, add distilled water only, then reinstall the cell caps.
   > **Caution:** Overfilling the cells may cause electrolyte to spill over during periods
   > of heavy charging, causing corrosion and damage to nearby components.
5. The external condition of the battery should be checked periodically — look for
   damage such as a cracked case.
6. Check the tightness of the battery cable bolts to ensure good electrical connections.
   Inspect the entire length of each cable for cracked or abraded insulation and frayed
   conductors.
7. If corrosion (visible as white, fluffy deposits) is evident, remove the cables from
   the terminals, clean them with a battery brush and reinstall them. Corrosion can be
   kept to a minimum by applying a layer of petroleum jelly or grease to the terminals.
8. Make sure the battery carrier is in good condition and the holddown clamp is tight.
   Don't overtighten the hold-down clamp nuts.
9. Corrosion on the carrier, battery case and surrounding areas can be removed with a
   solution of water and baking soda — apply with a small brush, let it work, then rinse
   with plenty of clean water.
10. Any metal parts damaged by corrosion should be coated with a zinc-based primer, then
    painted.
11. Additional information on the battery, charging and jump starting can be found in the
    front of this manual and in Chapter 5.
12. Always replace the battery with a like kind and size.

### 12 Drivebelt Check, Adjustment and Replacement

(PDF p.38-39) <a id="p38"></a>

> **Warning:** The electric cooling fan can activate at any time. Disconnect the fan
> motor or negative battery cable when working in the vicinity of the fan.

**Check**

1. The alternator, power steering and air conditioning compressor drivebelts ("fan"
   belts) are located at the right (passenger's) side of the engine compartment. Since
   they stretch and deteriorate as they get older, inspect them periodically.
2. The number of belts depends on the accessories installed. One belt drives the
   alternator and water pump; air conditioning and power steering, if equipped, are
   driven by separate belts.
3. With the engine off, check each belt with a flashlight for separation of the adhesive
   rubber on both sides of the core, core separation from the belt side, a severed core,
   separation of the ribs from the adhesive rubber, cracking or separation of the ribs,
   and torn or worn ribs or cracks in the inner ridges of the ribs. Also check for fraying
   and glazing (a shiny appearance). Inspect both sides of the belt — twist it to check
   the underside.

**Drive belt tension** — see [Accessory drivebelt tension](#specifications) in the
Specifications.

4. To check the tension of each belt, install a belt tension gauge and compare your
   measurement to the used-belt tension listed in the Specifications.
   > **Note:** A "used" belt is defined as any belt operated more than five minutes on
   > the engine; run a new belt for at least five minutes and readjust it.
5. If you don't have a tension gauge, the rule-of-thumb method: push firmly on the belt
   with your thumb at a point halfway between the pulleys and note the deflection. The
   belt should deflect 1/4-inch if the pulley-center-to-pulley-center distance is between
   7 and 11 inches; 1/2-inch if the distance is between 12 and 16 inches.

**Adjustment**

6. If the alternator or power steering pump belt must be adjusted, loosen the adjustment
   bolt that secures the alternator or power steering pump to the slotted bracket and
   pivot the component (away from the block to tighten, toward the block to loosen). It's
   helpful to lever the component with a large pry bar while adjusting. Be very careful
   not to damage the aluminum housing of the alternator or power steering pump.
7. If the air conditioner compressor drivebelt must be adjusted, locate the idler pulley
   on the front right corner of the block, just above the compressor. Loosen the idler
   pulley locknut and turn the idler pulley adjuster bolt, then tighten the locknut.

**Replacement**

8. To replace a belt, follow the adjustment procedures above but slip the belt off the
   crankshaft pulley and remove it. If replacing the power steering or air conditioning
   compressor belt, remove the alternator belt first because of how they're arranged on
   the pulleys. Because belts tend to wear out together, it's a good idea to replace both
   at the same time. Mark each belt and its pulley groove so replacements go on correctly.
9. Take the old belts to the parts store for a direct comparison of length, width and
   design.
10. After replacing a new-style V-ribbed drivebelt, make sure it fits properly and is
    centered in the ribbed grooves.
11. Adjust the belt(s) as described above, setting belt tension to the new-belt setting
    listed in the Specifications.

### 13 Underhood Hose Check and Replacement

(PDF p.39-40) <a id="p39"></a>

> **Caution:** Replacement of air conditioning hoses should be left to a dealer service
> department or air conditioning shop that has the equipment to depressurize the system
> safely. Never remove air conditioning components or hoses until the system has been
> depressurized.

**General**

1. High temperatures in the engine compartment can cause deterioration of the rubber and
   plastic hoses used for engine, accessory and emission systems. Periodically inspect
   for cracks, loose clamps, material hardening and leaks.
2. Cooling system hose information is in Section 14.
3. Where clamps are used, check that they haven't lost their tension. Where clamps
   aren't used, make sure the hose hasn't expanded and/or hardened where it slips over
   the fitting.

**Vacuum hoses**

4. Vacuum hoses, especially in the emissions system, are often color coded or identified
   by colored stripes. When replacing hoses, be sure the new ones are made of the same
   material.
5. Often the only effective way to check a hose is to remove it completely. Label hoses
   and fittings if more than one is removed.
6. Include any plastic T-fittings in the check.
7. A small piece of vacuum hose (1/4-inch inside diameter) can be used as a stethoscope
   to detect vacuum leaks by listening for a hissing sound.
   > **Warning:** Be very careful not to come into contact with moving engine components
   > such as the drivebelts and cooling fan while probing.

**Fuel hose**

> **Warning:** Gasoline is extremely flammable, so take extra precautions when you work
> on any part of the fuel system. Don't smoke or allow open flames or bare light bulbs
> near the work area. Wear safety glasses and have a Class B fire extinguisher on hand.

8. Check all fuel lines for deterioration and chafing, especially at bends and near
   fittings.
9. High quality fuel line, identified by the word Fluoroelastomer printed on the hose,
   should be used for fuel line replacement. Never use unreinforced vacuum line, clear
   plastic tubing or water hose for fuel lines.
10. Spring-type clamps on fuel lines can lose tension over time. Replace all spring-type
    clamps with screw clamps whenever a hose is replaced.

**Metal lines**

11. Sections of metal line are often used between the fuel pump and carburetor or fuel
    injectors. Check for bending, crimping and cracks.
12. If a section of metal fuel line must be replaced, only seamless steel tubing should
    be used — copper and aluminum tubing don't have the strength to withstand normal
    engine vibration.
13. Check the metal brake lines where they enter the master cylinder and brake
    proportioning unit (if used) for cracks or loose fittings. Any sign of brake fluid
    leakage calls for an immediate thorough inspection of the brake system.

### 14 Cooling System Check

(PDF p.40) <a id="p40"></a>

1. Many major engine failures can be attributed to a faulty cooling system. On automatic
   transaxle vehicles the cooling system also cools the transmission fluid.
2. Check the cooling system with the engine cold, before the vehicle is driven for the
   day or after the engine has been shut off for at least three hours.
3. Remove the radiator cap by turning it to the left until it reaches a stop; if you
   hear hissing (residual pressure), wait until it stops before continuing. Thoroughly
   clean the cap and the filler neck. The coolant should be relatively transparent — if
   rust colored, drain and refill (Section 29).
4. Carefully check the large upper and lower radiator hoses along with the smaller
   heater hoses. Replace any hose that is cracked, swollen or shows deterioration; cracks
   may become more apparent if the hose is squeezed. It's a good idea to replace hoses
   with new ones every two years.
5. Make sure all hose connections are tight. A cooling system leak usually shows up as
   white or rust colored deposits near the leak. Consider replacing wire-type hose clamps
   with screw-type clamps.
6. Use a garden hose or soft brush to remove bugs, leaves, etc. from the front of the
   radiator or air conditioning condenser without damaging the cooling fins.
7. Every other inspection, or at the first sign of cooling system problems, have the cap
   and system pressure tested.

### 16 Brake Check

(PDF p.41) <a id="p41"></a>

> **Warning:** Brake dust produced by lining wear and deposited on brake components may
> contain asbestos, which is hazardous to your health. DO NOT blow it out with compressed
> air and DO NOT inhale it. DO NOT use gasoline or solvents to remove the dust — use brake
> system cleaner and flush the dust into a drain pan. Dispose of contaminated rags and
> solvent in a covered, labeled container.

1. In addition to the specified intervals, the brakes should be inspected every time the
   wheels are removed or whenever a defect is suspected — the vehicle pulling to one
   side, squealing or dragging noises, excessive pedal travel, pedal pulsation, or fluid
   leaks are all potential brake system defects.
2. Loosen the wheel lug nuts.
3. Raise the vehicle and place it securely on jackstands.
4. Remove the wheels.

**Disc brakes (front or rear)**

5. There are two pads — an outer and an inner — in each caliper, visible through small
   inspection holes in the caliper.
6. Check the pad thickness through the inspection hole. If the lining material is less
   than the specified thickness, replace the pads. (The lining material is riveted or
   bonded to a metal backing plate; the metal portion is not included in the
   measurement.)
7. If it's difficult to determine the exact thickness by the above method, remove the
   caliper(s) and pads for further inspection (see Chapter 9).
8. Once removed, clean the pads with brake cleaner and remeasure with a steel pocket
   ruler or vernier caliper.
9. Check the disc for score marks, deep scratches and burned spots. If these conditions
   exist, the hub/disc assembly will have to be removed (see Chapter 9).
10. Before installing the wheels, check all brake lines and hoses for damage, wear,
    deformation, cracks, corrosion, leakage, bends and twists, particularly near the
    caliper hoses. Check the clamps for tightness and the connections for leakage.

**Drum brakes**

11. Refer to Chapter 9 and remove the rear brake drums.
12. Note the thickness of the lining material on the rear brake shoes and look for signs
    of contamination by brake fluid and grease. If the lining material is within 1/16-inch
    of the recessed rivets or metal shoes, or if the shoes are cracked, glazed or
    contaminated, replace the brake shoes (see Chapter 9).
13. Check the shoe return and hold-down springs and the adjusting mechanism for correct
    installation and condition.
14. Check the wheel cylinders for leakage by carefully peeling back the rubber boots — if
    brake fluid is noted behind the boots, the wheel cylinders must be replaced (see
    Chapter 9).
15. Check the drums for cracks, score marks, deep scratches and hard spots. If
    imperfections cannot be removed with emery cloth, the drums must be resurfaced by an
    automotive machine shop.
16. Refer to Chapter 9 and install the brake drums.
17. Install the wheels and tighten the wheel lug nuts finger tight.
18. Remove the jackstands and lower the vehicle.
19. Tighten the wheel lug nuts to the torque listed in the [Specifications](#specifications).

**Parking brake**

20. A simple method of checking the parking brake is to park the vehicle on a steep hill
    with the parking brake set and the transaxle in Neutral. If the parking brake can't
    prevent the vehicle from rolling, it needs adjustment (see Chapter 9).

> **Warning:** The electric cooling fan can activate at any time. Disconnect the fan

2. On carbureted engines, the filter is located on top of the carburetor and is replaced

### 18 Fuel Filter Replacement

(PDF p.42-43) <a id="p42"></a>

> **Warning:** Gasoline is extremely flammable, so take extra precautions when you work
> on any part of the fuel system. Don't smoke or allow open flames or bare light bulbs
> near the work area. Wear safety glasses and have a Class B fire extinguisher on hand.

**Carbureted models**

1. The fuel filter is located on the left inner fender, below the brake master cylinder.
2. For easier access, pull the filter out of the spring clip so both fittings can be
   reached.
3. Release the hose clamps at the filter fittings and slide them back up the hoses.
4. Disconnect the hoses and remove the filter. This is a good time to replace the hoses
   if they're deteriorated.
5. Push the hoses onto the new filter and position the clamps approximately 1/4-inch
   back from the ends.
6. Push the filter back into the spring clip. Check that it's held securely and the
   hoses aren't kinked.
7. Start the engine and check for fuel leaks at the filter.

**Fuel-injected models**

> **Warning:** Before removing the fuel filter, the fuel system pressure must be relieved
> — refer to Chapter 4 for the procedure. Wear eye protection.

8. Loosen the banjo fitting bolts on both ends of the fuel filter (use a back-up wrench
   on the fitting at the filter). Disconnect both ends.
9. Remove the bracket bolts from the inner fender and remove the filter assembly from the
   support bracket.
10. Install the new filter assembly and tighten the bracket bolts securely. Make sure the
    filter is installed with the outlet facing up, and that the flanged end is facing
    down (don't install the filter backwards).
11. Using new sealing washers — two per banjo fitting — install the banjo fittings and
    tighten the bolts to the torque listed in the [Specifications](#specifications)
    (fuel filter banjo bolts).
12. The remainder of installation is the reverse of removal.
13. Start the engine and check for fuel leaks at the filter.

### 19 Fuel System Check

(PDF p.43) <a id="p43"></a>

> **Warning:** Gasoline is extremely flammable, so take extra precautions when you work
> on any part of the fuel system. Don't smoke or allow open flames or bare light bulbs
> near the work area. Wear safety glasses and have a Class B fire extinguisher on hand.

1. If you smell gasoline while driving or after the vehicle has been sitting in the sun,
   inspect the fuel system immediately.
2. Remove the gas filler cap and inspect for damage and corrosion — the gasket should
   have an unbroken sealing imprint.
3. Inspect the fuel feed and return lines for cracks. Check the metal fuel line
   connections to make sure they are tight.
4. Since some components — the fuel tank and part of the fuel feed and return lines —
   are underneath the vehicle, they can be inspected more easily with the vehicle raised
   on a hoist. If not possible, raise the vehicle and secure it on jackstands.
5. With the vehicle raised and safely supported, inspect the gas tank and filler neck for
   punctures, cracks and other damage. The connection between the filler neck and the
   tank is particularly critical.
   > **Warning:** Do not, under any circumstances, try to repair a fuel tank (except for
   > replacing rubber components). A welding torch or any open flame can easily cause
   > fuel vapors inside the tank to explode.
6. Carefully check all rubber hoses and metal lines leading away from the fuel tank for
   loose connections, deteriorated hoses, crimped lines and other damage. Repair or
   replace damaged sections as necessary.

### 20 Steering and Suspension Check

(PDF p.44) <a id="p44"></a>

**With the wheels on the ground**

1. With the vehicle stopped and the front wheels pointed straight ahead, rock the
   steering wheel gently back-and-forth. Excessive freeplay indicates a worn front wheel
   bearing, main shaft yoke, intermediate shaft yoke, lower arm balljoint or steering
   system joint, or a steering gear out of adjustment or broken. Refer to Chapter 10 for
   the repair procedure.
2. Other symptoms — excessive body movement over rough roads, swaying around corners,
   binding as the steering wheel is turned — may indicate faulty steering and/or
   suspension components.
3. Check the shock absorbers by quickly pushing down and releasing the vehicle several
   times at each corner. If the vehicle does not come back to a level position within one
   or two bounces, the shocks/struts are worn and must be replaced. Listen for squeaks and
   noises from the suspension components while bouncing.

**With the vehicle raised**

4. Raise the vehicle and support it securely on jackstands. See Jacking and towing at the
   front of this book for the proper jacking points.
5. Check the tires for irregular wear patterns and proper inflation.
6. Inspect the universal joint between the steering shaft and the steering gear housing.
   Check the steering gear housing for grease leakage or oozing, and make sure the dust
   seals and boots are not damaged and the boot clamps are not loose.
7. Check the steering linkage for looseness and damage. Look for loose bolts, broken or
   disconnected parts and deteriorated rubber bushings on all suspension and steering
   components. While an assistant turns the steering wheel from side to side, check the
   steering components for free movement, chafing and binding.
8. Inspect the balljoint boots for damage and leaking grease. Replace the boots with new
   ones if damaged (see Chapter 10).
9. Check the suspension crossmember mounting fasteners for looseness and tighten as
   needed.

### 22 Exhaust System Check

(PDF p.44) <a id="p44"></a>

1. With the engine cold (at least three hours after the vehicle has been driven), check
   the complete exhaust system from its starting point at the engine to the end of the
   tailpipe. This should be done on a hoist where unrestricted access is available.
2. Check the pipes and connections for evidence of leaks, severe corrosion or damage.
   Make sure all brackets and hangers are in good condition and tight.
3. At the same time, inspect the underside of the body for holes, corrosion, open seams,
   etc. which may allow exhaust gases to enter the passenger compartment. Seal all body
   openings with silicone or body putty.
4. Rattles and other noises can often be traced to the exhaust system, especially the
   mounts and hangers. If components can come in contact with the body or suspension
   parts, secure the exhaust system with new mounts.
5. Check the running condition of the engine by inspecting inside the end of the
   tailpipe. If the pipe is wet with oil or coated with white deposits, the engine is in
   need of service, including a compression test, a thorough fuel system inspection and
   adjustment.

(PDF p.44) <a id="p44"></a>

1. The manual transaxle does not have a dipstick. To check the lubrication level, raise

### 24 Valve Clearance Check and Adjustment

(PDF p.44-46) <a id="p44"></a>

> **Warning:** The electric cooling fan can activate at any time. Disconnect the fan
> motor or negative battery cable when working in the vicinity of the fan.

See [Valve clearances](#specifications) in the Specifications for the cold/hot clearance
values by engine.

#### SOHC engines (4A-C)

1. The valve clearances are checked and adjusted with the engine at normal operating
   temperature.
2. Remove the air cleaner assembly (see Chapter 4).
3. Remove the valve cover (see Chapter 2, Part A).
4. Place the number one piston at Top Dead Center (TDC) on the compression stroke (see
   Chapter 2, Part A). The number one cylinder rocker arms (closest to the timing belt end
   of the engine) should be loose (able to move up-and-down slightly) and the camshaft
   lobes should be facing away from the rocker arms.
5. With the crankshaft in this position the valves labeled "First" can be checked and
   adjusted.
6. First, check and adjust the clearance of both intake valves. Loosen the locknut, turn
   the adjusting screw counterclockwise and insert the appropriate size feeler gauge
   between the intake valve stem and the adjusting screw. Carefully tighten the adjusting
   screw until you can feel a slight drag on the feeler gauge as you withdraw it.
7. Hold the adjusting screw with a screwdriver (to keep it from turning) and tighten the
   locknut. Recheck the clearance to make sure it hasn't changed.
8. Now adjust both exhaust valves the same way.
9. Hold the adjusting screw with a screwdriver and tighten the locknut. Recheck the
   clearance.
10. Rotate the crankshaft one full revolution (360-degrees) until the number four piston
    is at TDC on the compression stroke. The number four cylinder rocker arms (closest to
    the transaxle end of the engine) should be loose, with the camshaft lobes facing away
    from the rocker arms.
11. Adjust the valves labeled "Second" as described in Steps 6 through 9.
12. Install the valve cover and air cleaner assembly.

<!-- DIAGRAM CANDIDATE: p.44 — "Adjust these valves first / next on SOHC engines" diagrams (24.5 and 24.11) showing which valve positions correspond to the "First" and "Second" adjustment groups. -->

#### DOHC engines (4A-F, 4A-FE, 4A-GE)

> **Note:** The following procedure requires a special valve lifter tool set available at
> most auto parts stores. The engine MUST be cold for this procedure.

13. Disconnect the cable from the negative terminal of the battery.
14. Blow out the recessed area between the camshafts with compressed air, if available,
    to remove any debris that might fall into the cylinders, then remove the spark plugs
    (see Section 25).
    > **Warning:** Wear eye protection when using compressed air.
15. Remove the valve covers (see Chapter 2, Part A).
16. Set the no. 1 cylinder at Top Dead Center (TDC) on the compression stroke by turning
    the crankshaft pulley clockwise until the groove in the pulley is aligned with the "0"
    on the stationary pointer (see Chapter 2, Part A). To verify the no. 1 piston is on
    the compression stroke, make sure the valve lifters for the no. 1 cylinder are loose
    (the cam lobes won't be pushing on the lifters) and the valve lifters for the no. 4
    cylinder are tight. If they aren't, turn the crankshaft one more complete revolution.
17. Measure the clearances of the indicated valves with a feeler gauge of the specified
    thickness. When the no. 1 piston is at TDC on the compression stroke, the valve
    clearance for the no. 1 and no. 3 exhaust valves and the no. 1 and no. 2 intake valves
    can be checked/adjusted. Record the measurements which are out of specification — they
    will be used later to determine the required replacement shims.
18. Turn the crankshaft pulley one more complete revolution (360-degrees) and align the
    timing marks as described in Step 16 above. The no. 4 piston is now at TDC. Measure
    the clearances for the remaining valves (the no. 2 and no. 4 exhaust valves and the
    no. 3 and no. 4 intake valves).
19. After all the valve clearances have been measured, turn the crankshaft pulley
    clockwise until the camshaft lobe above the first valve you intend to adjust is
    pointing up, away from the shim.
20. Position the notch in the valve lifter toward the spark plug. Then compress the valve
    spring with the special tools. Remove the adjusting shim with a small screwdriver and
    magnet.
21. Measure the thickness of the shim with a micrometer. To calculate the correct
    thickness of a replacement shim that will place the valve clearance within the
    specified limits, use the following formula:

    ```
    Intake side:  N = T + (A - 0.008 in)
    Exhaust side: N = T + (A - 0.010 in)

    T = thickness of the old shim
    A = valve clearance measured
    N = the thickness of the new shim
    ```

22. Select a shim with a thickness as close as possible to the valve clearance
    calculated. Shims are available in 17 sizes in increments of 0.0020-inch (0.050 mm),
    ranging from 0.0984-inch (2.500 mm) to 0.1299-inch (3.300 mm).
    > **Note:** Through careful analysis of the shim sizes needed to bring all the out of
    > specification valve clearances within specification, it's often possible to simply
    > move a shim to another valve lifter requiring a shim of that particular size,
    > reducing the number of new shims that must be purchased.

    **Available shim numbers and thicknesses — mm (in.)**

    | Shim No. | Thickness | Shim No. | Thickness |

    | 02 | 2.500 (0.0984) | 20 | 2.950 (0.1161) |

23. With the special valve lifter tools still in position, install the new adjusting
    shim. Measure the clearance with a feeler gauge to make sure your calculations are

25. Installation of the remaining components is the reverse of removal.

### 25 Spark Plug Replacement

(PDF p.46-48) <a id="p46"></a>

1. Spark plug replacement requires a spark plug socket which fits onto a ratchet wrench.
   This socket is lined with a rubber grommet to protect the porcelain insulator of the
   spark plug and to hold the plug while you insert it into the spark plug hole. You will
   also need a wire-type feeler gauge to check and adjust the spark plug gap and a torque
   wrench to tighten the new plugs to the specified torque.
2. When replacing the plugs, purchase the new plugs in advance, adjust them to the proper
   gap and then replace each plug one at a time.
   > **Note:** When buying new spark plugs, it's essential that you obtain the correct
   > plugs for your specific vehicle.
3. Inspect each of the new plugs for defects. If there are any signs of cracks in the
   porcelain insulator of a plug, don't use it.
4. Check the electrode gaps of the new plugs by inserting a wire gauge of the proper
   thickness between the electrodes at the tip of the plug. The gap between the
   electrodes should be identical to that specified on the VECI label. If the gap is
   incorrect, use the notched adjuster on the feeler gauge body to bend the curved side
   electrode slightly.
5. If the side electrode is not exactly over the center electrode, use the notched
   adjuster to align them.
   > **Caution:** If the gap of a new plug must be adjusted, bend only the base of the
   > ground electrode, do not touch the center tip.

**Removal**

6. To prevent the possibility of mixing up spark plug wires, work on one spark plug at a
   time. Remove the wire and boot from one spark plug. Grasp the boot — not the cable —
   give it a half twisting motion and pull it off.
7. If compressed air is available, blow any dirt or foreign material away from the spark
   plug area before proceeding (a bicycle tire pump will also work).
8. Remove the spark plug.
9. Compare each old spark plug with those shown in the photos on the inside back cover of
   this manual to determine the overall running condition of the engine.

**Installation**

10. It's often difficult to insert spark plugs into their holes without cross-threading
    them. To avoid this, fit a short piece of 3/16-inch ID rubber hose over the end of the
    spark plug — the flexible hose acts as a universal joint to help align the plug with
    the plug hole and will slip if the plug begins to cross-thread, preventing thread
    damage. Tighten the plug securely.
11. Attach the plug wire to the new spark plug, again using a twisting motion on the boot
    until it is firmly seated on the end of the spark plug.
12. Follow the above procedure for the remaining spark plugs, replacing them one at a
    time to prevent mixing up the spark plug wires.

### 26 Spark Plug Wire, Distributor Cap and Rotor Check and Replacement

(PDF p.47-49) <a id="p47"></a>

1. The spark plug wires should be checked whenever new spark plugs are installed.
2. Begin with a visual check of the spark plug wires while the engine is running. In a
   darkened garage (make sure there is ventilation) start the engine and observe each
   plug wire, being careful not to contact any moving engine parts. A break in the wire
   will show as arcing or a small spark at the damaged area — if noticed, allow the
   engine to cool before continuing.
3. Inspect the spark plug wires one at a time to prevent mixing up the order, which is
   essential for proper engine operation. If a wire's number is illegible, mark a piece
   of tape and wrap it around the wire.
4. Disconnect the plug wire from the spark plug. A removal tool can be used, or grasp the
   rubber boot, twist it half a turn and pull it free. Do not pull on the wire itself.
5. Check inside the boot for corrosion (a white crusty powder).
6. Push the wire and boot back onto the end of the spark plug — it should fit tightly. If
   it doesn't, remove the wire and use pliers to carefully crimp the metal connector
   inside the wire boot until the fit is snug.
7. Using a clean rag, wipe the entire length of the wire to remove built-up dirt and
   grease, then check for burns, cracks and other damage. Do not bend the wire sharply, as
   the conductor within might break.
8. Disconnect the wire from the distributor, again pulling only on the rubber boot. Check
   for corrosion and a tight fit.
9. Inspect the remaining spark plug wires, making sure each one is securely fastened at
   the distributor and spark plug when the check is complete.
10. If new spark plug wires are required, purchase a set for your specific engine model.
    Remove and replace the wires one at a time to avoid mix-ups in the firing order.
11. Detach the distributor cap by removing the screws. Look inside it for cracks, carbon
    tracks and worn, burned or loose contacts.
12. Pull the rotor off the distributor shaft and examine it for cracks and carbon tracks.
    Replace the cap and rotor if any damage or defects are noted.
13. It is common practice to install a new cap and rotor whenever new spark plug wires
    are installed, but if you wish to continue using the old cap, clean the terminals
    first.
14. When installing a new cap, remove the wires from the old cap one at a time and attach
    them to the new cap in the exact same location — do not simultaneously remove all the
    wires from the old cap, or firing order mix-ups may occur.

### 27 Carburetor Choke Check

(PDF p.48-49) <a id="p48"></a>

> **Warning:** The electric cooling fan can activate at any time. Disconnect the fan
> motor or negative battery cable when working in the vicinity of the fan.

1. The choke operates only when the engine is cold, so this check should be performed
   before the engine has been started for the day.
2. Take off the top plate of the air cleaner assembly (held by a wing nut and side
   clips). Tag any vacuum hoses that must be disconnected.
3. Look at the center of the air cleaner housing — you will notice a flat plate at the
   carburetor opening.
4. Press the accelerator pedal to the floor — the plate should close completely. Start
   the engine while watching the plate.
   > **Warning:** Don't position your face near the carburetor, as the engine could
   > backfire, causing serious burns.
   When the engine starts, the choke plate should open slightly.
5. Allow the engine to continue running at idle speed. As the engine warms up to
   operating temperature, the plate should slowly open, allowing more air to enter
   through the top of the carburetor.
6. After a few minutes, the choke plate should be fully open to the vertical position.
   Blip the throttle to make sure the fast idle cam disengages.
7. As the plate opens and the throttle is moved to disengage the fast idle cam, the
   engine speed will decrease.
8. Refer to Chapter 4 for specific information on adjusting and servicing the choke
   components.

### 28 Thermostatically Controlled Air Cleaner Check (carbureted models only)

(PDF p.49) <a id="p49"></a>

> **Warning:** The electric cooling fan can activate at any time. Disconnect the fan
> motor or negative battery cable when working in the vicinity of the fan.

1. Carbureted engines have a thermostatically controlled air cleaner which draws air
   from different locations depending on engine temperature.
2. This is a visual check, requiring use of a small mirror.
3. When the engine is cold, locate the air control valve inside the air cleaner
   assembly — it's inside the snorkel of the air cleaner housing.
4. There is a flexible air duct attached to the end of the snorkel; disconnect it at the
   snorkel so you can look through the end and see the air control valve inside.
5. Start the engine and look through the snorkel at the valve, which should move up to
   block off the air cleaner snorkel. With the valve closed, air cannot enter through the
   end of the snorkel, but instead enters through the flexible duct attached to the
   exhaust manifold and the heat stove passage.
6. As the engine warms up to operating temperature, the valve should move down to allow
   air to be drawn through the snorkel end — depending on outside temperature, this may
   take 10 to 15 minutes. To speed up the check, reconnect the snorkel air duct, drive the
   vehicle, then check whether the valve is completely open.
7. If the thermostatically controlled air cleaner isn't operating properly, see Chapter 6
   for more information.

### 29 Cooling System Servicing (Draining, Flushing and Refilling)

(PDF p.49-50) <a id="p49"></a>

> **Warning:** Do not allow antifreeze to come in contact with your skin or painted
> surfaces of the vehicle. Rinse off spills immediately with plenty of water. Antifreeze
> is highly toxic if ingested. Never leave antifreeze lying around in an open container or
> in puddles on the floor. Also, the electric cooling fan can activate at any time, even
> when the ignition is in the Off position — disconnect the fan motor or negative battery
> cable when working in the vicinity of the fan.

1. Periodically, the cooling system should be drained, flushed and refilled to replenish
   the antifreeze mixture and prevent formation of rust and corrosion. When the cooling
   system is serviced, all hoses and the radiator cap should be checked and replaced if
   necessary.

**Draining**

2. Apply the parking brake and block the wheels. If the vehicle has just been driven,
   wait several hours to allow the engine to cool down before beginning.
3. Once the engine is completely cool, remove the radiator cap.
4. Remove the splash panel located beneath the radiator (if equipped). Move a large
   container under the radiator drain to catch the coolant. Attach a 3/8-inch inner
   diameter hose to the drain fitting to direct the coolant into the container, then open
   the drain fitting.
5. After the coolant stops flowing out of the radiator, move the container under the
   engine block drain plug. Loosen the plug and allow the coolant in the block to drain.
6. While the coolant is draining, check the condition of the radiator hoses, heater hoses
   and clamps (see Section 14).
7. Replace any damaged clamps or hoses (see Chapter 3 for detailed replacement
   procedures).

**Flushing**

8. Once the system is completely drained, flush the radiator with fresh water from a
   garden hose until water runs clear at the drain. This removes sediments but will not
   remove rust and scale from the engine and cooling tube surfaces.
9. Deposits can be removed by the chemical action of a cleaner — follow the procedure
   outlined in the manufacturer's instructions. If the radiator is severely corroded,
   damaged or leaking, it should be removed (see Chapter 3) and taken to a radiator
   repair shop.
10. Remove the overflow hose from the coolant recovery reservoir. Drain the reservoir and
    flush it with clean water, then reconnect the hose.

**Refilling**

11. Close and tighten the radiator drain. Install and tighten the block drain plug.
12. Place the heater temperature control in the maximum heat position.
13. Slowly add new coolant (a 50/50 mixture of water and antifreeze) to the radiator until
    it's full. Add coolant to the reservoir up to the lower mark.
14. Leave the radiator cap off and run the engine in a well-ventilated area until the
    thermostat opens (coolant will begin flowing through the radiator and the upper
    radiator hose will become hot).
15. Turn the engine off and let it cool. Add more coolant mixture to bring the level back
    up to the lip on the radiator filler neck.
16. Squeeze the upper radiator hose to expel air, then add more coolant mixture if
    necessary. Replace the radiator cap.
17. Start the engine, allow it to reach normal operating temperature and check for leaks.

### 30 Idle Speed Check and Adjustment

(PDF p.50-51) <a id="p50"></a>

1. Engine idle speed is the speed at which the engine operates when no accelerator pedal
   pressure is applied, as when stopped at a traffic light. It is adjusted under the
   following conditions:
   - Air cleaner installed
   - All accessories (heater, cooling fan, lights, etc.) off
   - All vacuum lines in good condition and properly connected
   - Engine at normal operating temperature (choke all the way open on carbureted
     vehicles)
   - Transaxle in Neutral or Park
2. Set the parking brake firmly and block the wheels to prevent the vehicle from rolling.
   Put the transaxle in Neutral.

**Carbureted models**

3. Connect a hand held tachometer to the check connector.
   > **Caution:** Do not ground the check connector, or damage to the igniter may occur.
   > If your tachometer is not of the type shown in the illustration, consult the
   > tachometer manufacturer to find out whether your tachometer is compatible with the
   > vehicle's ignition system.
4. Start the engine and allow it to reach normal operating temperature.
5. Check, and adjust if necessary, the ignition timing (see Chapter 4).

**Fuel-injected models**

6. Uncap the check connector in the engine compartment and connect a tachometer to the IG
   terminal.
   > **Caution:** Do not ground the service connector, or damage to the igniter may occur.
   > If your tachometer is not of the type shown in the illustration, consult the
   > tachometer manufacturer to find out whether it is compatible with the vehicle's
   > ignition system.
7. On 4A-FE engines only, connect terminals E1 and TE1 in the check connector to each
   other with a jumper wire.
8. Run the engine at 2,500 rpm for 90 seconds (4A-FE) or 2 minutes (4A-GE).

**All models**

9. Make sure the cooling fan is off. If it is running, wait for it to stop.
10. Check the engine idle speed on the tachometer and compare it to the Vehicle Emission
    Control Information label in the engine compartment.
11. If the idle speed is too low or too high, turn the idle speed adjusting screw until
    the specified idle speed is obtained.

### 31 Automatic Transaxle Fluid Change

(PDF p.51-52) <a id="p51"></a>

1. At the specified time intervals, the automatic transaxle fluid should be drained and
   replaced.
2. Before beginning work, purchase the specified transmission fluid (see
   [Specifications](#specifications)).
3. Other tools necessary include jackstands, a drain pan capable of holding at least two
   gallons, newspapers and clean rags.
4. The fluid should be drained immediately after the vehicle has been driven — hot fluid
   is more effective than cold fluid at removing built up sediment.
   > **Warning:** Fluid temperature can exceed 350-degrees in a hot transaxle. Wear
   > protective gloves.
5. After the vehicle has been driven to warm up the fluid, raise it and place it on
   jackstands for access to the transaxle drain plug. Remove any splash pans that are in
   the way.
6. Move the necessary equipment under the vehicle, being careful not to touch any hot
   exhaust components.
7. Place the drain pan under the drain plugs in the transaxle housing and pan. Remove the
   drain plugs one at a time.
   > **Note:** Be sure the drain pan is in position, as fluid will come out with some
   > force. Once the fluid is drained, reinstall the drain plugs securely.
8. Remove the transaxle pan bolts, carefully pry the pan loose with a screwdriver and
   remove it.
9. If necessary, remove the strainer retaining bolts and detach the strainer from the
   transaxle. Be careful when lowering the strainer, as it contains residual fluid.
   > **Note:** The manufacturer does not recommend replacing the oil strainer during
   > routine maintenance. If necessary, remove the strainer and clean it using an
   > approved cleaning solvent.
10. Place the strainer in position and install the bolts. Tighten the bolts to the torque
    listed in the [Specifications](#specifications) (automatic transaxle strainer bolts).
11. Carefully clean the gasket surfaces of the fluid pan, removing all traces of old
    gasket material. Wash the pan in clean solvent and dry it with compressed air. Clean
    and reinstall the magnets.
12. Install a new gasket, place the fluid pan in position and install the bolts. Tighten
    the bolts to the torque listed in the Specifications (automatic transaxle pan bolts).
13. Add the specified amount and type of lubricant to the differential. Reinstall any
    splash pans and lower the vehicle.
14. With the engine off, add new fluid to the transaxle through the dipstick tube (see
    [Specifications](#specifications) for the recommended fluid type and capacity). Use a
    funnel to prevent spills, adding a little fluid at a time while checking the level
    with the dipstick.
15. Start the engine and shift the selector into all positions from Park through Low,
    then shift into Park and apply the parking brake.
16. With the engine idling, check the fluid level. Add fluid up to the HOT level on the
    dipstick.

### 34 Evaporative Emissions Control System Check

(PDF p.53) <a id="p53"></a>

1. The function of the evaporative emissions control system is to draw fuel vapors from
   the gas tank and fuel system, store them in a charcoal canister and route them to the
   intake manifold during normal engine operation.
2. The most common symptom of a fault in the evaporative emissions system is a strong
   fuel odor in the engine compartment. If a fuel odor is detected, inspect the charcoal
   canister, located in the engine compartment below the brake master cylinder. Check the
   canister and all hoses for damage and deterioration.
3. The evaporative emissions control system is explained in more detail in Chapter 6.

### 35 Positive Crankcase Ventilation (PCV) Valve Check and Replacement

(PDF p.53) <a id="p53"></a>

1. The PCV valve is located in the valve cover.
2. Disconnect the hose, pull the PCV valve from the cover, then reconnect the hose.

### 36 Fuel Tank Cap Gasket Replacement

(PDF p.53) <a id="p53"></a>

1. Obtain a new gasket.
2. Remove the tank cap and carefully pry the old gasket out of the recess. Avoid damaging
   the sealing surface inside the cap.
3. Work the new gasket into the cap recess.
4. Install the cap, then remove it and look for an impression all the way around the
   gasket — this indicates the gasket is sealing completely.

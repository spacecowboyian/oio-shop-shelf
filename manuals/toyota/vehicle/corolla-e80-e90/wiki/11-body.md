## 1. General information (PDF p.232) <a id="p232"></a>

These models use a "unibody" layout — a floor pan with front and rear frame side rails
supporting the body components, front/rear suspension, and other mechanical components.
Components particularly vulnerable to accident damage — body moldings, bumpers, hood and
trunk lids, and all glass — can be unbolted and repaired or replaced. This Chapter covers
only general body maintenance and body panel repair procedures within the do-it-yourselfer's
scope.

**[PDF p.232]**
## 2. Body - maintenance (PDF p.232-233) <a id="p232"></a>

Body condition matters a lot for resale value — a neglected or damaged body is much harder
to repair than mechanical components. Hidden areas (wheel wells, frame, engine compartment)
matter too, though they need less frequent attention. Once a year, or every 12,000 miles,
have the underside steam cleaned — this removes dirt/oil and lets you inspect for rust,
damaged brake lines, frayed electrical wires, damaged cables, and other problems. Clear body
drain holes of obstructions (illustrations 2.2a, 2.2b — check periodically for clogging;
clogged drain holes, e.g. the sill drain hole, can be cleared with a small screwdriver). Grease
the front suspension components after this job.

At the same time, clean the engine and engine compartment with a steam cleaner or water-
soluble degreaser. Give the wheel wells close attention — undercoating can peel, and stones/
dirt thrown up by tires can chip/flake paint, letting rust set in; if rust is found, clean to
bare metal and apply anti-rust paint. Wash the body about once a week — wet thoroughly to
soften dirt, then wash with a soft sponge and plenty of clean soapy water (surplus dirt not
carefully washed off can wear down the paint). Remove tar/asphalt spots with a
solvent-soaked cloth. Wax the body and chrome trim every six months — if using a chrome
cleaner to remove rust, note it also removes some chrome, so use it sparingly.

## 3. Vinyl trim - maintenance (PDF p.233) <a id="p233"></a>

Don't clean vinyl trim with detergents, caustic soap, or petroleum-based cleaners — plain
soap and water works fine, with a soft brush for ingrained dirt. Wash as frequently as the
rest of the vehicle. After cleaning, a high-quality rubber and vinyl protectant helps
prevent oxidation and cracking — can also be applied to weatherstripping, vacuum lines, and
rubber hoses, which often fail from chemical degradation, and to [tires, per typical Haynes
phrasing — see NEEDS REVIEW].

## 4. Upholstery and carpets - maintenance (PDF p.234) <a id="p234"></a>

Every three months, remove the carpets/mats and clean the vehicle interior (more frequently
if needed) — vacuum the upholstery and carpets to remove loose dirt/dust. Leather upholstery
requires special care: remove stains with warm water and a very mild soap solution, use a
clean damp cloth to remove the soap, then wipe again with a dry cloth. **Never use alcohol,
gasoline, nail polish remover, or thinner on leather upholstery.** After cleaning,
regularly treat leather with a leather wax — never use car wax on leather. In areas with
bright sunlight, cover leather seats with a sheet if the vehicle will sit out for any length
of time.

## 5. Body repair - minor damage (PDF p.234-236) <a id="p234"></a>

*Note (as printed): the accompanying photos illustrate a method of repairing simple dents.
They supplement the Body repair - minor damage text in this Chapter and should not be used
as the sole instructions for body repair.*

**Repair of minor scratches:** if a scratch is superficial (doesn't reach the metal), rub it
lightly with fine rubbing compound to remove loose paint/built-up wax, rinse with clean
water. Apply touch-up paint with a small brush in thin layers until level with the
surrounding paint; let it harden at least two weeks, then blend into the surrounding paint
with very fine rubbing compound; apply a coat of wax.

If the scratch penetrates to the metal and has caused rust: remove all loose rust from the
bottom of the scratch with a pocket knife, apply rust-inhibiting paint to prevent future
rust. Using a rubber or nylon applicator, coat the scratched area with glaze-type filler
(can be mixed with thinner for a thin paste, ideal for narrow scratches). Before the glaze
hardens, wrap smooth cotton cloth around a fingertip, dip in thinner, and quickly wipe along
the scratch surface — this leaves the filler surface slightly hollow. Paint over as described
above.

**Repair of dents:** first pull the dent out until close to its original shape — there's no
point trying to restore the shape completely, since impact-stretched metal can't be
restored to its original contours; bring the dent level to about 1/8-inch below the
surrounding metal (very shallow dents aren't worth pulling out at all). If the backside is
accessible, gently hammer it out from behind with a soft-face hammer, holding a wood block
firmly against the opposite side to absorb blows and prevent stretching. If the dent is in a
double-layer section or otherwise inaccessible from behind: drill several small holes through
the metal inside the damaged area (particularly in deeper sections), screw long self-tapping
screws into the holes just enough to grip, then pull the dent out by pulling on the
protruding screw heads with locking pliers.

The next stage removes paint from the damaged area and an inch or so of surrounding metal —
wire brush or sanding disk in a drill motor, or by hand with sandpaper. To complete
preparation for filling, score the bare metal surface with a screwdriver or file tang, or
drill small holes in the affected area, for a good filler grip. See "Filling and painting"
below.

**Repair of rust holes or gashes:** remove all paint from the affected area and an inch or so
around it (sanding disk/wire brush in a drill, or sandpaper by hand). With paint removed,
assess corrosion severity — decide whether to replace the whole panel (new panels aren't as
expensive as most people think, and often quicker than repairing large rust areas) or repair
the affected area. Remove trim pieces from the area except those acting as a shape guide
(e.g. headlight shells). Using metal snips or a hacksaw blade, remove all loose metal and any
badly rust-affected metal; hammer the hole edges inward to create a slight depression for the
filler. Wire brush the affected area to remove powdery rust — treat the back of the area with
rust-inhibiting paint if accessible. Before filling, block the hole with sheet metal riveted
or screwed into place, or by stuffing it with wire mesh. Once blocked, fill and paint per the
subsection below.

**Filling and painting:** body repair kits with filler paste and resin hardener tube are best
for this. A wide, flexible plastic or nylon applicator gives a smooth, contoured filler
finish. Mix a small amount of filler on clean wood or cardboard (use hardener sparingly),
following the package instructions exactly or the filler will set incorrectly. Apply the
filler paste with the applicator, drawing it across the surface to achieve the desired
contour and level the surface — stop working the paste once the contour approximates the
original (continuing causes the paste to stick to the applicator); add thin layers at
20-minute intervals until the filler level is just above the surrounding metal.

Once hardened, remove the excess with a body file, then progressively finer sandpaper from
180-grit to 600-grit wet-or-dry, always wrapped around a flat rubber or wooden block (otherwise
the filler surface won't be flat). Periodically rinse the wet-or-dry paper in water during
sanding for a smooth finish. At this point the repair area should be surrounded by a ring of
bare metal, itself encircled by finely feathered good paint — rinse with clean water until all
sanding dust is gone.

Spray the entire area with a light primer coat — this reveals filler imperfections, repaired
with fresh filler paste or glaze filler and sanded smooth again; repeat spray-and-repair until
satisfied the filler surface and feathered paint edge are perfect. Rinse and let dry
completely.

Spray painting must be done in a warm, dry, windless, dust-free atmosphere — pick the day
carefully if working outdoors; if indoors, dousing the floor with water helps settle dust.
Masking off adjacent panels/trim helps minimize the effect of a slight color mismatch; mask
with masking tape and several thicknesses of newspaper.

Before spraying, shake the paint can thoroughly, spray a test area until the technique is
mastered. Cover the repair area with a thick coat of primer, built up from several thin
layers rather than one thick one; between coats, rinse the work area and wet-or-dry
sandpaper with water and rub the primer surface smooth with 600-grit; let each coat dry
before spraying the next. Spray the top coat, again in several thin layers — begin at the
center and work outward in a circular motion until the whole repair area plus about two
inches of surrounding original paint is covered. Remove masking material 10-15 minutes after
the final coat. Let the new paint harden at least two weeks, then blend the edges into the
existing paint with very fine rubbing compound. Finally, apply a coat of wax.

## 6. Body repair - major damage (PDF p.235) <a id="p235"></a>

Major damage must be repaired by an auto body shop specifically equipped for unibody
repairs — they have the specialized equipment required. If damage is extensive, the body
must be checked for proper alignment, or the vehicle's handling characteristics may be
adversely affected and other components may wear at an accelerated rate. Since all major body
components (hood, fenders, etc.) are separate, replaceable units, seriously damaged
components should be replaced rather than repaired — sometimes available from a wrecking yard
specializing in used vehicle components, often at considerable savings over new parts.

## 7. Hinges and locks - maintenance (PDF p.235) <a id="p235"></a>

Every 3,000 miles or three months, give the door/hood/trunk hinges and latch assemblies a
few drops of light oil or lock lubricant. Lubricate the door latch strikers with a thin coat
of grease to reduce wear and ensure free movement. Lubricate the door and trunk locks with
spray-on graphite lubricant. Installation (after unbolting hinges from the body) is the
reverse of removal — apply rust inhibitor around the hinges whenever they're unbolted.

## 8. Windshield and fixed glass - replacement (PDF p.235) <a id="p235"></a>

Replacement requires special fast-setting adhesive/caulk materials and specialized tools. It
is recommended that these operations be left to a dealer or a shop specializing in glass
work.

## 9. Hood - removal, installation and adjustment (PDF p.235-236) <a id="p235"></a>

*Note (as printed): the hood is heavy and somewhat awkward to remove/install — at least two
people should perform this procedure.*

**Removal and installation:** use blankets or pads to cover the cowl area and fenders, to
protect the body/paint as the hood is lifted off. Make marks around the bolt heads for
correct reinstallation alignment (illustration 9.2). Disconnect any interfering cables or
wires. Have an assistant support the hood, remove the hinge-to-hood screws/bolts, lift off
the hood. Installation is the reverse of removal.

**Adjustment:** fore-and-aft and side-to-side adjustment is done by moving the hinge plate
slot after loosening the bolts/nuts. *Note: on some models, the hood is secured with
centering bolts (illustration 9.7) — these must be replaced with standard bolts, lockwashers,
and washers to allow adjustment.* Scribe a line around the entire hinge plate to judge
movement. Loosen the bolts/nuts, move the hood into alignment a little at a time, tighten the
hinge bolts, and carefully lower the hood to check position. To align the rear edge
vertically, unbolt the hinges from the body and add/remove shims between hinges and body as
necessary (illustration 9.10). To align the front edge vertically, adjust the hood bumpers on
the radiator support so the closed hood is flush with the fenders (illustration 9.11 — turn
the bumpers with a screwdriver).

If necessary after installation, the entire hood latch assembly can also be adjusted
up-and-down and side-to-side on the radiator support so the hood closes securely and flush.
Scribe a line around the hood latch mounting bolts for reference, loosen them, reposition the
latch assembly as necessary (illustration 9.12), retighten. *Note: on models with centering
bolts securing the hood latch, replace them with standard bolts/lockwashers/washers to make
adjustments.* The hood latch assembly and hinges should be periodically lubricated with
white, lithium-base grease to prevent binding and wear.

## 10. Door trim panel - removal and installation (PDF p.236-238) <a id="p236"></a>

**Removal:** disconnect the negative battery cable. Remove the trim panel for the outside
mirror (Section 15). Remove the door handle bezel and door pull/armrest assembly
(illustrations 10.2a — pull out the door handle for access to the bezel screw; 10.2b — the
armrest retaining screws are hidden under covers, pried off with a small screwdriver; 10.2c —
on later models, remove the door trim screw, pull the handle forward, disconnect the linkage
rod from the handle). On 1988 and later models, remove the screw securing the inside door
handle, slide it forward, disconnect its linkage rod, and take it out.

On models with manual window regulators, remove the window crank by working a cloth
back-and-forth behind the handle to dislodge the retainer (illustrations 10.3a, 10.3b — a
special tool exists for this but isn't essential); with the retainer removed, pull off the
handle. Some models have trim panel/armrest retaining screws behind trim caps beneath the
armrest or hidden behind plastic caps — pry the caps out and remove the screws; detach the
armrest from the door if it's removable.

Insert a wide putty knife, pry bar, or special clip removal tool between the trim panel and
door to disengage the retaining clips, working around the outer edge until free (illustration
10.5). Once all clips are disengaged, detach the trim panel, unplug any electrical connectors,
remove the panel. For access to the inner door, peel back the plastic water deflector,
careful not to tear it (illustration 10.7).

**Installation:** before installing, reinstall any clips that came out of the panel and
stayed in the door during removal. Plug in electrical connectors, position the panel, press
into place until the clips seat, install retaining screws and armrest/door pulls. With the
window closed, install the window crank or power switch assembly — illustration 10.9 shows
the correct crank position after installation (window closed). *Note: when installing a
manual window crank, it's easier to place the clip on the crank first, then push the crank
onto the shaft, than to install the clip after positioning the crank on the shaft.* Press the
water deflector into place first when reinstalling the trim panel.

**Front door components** — front hood/door exploded views for reference (illustration 10.4a
— 1984-1987 models; 10.4b — 1988 and later sedan/wagon models, coupe similar): labeled
components include rear view mirror cover, door lock cylinder, door outside handle, door
lock knob, channel, door belt moulding/outer weatherstrip, door inside handle (4-door/5-door
and 3-door variants), door hinge, door check, window regulator (with/without power window),
striker, door lock (with/without power door lock), door inside handle bezel, front/rear lower
frame, armrest, service hole cover, power window switch, window regulator handle, plate,
shoulder anchor cover, shoulder buckle pin, front door belt moulding, door glass run, door
outside handle with lock cylinder, glass channel, inside locking knob, remote opening control
link, screw cap/screw, pull handle, armrest base, snap ring, regulator handle, power window
relay.

## 11. Door latch, lock cylinder and handles - removal and installation (PDF p.238-239) <a id="p238"></a>

Remove the door trim panel and water deflector (Section 10).

**Door latch:** reach in through the door service hole and disconnect the control links from
the latch (illustrations 10.4a, 10.4b for reference). Remove the three door latch retaining
screws from the end of the door. Detach the door latch and (if equipped) solenoid.
Installation is the reverse of removal.

**Lock cylinder** (illustration 11.6): disconnect the control link and electrical connector
(if equipped) from the lock cylinder. Use pliers to slide the retaining clip off and remove
the lock cylinder from the door. Installation is the reverse of removal.

**Inside handle (1984 through 1987 models only)** (illustration 11.8): remove the retaining
bolts, rotate the handle away and detach it from the door. Installation is the reverse of
removal.

**Outside handle** (illustration 11.12): disconnect the control link from the handle. Remove
the nuts and detach the handle from the door. Installation is the reverse of removal.

## 12. Door - removal, installation and adjustment (PDF p.239-240) <a id="p239"></a>

Remove the door trim panel (Section 10). Disconnect any electrical connectors and push them
through the door opening so they don't interfere with removal. Position a jack or jackstands
under the door, or have an assistant support it, when the hinge bolts are removed
(illustration 12.2 — *if using a jack/stand, place a rag between it and the door to protect
the paint*). Scribe around the door bolts (illustration 12.3). Remove the hinge-to-door bolts
and carefully detach the door. Installation is the reverse of removal.

**Adjustment (following installation):** (a) on front and rear doors, up-and-down and
forward-and-backward adjustments are made by loosening the hinge-to-body or hinge-to-door
bolts and moving the door as necessary — a special offset tool may be required to reach some
bolts (illustration 12.5a); (b) on hatchback and station wagon back doors, left-right and
forward-and-backward adjustments are made by loosening the hinge-to-body bolts; (c) the door
lock striker can also be adjusted up-and-down and sideways for positive engagement with the
locking mechanism — loosen the screws and move the striker as necessary (illustration 12.5b —
tap the striker with a soft-face hammer in the desired direction).

## 16. Trunk lid/rear hatch - removal, installation and adjustment (PDF p.241-243) <a id="p241"></a>

Open the trunk lid and cover the compartment edges with pads/cloths to protect painted
surfaces when the lid is removed. Disconnect any interfering cables/electrical connectors.
Make alignment marks around the hinge bolt mounting flanges (illustrations 16.3a — mark
around the hinge bolts so you can return the rear hatch to the same position; 16.3b — mark
around the hinge bolt mounting flange (1) so you can reinstall the lid in its original
location; unscrew or loosen the trunk lid-to-hinge bolts (2) to remove or adjust it). On
hatchback models, have an assistant support the hatch and detach the support struts
(illustration 16.4 — use an open-end wrench to detach the strut end from the rear hatch).
While an assistant supports the lid/hatch, remove the lid-to-hinge bolts on both sides and
lift it off. Installation is the reverse of removal — *align the lid-to-hinge bolts with the
marks made during removal.*

After installation, close the lid to confirm proper alignment with surrounding panels.
Fore-and-aft and side-to-side adjustments are controlled by the position of the hinge bolts
in the slots — loosen the hinge bolts, reposition the lid, retighten. The height of the lid
relative to surrounding panels when closed can be changed by loosening the lock and/or
striker bolts, repositioning the striker, and tightening (illustrations 16.8a — the trunk
lid/rear hatch striker position can be changed after loosening the retaining screw; 16.8b —
to change the engagement, loosen the three latch mounting screws).

## 17. Radiator grille - removal and installation (PDF p.243) <a id="p243"></a>

On early models, the grille extends beyond the parking-turn signal lights — remove those
light assemblies first. Disengage the grille retaining clips — squeeze expansion-type
retainers with needle-nose pliers (illustration 17.2a); lift tab-type retainers out of their
slots with a screwdriver (illustration 17.2b). *Some models use one or more screws in
addition to clips.* Once all retaining clips/screws are disengaged, pull the grille out and
remove it.

To install: press the grille into place until the clips lock in position. *Caution: don't use
force — if the clips won't engage readily, use pliers or a screwdriver to ease them into
their locked positions.* Install the screws (if used).

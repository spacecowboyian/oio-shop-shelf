## 2. Engine overhaul - general information (PDF p.90-91) <a id="p90"></a>

Determining whether a complete overhaul is needed isn't always easy — high mileage doesn't
necessarily mean an overhaul is needed, and low mileage doesn't preclude one. Frequency of
servicing is probably the most important factor: an engine with regular oil/filter changes
and other required maintenance will likely give many thousands of reliable miles; a
neglected engine may need an overhaul early. Excessive oil consumption indicates piston
rings, valve seals, and/or valve guides need attention — rule out oil leaks first before
blaming rings/guides. Perform a cylinder compression check (Section 3) to determine the
extent of work required. Check oil pressure with a gauge in place of the oil pressure
sending unit (illustrations 2.4a — sending unit location, front of block adjacent to the
oil filter; 2.4b — gauge installed in the hole) and compare to Specifications — extremely
low pressure suggests worn bearings and/or oil pump. Loss of power, rough running,
knocking/metallic noises, excessive valve train noise, and high fuel consumption may also
point to needing an overhaul, especially in combination — if a complete tune-up doesn't fix
it and the engine idles slightly rough with one cylinder more affected than others, a worn
exhaust lobe on the camshaft could be the cause.

An overhaul restores internal parts to new-engine specifications: piston rings are replaced
and cylinder walls reconditioned (rebored and/or honed); a machine-shop rebore means new
oversize pistons too. Main, connecting rod, and camshaft bearings are generally replaced
with new ones; the crankshaft may be reground to restore journals. Valves are usually
serviced as well. Other components (distributor, starter, alternator) can be rebuilt at the
same time.

> *Note (as printed): critical cooling system components — hoses, drivebelts, thermostat,
> water pump — MUST be replaced with new parts when an engine is overhauled. The radiator
> should be checked carefully for clogging or leaking (Chapter 3).*

Read through the entire procedure before starting to understand the scope. Not difficult,
but time-consuming — plan on the vehicle being tied up at least two weeks, especially if
parts go to a machine shop. Check parts availability and obtain special tools/equipment in
advance. Automotive machine shops often handle part inspection and reconditioning/
replacement advice. *Note: always wait until the engine is completely disassembled and all
components — especially the block — have been inspected before deciding what work is
needed; the block's condition is the major factor in deciding whether to overhaul the
original engine or buy a rebuilt one — never purchase parts or have machine work done on
other components until the block has been thoroughly inspected. Time is the primary cost of
an overhaul, so it doesn't pay to install worn or substandard parts.* Everything must be
assembled with care in a spotlessly clean environment for maximum life and minimum trouble.

## 3. Compression check (PDF p.91-92) <a id="p91"></a>

*Note (as printed): the engine must be at normal operating temperature and the battery must
be fully charged for this check.*

A compression check reveals the mechanical condition of the upper end (pistons, rings,
valves, head gasket) — specifically whether compression is down due to worn rings,
defective valves/seats, or a blown head gasket. Clean the area around the spark plugs
before removing them (compressed air, a small brush, or a bicycle tire pump — to keep dirt
out of the cylinders). Remove all spark plugs (Chapter 1). Block the throttle wide open. On
vehicles with separate ignition coils, detach the coil wire from the distributor cap center
and ground it on the engine block with a jumper wire (alligator clips both ends, for a good
ground). On vehicles with the IIA integral coil and distributor, disconnect the ignition
primary wiring. On EFI-equipped vehicles, also disable the fuel pump circuit (Chapter 4).
Install the compression gauge in the spark plug hole (illustration 3.6 — a gauge with a
threaded fitting is preferred over the hand-pressure type; *be sure to open the throttle
valve, and the choke plate on carbureted models, as far as possible during the check*).

Crank the engine over at least seven compression strokes, watching the gauge. Compression
should build up quickly in a healthy engine. Low compression on the first stroke followed
by gradually increasing pressure on successive strokes indicates worn piston rings. A low
first-stroke reading that doesn't build up indicates leaking valves or a blown head gasket
(a cracked head is also possible). Deposits on the valve head undersides can also cause low
compression. Record the highest gauge reading obtained. Repeat for the remaining cylinders
and compare to this Chapter's Specifications.

Add about three squirts of engine oil (plunger-type oil can) to each cylinder through the
spark plug hole and repeat the test. If compression increases after the oil is added, the
piston rings are definitely worn; if it doesn't increase significantly, the leakage is at
the valves or head gasket (burned valve seats/faces, or warped/cracked/bent valves). If two
adjacent cylinders have equally low compression, there's a strong possibility the head
gasket between them is blown — coolant in the combustion chambers or crankcase would verify
this.

If compression is unusually high, the combustion chambers are probably carbon-coated —
remove the head and decarbonize. If compression is way down or varies greatly between
cylinders, have a leak-down test performed by an automotive repair shop — it pinpoints
exactly where and how severely leakage is occurring.

## 4. Engine removal - methods and precautions (PDF p.92) <a id="p92"></a>

Locate a suitable, adequate work/storage space (a garage or, at minimum, a flat, level,
clean concrete/asphalt surface). Clean the engine compartment and engine beforehand. An
engine hoist or A-frame is necessary, rated in excess of the combined engine + transaxle
weight — safety is paramount given the hazards of lifting an engine out. If removed by a
novice, have a helper available; one person often cannot perform every step alone. Plan the
operation ahead; arrange for or obtain all needed tools/equipment in advance (in addition to
an engine hoist: a heavy-duty floor jack, complete wrench/socket sets, wooden blocks, and
plenty of rags/cleaning solvent for spilled oil/coolant/gasoline). If renting a hoist,
arrange it in advance and do all possible prep work beforehand to save time/money.

Plan for the vehicle to be out of use for a while — a machine shop will be needed for work
beyond a do-it-yourselfer's equipment, and these shops are often busy, so consult them
before removing the engine to estimate turnaround time.

> **WARNING** — always be extremely careful when removing and installing the engine.
> Serious injury can result from careless actions. Plan ahead, take your time.

## 5. Engine - removal and installation (PDF p.92-95) <a id="p92"></a>

*Note (as printed): read through the entire Section before beginning. The engine and
transaxle are removed as a unit and separated outside the vehicle.*

**Removal:** on fuel-injected models, relieve the fuel system pressure (Chapter 4). Remove
the battery and coolant reservoir (Chapters 3, 5). Place protective covers on the fenders
and cowl, remove the hood (Chapter 11). Remove the air cleaner assembly (Chapter 4). Raise
and support the vehicle securely on jackstands. Remove the lower splash guards. Drain the
cooling system and engine oil, remove the drivebelts (Chapter 1). Clearly label (masking
tape/paint) then disconnect all vacuum lines, coolant/emissions hoses, wiring harness
connectors, ground straps, and fuel lines (illustration 5.6) — take instant photos or
sketch component/bracket locations. Remove the cooling fan(s) and radiator (Chapter 3).
Release residual fuel pressure in the tank by removing the gas cap, then undo the fuel
lines connecting the engine to the chassis (Chapter 4) — plug/cap all open fittings.
Disconnect the throttle linkage (and TV linkage/speed control cable-actuator if equipped)
from the engine (Chapter 4). On power-steering-equipped vehicles, unbolt the pump — tie it
aside without disconnecting hoses if clearance allows, otherwise remove it (Chapter 10). On
air-conditioned vehicles, unbolt the compressor and set it aside without disconnecting the
refrigerant hoses (illustration 5.11). Detach the exhaust pipe from the exhaust manifold.
Remove the driveaxles (Chapter 8), wire harness, shift linkage, and speedometer cable from
the transaxle (Chapter 7). On automatic transaxle models, disconnect the two oil cooler
lines (Chapter 7B). On manual transaxle models, unbolt the clutch release cylinder without
disconnecting the hose (Chapter 7A).

Attach a lifting sling to the engine's lifting brackets (illustration 5.16), position a
hoist, connect and take up slack until there's tension. Recheck that nothing except the
mounts still connects the engine/transaxle to the vehicle — disconnect anything remaining.
Support the transaxle with a floor jack (block of wood on the jack head to protect it);
remove the bolts/nuts from the engine and transaxle mounts (illustration 5.18).

> **WARNING** — do not place any part of your body under the engine/transaxle when it's
> supported only by a hoist or other lifting device.

Slowly lift the engine/transaxle out of the vehicle (illustrations 5.19a, 5.19b) — may need
to pry the mounts away from the frame brackets. Move it away from the vehicle (illustration
5.20) and carefully lower the hoist until the transaxle is supported level.

On automatic-transaxle models: detach the torque converter dust shield from the lower
bellhousing, remove the torque converter-to-driveplate fasteners (Chapter 7) and push the
converter back slightly into the bellhousing. Remove the engine-to-transaxle bolts and
separate the engine from the transaxle — the torque converter should stay in the transaxle.
Place the engine on the floor, or remove the flywheel/driveplate and mount the engine on an
engine stand.

**Installation:** check the engine/transaxle mounts, replace if worn/damaged. On
manual-transaxle models, inspect the clutch components (Chapter 8); on automatic models,
inspect the converter seal and bushing. On automatic models, apply a dab of grease to the
converter nose and seal lips. Carefully guide the transaxle into place per Chapter 7.
*Caution: DO NOT use the bolts to force the engine and transaxle into alignment — this may
crack or damage major components.* Install the engine-to-transaxle bolts and tighten
securely. Attach the hoist to the engine and carefully lower the engine/transaxle assembly
into the compartment. Install the mount bolts and tighten securely. Reinstall the remaining
components/fasteners in reverse order. Add coolant, oil, power steering, and transmission
fluids as needed (Chapter 1). Run the engine and check for proper operation and leaks; shut
off and recheck fluid levels.

## 6. Engine rebuilding alternatives (PDF p.95) <a id="p95"></a>

The number-one consideration is the block's condition; other factors: cost, machine-shop
access, parts availability, time, and the do-it-yourselfer's mechanical experience.
Alternatives:

- **Individual parts** — if inspection shows the block and most components are reusable,
  purchasing individual parts may be most economical. The block, crankshaft, and
  piston/connecting rod assemblies should still be carefully inspected; even a
  little-worn block's cylinder bores should be surface honed.
- **Short block** — a block with crankshaft and piston/connecting rod assemblies already
  installed, all new bearings, correct clearances. The existing camshaft, valve train,
  cylinder head, and external parts bolt on with little/no machine shop work.
- **Long block** — a short block plus oil pump, oil pan, cylinder head, valve cover,
  camshaft/valve train, timing sprockets/belt/cover — all with new bearings, seals, and
  gaskets throughout. Only manifolds and external parts need installing.

Discuss the best option with local machine shops, parts dealers, and experienced rebuilders
before ordering parts.

## 7. Engine overhaul - disassembly sequence (PDF p.95-97) <a id="p95"></a>

It's much easier to work on the engine mounted on a portable engine stand (often cheap to
rent) — remove the flywheel/driveplate and rear oil seal retainer before mounting. Without a
stand, the engine can be disassembled blocked up on the floor — be extra careful not to tip
or drop it.

If obtaining a rebuilt engine, all external components must come off first to transfer to
the replacement (same as for a full overhaul): alternator and brackets; emissions control
components; distributor, spark plug wires, spark plugs; thermostat and housing cover; water
pump; carburetor or fuel injection components; intake/exhaust manifolds; oil filter; engine
mounts; clutch and flywheel or driveplate; engine rear plate. *Note: pay close attention to
gasket/seal/spacer/pin/bracket/washer/bolt installed positions and other small-item details
that matter for reinstallation.*

If obtaining a short block (engine block, crankshaft, pistons, connecting rods assembled),
the cylinder head, oil pan, and oil pump must also be removed — see Section 6 for the
different rebuild alternatives.

For a complete overhaul, disassemble the internal components in this order (per illustrations
7.5a-7.5d and 1.5a-1.5d in Part A): valve cover; intake and exhaust manifolds; timing belt
covers; timing belt and sprockets; camshafts; cylinder head; oil pan; oil pump;

piston ring groove cleaning tool; electric drill motor; tap and die set; wire brushes; oil
gallery brushes; cleaning solvent. (See Section 20 for the reassembly tool/materials list.)

## 8. Cylinder head - disassembly (PDF p.96-97) <a id="p96"></a>

*Note (as printed): new/rebuilt cylinder heads are commonly available for most engines at
dealerships and auto parts stores — given the specialized tools needed for
disassembly/inspection and possible replacement-part scarcity, it may be more practical and
economical to purchase a replacement head than to disassemble/inspect/recondition the
original.*

Assumes lifters or rocker arms and camshaft(s) have already been removed (see Part A).
Before removing the valves, arrange to label and store them with their related components
so they can be reinstalled in the same guides (illustration 8.2 — several plastic bags, one
per valve). Compress the springs on the first valve with a spring compressor and remove the
keepers (illustration 8.3); carefully release the compressor and remove the retainer,
spring, and spring seat (if used).

> *Caution: be very careful not to nick or otherwise damage the lifter bores when

or whetstone. Repeat for the remaining valves, keeping each valve's parts together for
reinstallation in the same location. Once all valves/related components are removed and
stored, thoroughly clean and inspect the head (finish disassembling the whole engine first
if doing a complete overhaul, before starting head cleaning/inspection).

## 9. Cylinder head - cleaning and inspection (PDF p.97-99) <a id="p97"></a>

*Note (as printed): if the engine was severely overheated, the cylinder head is probably
warped (see Step 12/warpage check below).*

**Cleaning:** scrape all old gasket material/sealing compound off the head gasket, intake
manifold, and exhaust manifold sealing surfaces — very careful not to gouge the head
(special gasket-removal solvents that soften gaskets are available at auto parts stores).
Remove all built-up scale from the coolant passages. Run a stiff wire brush through the
various holes to clear deposits. Run an appropriately-sized tap into each threaded hole to
remove corrosion/thread sealant; clear debris with compressed air if available.

> **WARNING** — wear eye protection when using compressed air.

Clean the exhaust and intake manifold stud threads with a wire brush. Clean the head with
solvent and dry thoroughly (compressed air speeds drying and ensures all holes/recesses are
clean). *Note: decarbonizing chemicals are available and useful for cleaning heads and
valve train components — very caustic, use with caution and follow container instructions.*
Clean the lifters or rocker arms with solvent, dry thoroughly, don't mix them up.
Compressed air speeds drying and can clean out oil passages. Clean all valve springs, spring
seats, keepers, and retainers with solvent, dry thoroughly — do one valve's components at a
time to avoid mixing parts. Scrape off heavy valve deposits, then use a motorized wire brush
on the valve heads/stems — again, don't mix valves up.

**Inspection:** *Note (as printed): perform all inspection procedures before concluding
machine shop work is required — make a list of items needing attention. Lifter/rocker arm
and camshaft inspection procedures are in Part A.*

Cylinder head — inspect very carefully for cracks, coolant leakage evidence, and other
damage; if cracks are found, consult a machine shop about repair (a new head is needed if
repair isn't possible). Using a straightedge and feeler gauge, check the head gasket and
manifold mating surfaces for warpage (illustration 9.12) — a machine shop can resurface it
if warpage exceeds the Specifications limit. Examine the valve seats in each combustion
chamber — pitted, cracked, or burned seats require valve service beyond the home mechanic's
scope. Check the valve stem-to-guide clearance with a small hole gauge and micrometer
(illustration 9.14) and compare to Specifications; if still in doubt about guide condition,
have a machine shop check them (minimal cost).

Valves — carefully inspect each valve face for uneven wear, deformation, cracks, pits, and
burned areas. Check the stem for scuffing/galling and the neck for cracks. Rotate and check
for bending; look for pits/excessive wear on the stem end. Any of these conditions means
valve service by a machine shop is needed. Measure the margin width on each valve
(illustration 9.16) — replace any valve with a margin narrower than specified.

Valve components — check each spring for wear (on the ends) and pits. Measure free length
(illustration 9.17) and compare to Specifications — sagged (shorter-than-spec) springs
shouldn't be reused; have all springs' tension checked with a special fixture at a machine
shop before deciding they're suitable for a rebuilt engine. Stand each spring on a flat
surface and check squareness (illustration 9.18) — replace all springs if any are distorted
or sagged. Check spring retainers and keepers for obvious wear/cracks — replace questionable
parts (extensive damage can occur if they fail during operation). Replace any damaged or
excessively worn parts.

If the inspection shows valve components are generally poor/worn beyond limits (usual for an
overhauled engine), reassemble the valves in the head and refer to Section 10 for valve
servicing.

## 10. Valves - servicing (PDF p.99-100) <a id="p99"></a>

Given the complex nature of the job and special tools/equipment needed, a "valve job"
(valves, valve seats, valve guides) should be done by a professional. The home mechanic can
remove/disassemble the head, do the initial cleaning/inspection, then reassemble and deliver
it to a dealer or machine shop — this lets you see the head/valvetrain condition and know
what work/parts are needed.

The shop will remove the valves and springs, recondition or replace the valves and seats,
recondition the valve guides, check/replace the springs/retainers/keepers as necessary,
replace the valve seals with new ones, reassemble the valve components, and confirm correct
installed spring height; the cylinder head gasket surface will also be resurfaced if warped.
After the valve job, clean the head again before installing on the engine (remove metal
particles/abrasive grit from the service work). Use compressed air, if available, to blow
out all oil holes and passages.

> **WARNING** — wear eye protection.

## 11. Cylinder head - reassembly (PDF p.100-101) <a id="p100"></a>

Refer to illustration 11.3. Make sure the head is clean before reassembly, regardless of
whether it was sent out for valve service (if so, valves/related components will already be
in place). Install new seals on each valve guide. *Note: intake and exhaust valves may
require different seals — DO NOT mix them up! The original seals on 4A-F and 4A-FE engines
are color-coded — intake brown, exhaust black. Check the gasket set instructions to confirm
proper installation.* Gently tap each intake valve seal into place until seated on the guide
(illustration 11.3).

> *Caution: don't hammer on the valve seals once seated — you may damage them. Don't twist
> or cock the seals during installation or they won't seat properly on the valve stems.*

Beginning at one end of the head, lubricate and install the first valve (moly-base grease or
clean engine oil on the stem). Drop the spring seat or shim(s) over the valve guide and set
the spring and retainer in place. Compress the springs with a valve spring compressor and
carefully install the keepers in the upper groove, then slowly release the compressor and
confirm the keepers seat properly — a small dab of grease on each keeper holds it in place
if needed. Repeat for the remaining valves, keeping each valve's parts together (don't mix
them up).

## 12. Pistons/connecting rods - removal (PDF p.100-102) <a id="p100"></a>

*Note (as printed): prior to removing the piston/connecting rod assemblies, remove the
cylinder head, oil pan, and oil pump pick-up tube per the appropriate Sections in Chapter 2.*

Feel with a fingernail for a ridge at the upper limit of ring travel (~1/4-inch down from
each cylinder's top). If carbon deposits/cylinder wear have produced ridges, remove them
completely with a special tool per the manufacturer's instructions (illustration 12.1).

> *Failure to remove the ridges before attempting to remove the piston/connecting rod
> assemblies may result in piston breakage.*

Turn the engine upside-down so the crankshaft faces up. Before removing the connecting rods,
check endplay with feeler gauges — slide them between the first connecting rod and the
crankshaft throw until play is removed (illustration 12.3); the endplay equals the feeler
gauge thickness. If endplay exceeds the service limit, new connecting rods are required
(consult a machine shop if endplay falls under the specified minimum with new rods/
crankshaft — the rods may need machining). Repeat for the remaining rods.

Check the connecting rods and caps for identification numbers — if not plainly marked, mark
them with a center punch (1, 2, 3, 4 per cylinder, illustration 12.4). *Note: don't mistake
bearing grade marks for cylinder numbers!* Loosen each connecting rod cap nut 1/2-turn at a
time until removable by hand. Remove the No. 1 connecting rod cap and bearing insert
(careful not to drop the insert). Slip a short length of plastic or rubber hose over each
connecting rod cap bolt to protect the crankshaft journal and cylinder wall (illustration
12.6). Remove the bearing insert and push the connecting rod/piston assembly out through the
top of the engine using a wooden hammer handle on the upper bearing surface in the
connecting rod — if resistance is felt, double-check that all the ridge was removed. Repeat
for the remaining cylinders.

After removal, reassemble the connecting rod caps and bearing inserts in their respective
rods and install the cap nuts finger tight — leaving the old inserts in place until
reassembly protects the bearing surfaces from accidental nicks/gouges. Don't separate the
pistons from the connecting rods (see Section 17 for more).

## 13. Crankshaft - removal (PDF p.102-103) <a id="p102"></a>

*Note (as printed): the crankshaft can be removed only after the engine has been removed
from the vehicle. Assumes the flywheel/driveplate, crankshaft pulley, timing belt, oil pan,
oil pick-up tube, oil pump, and piston/connecting rod assemblies have already been removed.
The rear main oil seal retainer must be unbolted and separated from the block before
proceeding.*

Before removal, check endplay: mount a dial indicator with the stem in line with the
crankshaft, just touching its end (illustration 13.1). Push the crankshaft all the way to
the rear and zero the indicator; pry the crankshaft to the front as far as possible and read
the endplay. If greater than specified, check the crankshaft thrust surfaces for wear —
new thrust bearings should correct the endplay if no wear is evident. Without a dial
indicator, feeler gauges work: gently pry/push the crankshaft fully forward, slip feeler
gauges between the crankshaft and the front face of the thrust main bearing (illustration
13.3) — the thrust bearing is No. 3 (the center one).

Check the main bearing caps for location markings, numbered consecutively front-to-rear —
if unmarked, mark with number stamping dies or a center punch (caps generally have a cast-in
arrow pointing to the front of the engine). Loosen the main bearing cap bolts 1/4-turn at a
time, in the recommended sequence (illustration 13.4), until removable by hand — note any
stud bolts and ensure they return to their original locations. Gently tap the caps with a
soft-face hammer, then separate them from the block (use the bolts as levers if needed) —
try not to drop the bearing inserts. Carefully lift the crankshaft out (it's quite heavy —
an assistant helps). With the bearing inserts in place in the block and caps, return the
caps to their respective locations and tighten the bolts finger tight.

## 14. Engine block - cleaning (PDF p.103-104) <a id="p103"></a>

> *Caution: the core plugs (freeze/soft plugs) may be difficult or impossible to retrieve if
> driven into the block.*

Drill a small hole in the center of each core plug and pull them out with an auto body-type
dent puller (illustration 14.1). Using a gasket scraper, remove all gasket material from the
block, careful not to nick/gouge the sealing surfaces. Remove the main bearing caps or cap
assembly and separate the bearing inserts from the caps and block — tag each bearing with
its cylinder and cap-vs-block position, set aside. Remove all threaded oil gallery plugs
(usually very tight — may need drilling out and retapping); use new plugs on reassembly.

If extremely dirty, take the block to a machine shop for steam cleaning or hot tanking.
After it's returned (or if cleaning yourself), clean all oil holes/galleries thoroughly with
purpose-made brushes, flush passages with warm water until clear, dry completely, and coat
machined surfaces with light rust-preventive oil. If not extremely dirty/sludged, hot soapy
water and a stiff brush can do an adequate job — take plenty of time.

The threaded holes must be clean for accurate torque readings during reassembly — run the
proper-size tap into each hole to remove rust/corrosion/thread sealant/sludge and restore
damaged threads (illustration 14.8); clear debris with compressed air if possible. Also
clean the threads on the head bolts and main bearing cap bolts now. Reinstall the main
bearing caps and tighten the bolts finger tight.

Coat the sealing surfaces of the new core plugs with Permatex No. 2 sealant (or equivalent)
and install them in the block (illustration 14.10) — make sure they're driven in straight
and seated properly, or leakage could result. A large socket with an OD that just slips
into the core plug, a 1/2-inch drive extension, and a hammer work as well as special tools.
Apply non-hardening sealant (Permatex No. 2 or Teflon pipe sealant) to the new oil gallery
plugs and thread them into the block, tightened securely.

If not reassembling right away, cover the block with a large plastic trash bag to keep it
clean.

## 15. Engine block - inspection (PDF p.104-107) <a id="p104"></a>

Clean the block first (Section 14). Visually check for cracks, rust, and corrosion; look for
stripped threads in threaded holes. Also have the block checked for hidden cracks at a
machine shop with the special equipment for this — if defects are found, have the block
repaired if possible, or replaced. Check the cylinder bores for scuffing and scoring.

Measure the diameter of each cylinder at the top (just under the ridge area), center, and
bottom, parallel to the crankshaft axis (illustrations 15.4a-15.4c). *Note: these
measurements should NOT be made with the bare block mounted on an engine stand — the
cylinders will be distorted and the measurements inaccurate.* Then measure each cylinder's
diameter at the same three locations across the crankshaft axis. Compare results to
Specifications. Out-of-round is the difference between the two perpendicular measurements
(A vs. B); taper is the difference between top and bottom at the same orientation. Without
precision measuring tools, feeler gauge stock can approximate piston-to-cylinder clearance:
select a feeler gauge and slip it into the cylinder along with the matching (ringless)
piston, positioned exactly as normal, on one of the thrust faces (90° to the piston pin
bore); the piston should slip through with moderate pressure — if it falls/slides through
easily, clearance is excessive and a new piston is needed; binding at the bottom but loose
toward the top indicates a tapered cylinder; tight spots as the piston-feeler gauge is
rotated indicate an out-of-round cylinder. Repeat for the remaining pistons/cylinders.

If cylinder walls are badly scuffed/scored, or out-of-round/tapered beyond the Specifications
limits, have the block rebored and honed at a machine shop (requires oversize
pistons/rings). Using a precision straightedge and feeler gauge, check the block deck (head
gasket mating surface) for distortion (illustration 15.12) — a machine shop can resurface it
if beyond the limit. If cylinders are in reasonably good condition and piston-to-cylinder
clearances can be properly maintained, reboring isn't necessary — honing alone suffices
(Section 16). On 1989 and later 4A-F/4A-FE/4A-GE engines, original bores and pistons came in
three sizes — note the size stamped on the block and pistons (illustrations 15.14a, 15.14b).

## 16. Cylinder honing (PDF p.106-107) <a id="p106"></a>

*Note (as printed): if you don't have the tools or don't want to tackle honing, most
machine shops will do it for a reasonable fee.*

Before honing, install the main bearing caps or cap assembly (without bearing inserts) and
tighten to the Specifications torque. Two common hone types: flex ("bottle brush") hone —
easier for beginners — and the traditional spring-loaded-stone surfacing hone. Procedure with
either: mount the hone in a drill motor, compress the stones, and slip it into the first
cylinder (illustration 16.3a).

> **WARNING** — wear safety goggles or a face shield!

Lubricate the cylinder with plenty of honing oil, turn on the drill, and move the hone
up-and-down at a pace producing a fine crosshatch pattern intersecting at approximately 60°
(illustration 16.3b) — use plenty of lubricant, remove no more material than necessary.
*Note: piston ring manufacturers may specify a smaller crosshatch angle than the traditional
60° — follow any instructions included with new rings.* Don't withdraw the hone while
running — shut off the drill first and continue moving the hone until it fully stops, then
compress the stones and withdraw (for a bottle-brush hone: stop the drill, then turn the
chuck in the normal rotation direction while withdrawing). Wipe the oil out and repeat for
the remaining cylinders.

After honing, chamfer the top edges of the cylinder bores with a small file so rings won't
catch during piston installation — very careful not to nick the cylinder walls with the
file end. Wash the entire block again thoroughly with warm soapy water to remove abrasive
grit — the bores are clean when a lint-free white cloth dampened with clean engine oil
wiped through doesn't pick up more gray honing residue. Run a brush through all oil
holes/galleries and flush with running water. Dry the block and apply a coat of light rust
preventive oil to all machined surfaces; wrap in a plastic trash bag and set aside until
reassembly.

## 17. Pistons/connecting rods - inspection (PDF p.106-109) <a id="p106"></a>

Before inspecting, clean the piston/connecting rod assemblies and remove the original piston
rings. *Note (as printed): always use new piston rings when the engine is reassembled.*
Using a piston ring installation tool, carefully remove the rings from the pistons, careful
not to nick/gouge the pistons. Scrape all carbon from the top of the piston — a handheld
wire brush or fine emery cloth works once the majority is scraped away. *Do not, under any
circumstances, use a wire brush mounted in a drill motor on the pistons — the soft piston
material may erode.* Use a piston ring groove cleaning tool to remove carbon from the ring
grooves (illustrations 17.4a, 17.4b — a piece broken off the old ring substitutes if no tool
is available); remove only carbon deposits — don't remove metal or nick/scratch the groove
sides. Clean the piston/rod assemblies with solvent, dry with compressed air if available;
confirm the oil return holes in the ring groove backs and the rod's lower end oil hole are
clear.

If pistons and cylinder walls aren't damaged/excessively worn and the block isn't rebored,
new pistons aren't necessary (normal wear = even vertical wear on thrust surfaces, slight top
ring looseness) — but new rings should always be used. Carefully inspect each piston for
cracks around the skirt, at the pin bosses, and at ring lands. Look for scoring/scuffing on
the skirt thrust faces, holes in the crown, and burned areas at the crown edge. A
scored/scuffed skirt suggests overheating and/or abnormal combustion — check the cooling and
lubrication systems thoroughly; a hole in the crown indicates abnormal combustion
(preignition); burned areas at the crown edge usually indicate spark knock (detonation). Any
of these require correcting the underlying cause (intake air leaks, incorrect fuel/air
mixture, incorrect ignition timing, EGR malfunctions) or the damage will recur. Small pits
(corrosion) indicate coolant leaking into the combustion chamber and/or crankcase — again,
correct the cause.

Measure piston ring side clearance by laying a new ring in each groove and slipping a feeler
gauge in beside it (illustration 17.10), at three or four points around each groove — use
the correct ring for each groove (they differ). If side clearance exceeds spec, new pistons
are needed. Check piston-to-bore clearance by measuring the bore (Section 15) and piston
diameter, confirming correct piston/bore matching (some pistons come in several standard
sizes/grades) — measure the piston across the skirt, 90° to the pin, at the specified
distance from the bottom of the piston or the oil ring groove's lower edge (illustrations
17.11a, 17.11b — see distances below). Subtract piston diameter from bore diameter for
clearance; if greater than spec, the block needs reboring with new pistons/rings.

**Piston measurement distances** (as printed): 1984-1986 4A-C engines — 13/64-inch (5mm)
below the oil ring groove. 1987 4A-C pistons — measured 1-1/2 inches (38mm) from the bottom.
4A-GE pistons — measured 1-21/32 inches (42mm) from the bottom. 4A-FE pistons — measured
1-33/64 inches (38.5mm) from the bottom.

Check piston-to-rod clearance by twisting the piston and rod in opposite directions — any

## 18. Crankshaft - inspection (PDF p.107-108) <a id="p107"></a>

Clean the crankshaft with solvent, dry with compressed air if available. Remove all burrs
from the crankshaft oil holes with a stone, file, or scraper (illustration 18.2). Check the
main and connecting rod bearing journals for uneven wear, scoring, pits, and cracks — clean
the oil holes with a stiff brush (illustration 18.3) and flush with solvent. Rub a penny
across each journal several times (illustration 18.4) — if the journal picks up copper from
the penny, it's too rough and must be reground. Check the rest of the crankshaft for cracks
and other damage — it should be magnafluxed to reveal hidden cracks (a machine shop handles
this).

Using a micrometer, measure the main and connecting rod journal diameters and compare to
Specifications (illustration 18.6) — measure at several points around each journal's
circumference to check for out-of-round, and at each end near the crank throws to check for
taper. Crankshaft runout should also be checked, but requires large V-blocks and a dial
indicator — have a machine shop check it if you lack the equipment. If journals are
damaged, tapered, out-of-round, or worn beyond the Specifications limits, have the
crankshaft reground at a machine shop — use the correct size bearing inserts if
reconditioned.

Check the oil seal journals at each end for wear/damage (illustration 18.8) — a worn groove
or nicked/scratched journal will make the new seal leak; a machine shop may be able to
repair the journal by pressing on a thin sleeve, or a new/different crankshaft is needed if
repair isn't feasible. Refer to Section 19 and examine the main and rod bearing inserts.

## 19. Main and connecting rod bearings - inspection and bearing selection (PDF p.108-110) <a id="p108"></a>

**Inspection** (illustration 19.1): even though bearings should be replaced with new ones
during an overhaul, old bearings should be retained for close examination — they can reveal
valuable information about engine condition. Bearing failure occurs from lack of
lubrication, dirt/foreign particles, engine overloading, and corrosion; whatever the cause,
it must be corrected before reassembly to prevent recurrence. When examining, lay the
bearings out on a clean surface in their same general engine positions to match problems to
the corresponding journal.

Dirt/foreign particles enter the engine various ways (left in during assembly, passed
through filters or the PCV system) and end up in the oil, then the bearings — metal chips
from machining and normal wear, plus abrasives left after reconditioning, are common
sources. Large particles score/gouge the bearing and journal rather than embedding. Best
prevention: thoroughly clean all parts and keep everything spotlessly clean during assembly,
plus frequent regular oil/filter changes.

Lack of lubrication (or lubrication breakdown) has interrelated causes: excessive heat
(thins the oil), overloading (squeezes oil from the bearing face), and oil leakage/throw-off
(from excessive bearing clearances, worn oil pump, or high engine speeds). Blocked oil
passages (usually misaligned oil holes in a bearing shell) also starve and destroy a
bearing. When lubrication is the cause, bearing material wipes or extrudes from the steel
backing — temperatures may rise enough to turn the steel backing blue.

Driving habits affect bearing life: full-throttle, low-speed operation (lugging the engine)
puts high loads on bearings, squeezing out the oil film, causing flex and fine cracks
(fatigue failure) — eventually the bearing material loosens in pieces and tears from the
backing. Short-trip driving causes bearing corrosion (insufficient heat to drive off
condensed water/corrosive gases, which collect in the oil as acid and sludge that attacks
the bearing material).

Incorrect bearing installation during assembly also causes failure: tight-fitting bearings
leave insufficient oil clearance, causing oil starvation; dirt/foreign particles trapped
behind an insert create high spots leading to failure.

**Bearing selection** (illustrations 19.9-19.13b): if original bearings are worn/damaged, or
oil clearances are incorrect (Sections 22, 24), use the following procedures to select
correct new bearings. *However, if the crankshaft has been reground, new undersize bearings
must be installed — the following procedure should NOT be used; the machine shop that
reconditions the crankshaft will provide or help select the correct undersize bearings.
Note: undersize main bearings are not supplied by the factory for 4A-C engines. Regardless
of how bearing sizes are determined, use the oil clearance measurement with Plastigage as
the final guide.*

**Main bearings:** for a STANDARD size, install one with the same number as the original
(illustration 19.9). 1984-1986 models: if the original number is obscured, install one
matching the number stamped into the block at the corresponding cap location (illustration
19.10). 1987 and later models: grade numbers are stamped on both the crankshaft and block —
use the applicable table (illustrations 19.11, 19.12a) to select the correct bearing. If the
original main bearing number is obscured on these, locate the main journal grade numbers
stamped into the oil pan mating surface on the block and on the crankshaft. Use the
accompanying tables to determine the correct bearing for each journal (see the 1987-and-
later main bearing size table below).

## 21. Piston rings - installation (PDF p.110-112) <a id="p110"></a>

Before installing new rings, check the end gaps (assumes ring side clearance was already
checked/verified per Section 17). Lay out the piston/connecting rod assemblies and new ring
sets so each ring set stays matched to its piston/cylinder through end-gap measurement and
assembly.

Insert the top (No. 1) ring into the first cylinder and square it with the walls by pushing
it in with the top of the piston (illustration 21.3) — position it near the bottom of the
cylinder, at ring travel's lower limit. Measure the end gap with feeler gauges until one
equal to the gap width is found (illustration 21.4 — should slide between the ring ends with
a slight drag) and compare to Specifications. If the gap is off-spec, double-check you have
the correct rings.

If the gap is too small, replace the rings — **do not file the ends to increase clearance.**
Excess gap isn't critical unless greater than the Specifications maximum (again, double-
check the rings are correct for the engine). Repeat for each ring in the first cylinder and
each ring in the remaining cylinders — keep rings/pistons/cylinders matched.

Once gaps are checked/corrected, install the rings on the pistons. The oil control ring
(lowest) is usually installed first — three components: slip the spacer/expander into the
groove (illustration 21.9a; if an anti-rotation tang is used, ensure it's inserted into the
drilled hole in the ring groove); install the lower side rail (don't use a ring installation
tool on the oil ring side rails — may damage them; instead place one end of the side rail
into the groove between spacer/expander and ring land, hold it firmly, and slide a finger
around the piston while pushing the rail into the groove); install the upper side rail the
same way. After the three oil ring components are installed, confirm both side rails turn
smoothly in the groove.

The No. 2 (middle) ring installs next — usually stamped with a mark that must face up,
toward the piston top. *Note: always follow the instructions printed on the ring
package/box — manufacturers may differ. Do not mix up the top and middle rings — different
cross sections.* Use a piston ring installation tool, mark facing the piston top, slip the
ring into the middle groove (illustration 21.9b context) — don't expand the ring more than
necessary to slide it over the piston. Install the No. 1 (top) ring the same way, mark
facing up — careful not to confuse No. 1 and No. 2 rings (illustration 21.12 notes the
beveled edge on the No. 2 ring faces down). Repeat for the remaining pistons/rings.

## 22. Crankshaft - installation and main bearing oil clearance check (PDF p.111-113) <a id="p111"></a>

Crankshaft installation is the first major reassembly step. Assumes the block and crankshaft
have been cleaned, inspected, and repaired/reconditioned. Position the engine with the
bottom facing up. Remove the main bearing cap bolts and lift out the caps/cap assembly —
lay them out in order. If old bearing inserts are still in place, remove them from the block
and caps; wipe the main bearing surfaces of both with a clean, lint-free cloth (must stay
spotlessly clean).

**Main bearing oil clearance check:** clean the back sides of the new main bearing inserts
and lay the bearing half with the oil groove in each main bearing saddle in the block
(illustration 22.5); lay the other half from each set in the corresponding cap. Confirm the
tab on each insert fits its recess and the oil holes align. *Caution: do not hammer the
bearings into place and don't nick/gouge the bearing faces. No lubrication should be used at
this time.* The thrust bearings install in the No. 3 (center) cap.

Clean the bearing faces in the block and the crankshaft main bearing journals with a clean,
lint-free cloth; check/clean the oil holes in the crankshaft (dirt here goes straight through
the new bearings). Once certain the crankshaft is clean, carefully lay it in position in the
main bearings.

Trim several pieces of appropriately-sized Plastigage (slightly shorter than the bearing
width) and place one piece on each main bearing journal, parallel to the journal axis
(illustration 22.10). Clean the bearing faces in the caps and install the caps in their
positions (don't mix them up), arrows pointing toward the front of the engine. Following the
recommended sequence (illustration), tighten the main bearing cap bolts in three steps to the
Specifications torque. *Don't rotate the crankshaft at any time during this operation!*
Remove the bolts and carefully lift off the caps/cap assembly, keeping them in order —
don't disturb the Plastigage or rotate the crankshaft (tap gently side-to-side with a
soft-face hammer if any cap resists removal). Compare the crushed Plastigage width on each
journal to the envelope scale (illustration 22.14) to get the main bearing oil clearance —
check against Specifications.

If clearance isn't as specified, the bearing inserts may be the wrong size (Section 19) —
but first confirm no dirt/oil got between the inserts and caps/block during measurement. If
the Plastigage is noticeably wider at one end, the journal may be tapered (Section 18).
Carefully scrape all Plastigage material off the journals and/or bearing faces — don't nick
or scratch the bearing faces.

**Final crankshaft installation:** carefully lift the crankshaft out of the engine. Clean
the bearing faces in the block, apply a thin uniform layer of clean moly-base grease or
engine assembly lube to each bearing surface (coat the thrust bearings too). Lubricate the
crankshaft surfaces that contact the oil seals with moly-base grease, engine assembly lube,
or clean engine oil. Confirm the crankshaft journals are clean, then lay it back in place.
Clean the bearing faces in the caps/cap assembly, apply lubricant, install the caps in their
respective positions with arrows pointing toward the front — be sure to install the thrust
bearings (illustrations 22.19a, 22.19b, oil grooves facing OUT against the crank throw).

Apply a light coat of oil to the bolt threads and bolt head undersides, then install them.
Tighten all except the center (No. 3) cap bolts (the one with the thrust bearings) to the
specified torque, working from the center out, approaching final torque in three steps.
Tighten the center cap bolts to 10-to-12 ft-lbs. Tap the ends of the crankshaft
forward/backward with a lead or brass hammer to line up the thrust washer and crankshaft
surfaces. Retighten all main bearing cap bolts to the Specifications torque, following the
recommended sequence.

Rotate the crankshaft several times by hand to check for obvious binding. Check crankshaft
endplay with a feeler gauge or dial indicator (Section 13) — should be correct if the thrust
faces aren't worn/damaged and new thrust bearings were installed. Install a new rear main oil
seal, then bolt the retainer to the block (Section 23).

## 23. Rear main oil seal - installation (PDF p.113-114) <a id="p113"></a>

The crankshaft must be installed first and the main bearing caps bolted in place, then the
new seal installed in the retainer and the retainer bolted to the block. Check the seal
contact surface on the crankshaft very carefully for scratches/nicks that could damage the
new seal lip and cause leaks — if damaged, the only alternative is a new/different
crankshaft.

The old seal can be removed from the retainer by driving it out from the back side with a
hammer and punch (illustration 23.3) — note how far it's recessed before removing it, since
the new seal must be recessed an equal amount. *Be very careful not to scratch or otherwise
damage the retainer bore or oil leaks could develop.* Make sure the retainer is clean, apply
a thin coat of engine oil to the new seal's outer edge. The seal must be pressed squarely
into the bore — hammering isn't recommended; without a press, sandwich the retainer and seal
between two smooth pieces of wood thick enough to distribute force evenly, and press the
seal in with a large vise's jaws — work slowly, ensure the seal enters squarely. As a last
resort, the seal can be tapped in with a hammer, using a block of wood to distribute force
evenly and ensure square entry.

The seal lips must be lubricated with clean engine oil or multi-purpose grease before the
seal/retainer is slipped over the crankshaft and bolted to the block. Use a new gasket and
sealant, and make sure the dowel pins are in place before installing the retainer
(illustration 23.6). Tighten the bolts a little at a time until all are at the Specifications
torque.

## 24. Pistons/connecting rods - installation and rod bearing oil clearance check (PDF p.113-116) <a id="p113"></a>

Before installing, the cylinder walls must be perfectly clean, the top edge of each cylinder
chamfered, and the crankshaft in place. Remove the cap from the end of the No. 1 connecting
rod (per removal marks). Remove the original bearing inserts and wipe the bearing surfaces
of the rod and cap with a clean, lint-free cloth (spotlessly clean).

**Connecting rod bearing oil clearance check:** clean the back of the new upper bearing
insert, lay it in place in the connecting rod, tab fitting the recess so oil holes align —
don't hammer it in, don't nick/gouge the bearing face, no lubrication yet. Clean the back of
the other insert and install it in the rod cap the same way. *It's critically important that
the mating surfaces of the bearing and connecting rod are perfectly clean and oil-free when
assembled.* Position the piston ring gaps at staggered intervals around the piston
(illustration 24.5). Slip a section of plastic or rubber hose over each connecting rod cap
bolt. Lubricate the piston and rings with clean engine oil and attach a piston ring
compressor, leaving the skirt protruding to guide the piston into the cylinder (rings must
be compressed flush with the piston).

Rotate the crankshaft until the No. 1 connecting rod journal is at BDC and apply a coat of
engine oil to the cylinder walls. With the marks on the piston and connecting rod
(illustration 24.9) facing the timing belt end of the engine, gently insert the
piston/connecting rod assembly into the No. 1 cylinder bore, resting the ring compressor's
bottom edge on the block. Tap the top edge of the compressor to confirm full contact around
its circumference. Gently tap the top of the piston with the end of a wooden or plastic
hammer handle (illustration 24.11) while guiding the connecting rod end onto the crankshaft
journal — the rings may try to pop out of the compressor just before entering the bore, so
keep downward pressure on the compressor. Work slowly — if any resistance is felt entering
the cylinder, **stop immediately**, find and fix what's hanging up before proceeding. *Do
not, for any reason, force the piston into the cylinder — you might break a ring and/or the
piston.*

Once installed, the connecting rod bearing oil clearance must be checked before permanently
bolting the rod cap. Cut a piece of appropriately-sized Plastigage (slightly shorter than the
bearing width) and lay it on the No. 1 connecting rod journal, parallel to the journal axis
(illustration 24.13). Clean the connecting rod cap bearing face, remove the protective hoses
from the rod bolts, and install the rod cap — the mating mark on the cap on the same side as
the mark on the rod (illustration 12.4 reference; some engines also have a protrusion on the
cap facing the timing belt end, illustration 24.14). Apply a light coat of oil to the nut
undersides, install and tighten to the Specifications torque, working up to it in three
steps. *Use a thin-wall socket to avoid erroneous torque readings from the socket wedging
between the rod cap and nut — if it tends to wedge, lift up slightly until it no longer
contacts the cap. Do not rotate the crankshaft during this operation.*

Remove the nuts and detach the rod cap, very careful not to disturb the Plastigage. Compare
the crushed Plastigage width to the envelope scale (illustration 24.17) for the oil
clearance; compare to Specifications. If not as specified, the bearing inserts may be wrong
size (Section 19) — first confirm no dirt/oil interfered with the measurement, and recheck
the journal diameter; if the Plastigage was wider at one end, the journal may be tapered
(Section 18).

**Final connecting rod installation:** carefully scrape all Plastigage material off the rod
journal and/or bearing face — use a fingernail or credit card edge, don't scratch the
bearing. Make sure the bearing faces are perfectly clean, then apply a uniform layer of clean
moly-base grease or engine assembly lube to both — push the piston into the cylinder to
expose the bearing insert face in the connecting rod, slipping the protective hoses over the
rod bolts first. Slide the connecting rod back onto the journal, remove the protective hoses
from the cap bolts, install the rod cap, tighten the nuts to the Specifications torque
(three steps). Repeat the entire procedure for the remaining pistons/connecting rods.

**Important points to remember** (as printed): keep the back sides of the bearing inserts
and the insides of the connecting rods/caps perfectly clean when assembling; make sure you
have the correct piston/rod assembly for each cylinder; the dimple on the piston must face
the front (timing belt end) of the engine; lubricate the cylinder walls with clean oil;
lubricate the bearing faces when installing the rod caps after the oil clearance has been
checked.

After all piston/connecting rod assemblies are installed, rotate the crankshaft several
times by hand to check for obvious binding. Finally, check the connecting rod endplay per
Section 12's procedure and compare to Specifications — if it was correct before disassembly
and the original crankshaft/rods were reinstalled, it should still be right; if new rods or
a new crankshaft were installed, endplay may be inadequate, requiring the rods to be removed
and taken to a machine shop for resizing.

## 25. Initial start-up and break-in after overhaul (PDF p.116) <a id="p116"></a>

> **WARNING** — have a fire extinguisher handy when starting the engine for the first time.

Once the engine is installed in the vehicle, double-check the engine oil and coolant levels.
With the spark plugs out and the ignition system disabled (Section 3), crank the engine
until oil pressure registers on the gauge or the warning light goes out. Install the spark
plugs, connect the plug wires, and restore the ignition system functions (Section 3).

Start the engine — it may take a few moments for the fuel system to build pressure, but it
should start without a great deal of effort. After starting, allow it to warm up to normal
operating temperature; while warming up, thoroughly check for fuel, oil, and coolant leaks.
Shut the engine off and recheck the oil and coolant levels.

Drive the vehicle to an area with minimal traffic, accelerate at full throttle from 30 to 50
mph, then allow it to slow to 30 mph with the throttle closed. Repeat 10 or 12 times — this
loads the piston rings and helps them seat properly against the cylinder walls. Check again
for oil and coolant leaks.

Drive gently for the first 500 miles (no sustained high speeds), keeping a constant check on
the oil level — it's not unusual for an engine to use oil during break-in. At approximately
500 to 600 miles, change the oil and filter. For the next few hundred miles, drive normally
— don't pamper it or abuse it. After [mileage unconfirmed — see NEEDS REVIEW] miles, change
the oil and filter again; consider the engine broken in.

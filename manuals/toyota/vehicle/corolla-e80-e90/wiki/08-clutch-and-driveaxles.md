## 1. General information (PDF p.185) <a id="p185"></a>

Covers components from the rear of the engine to the front wheels, excluding the transaxle
(Chapter 7) — grouped into Clutch and Driveaxles. Since nearly all procedures involve
working under the vehicle, make sure it's securely supported on sturdy jackstands or a
hoist.

## 2. Clutch - description and check (PDF p.185-186) <a id="p185"></a>

All manual-transaxle vehicles use a single dry-plate, diaphragm-spring clutch (illustration
2.1 exploded view: 1 flywheel, 2 clutch disc, 3 clutch cover, 4 clutch cover bolts, 5
release bearing, 6 clutch fork, 7 clutch fork ball stud, 8 fork boot). The clutch disc has a
splined hub sliding on the transaxle input shaft splines; the clutch and pressure plate are
held in contact by diaphragm spring pressure. The release system is hydraulic: clutch pedal,
master cylinder/reservoir, hydraulic line, slave cylinder actuating the release lever, and
the release (throwout) bearing. When the pedal is pressed, hydraulic pressure pushes the
outer end of the clutch release lever; as the lever pivots, shaft fingers push the release
bearing, which pushes the pressure plate's diaphragm spring fingers, releasing the clutch
plate.

*Note on terminology (as printed): the driven plate is also called the clutch plate or
disc; the pressure plate assembly is sometimes called the clutch cover; the release bearing
is sometimes called the throwout bearing; the release cylinder is sometimes called the
operating or slave cylinder.*

**Preliminary checks** (beyond replacing obviously-damaged components):
(a) First check the clutch master cylinder fluid level (Chapter 1) — if low, add fluid and
inspect the hydraulic system for leaks; if the reservoir ran dry, bleed the system (Section
7) and re-test.
(b) To check "clutch spin down time": run the engine at normal idle in Neutral (pedal up,
engaged); disengage the clutch (pedal down), wait several seconds, shift into Reverse — no
grinding noise should be heard; grinding most likely indicates a pressure plate or clutch
disc problem.
(c) To check for complete clutch release: run the engine (parking brake applied), hold the
pedal ~1/2-inch from the floor, shift between 1st gear and Reverse several times — a
non-smooth shift indicates component failure. Check the release cylinder pushrod travel —
with the pedal fully depressed it should extend substantially; if not, check the master
cylinder fluid level.
(d) Visually inspect the bushing at the top of the clutch pedal for sticking or excessive
wear.
(e) Under the vehicle, confirm the clutch release lever is solidly mounted on the ball stud.

## 3. Clutch components - removal, inspection and installation (PDF p.185-188) <a id="p185"></a>

> **WARNING** — dust from clutch wear may contain asbestos, hazardous to your health. DO NOT
> blow it out with compressed air and DO NOT inhale it. DO NOT use gasoline or petroleum-
> based solvents to remove the dust — use brake system cleaner to flush it into a drain pan.
> After wiping clutch components clean with a rag, dispose of the contaminated rags and
> cleaner in a labeled, covered container.

**Removal** (illustration 3.6): access is normally via removing the transaxle, leaving the
engine in place. *Note: if the engine is being removed for a major overhaul, always take the
opportunity to check the clutch for wear — the relatively low cost of clutch components
compared to the labor of accessing them warrants replacement any time the engine or
transaxle is out, unless they're new/near-perfect.* Remove the release cylinder (Section 6)
and hang it out of the way with wire — no need to disconnect the hose. Refer to Chapter 7
Part A and remove the transaxle from the vehicle, supporting the engine while it's out
(preferably with an engine hoist from above; if using a jack underneath, put a piece of wood
between the jack and oil pan to spread the load).

> *Caution: the oil pump pickup is very close to the bottom of the oil pan — if the pan is
> bent or distorted, engine oil starvation could occur.*

The release fork and bearing can remain attached to the transaxle for now. Install a clutch
alignment tool through the clutch disc hub to support the disc during removal. Carefully
inspect the flywheel and pressure plate for indexing marks (usually an X, an O, or a white
letter) — scribe your own marks if none are found (illustration 3.6), so the pressure plate
and flywheel realign the same way during installation. Turning each bolt only 1/2-turn at a
time, slowly loosen the pressure plate-to-flywheel bolts in a diagonal pattern until all
spring pressure is relieved; hold the pressure plate securely and completely remove the
bolts, then the pressure plate and clutch disc.

**Inspection** (illustrations 3.10, 3.12a, 3.12b): ordinarily clutch problems trace to wear
of the clutch disc, but inspect all components. Inspect the flywheel for cracks, heat
checking, score marks, other damage — slight imperfections can be resurfaced at a machine
shop (see `02a-engines.md` for the flywheel removal procedure). Inspect the disc lining —
should have at least 1/16-inch above the rivet heads; check for loose rivets, distortion,
cracks, broken springs, other damage (illustration 3.10) — since the disc is ordinarily
replaced as a matter of course, replace it if in any doubt. The release bearing should be
replaced along with the disc (Section 4). Check the pressure plate's machined surface and
diaphragm spring fingers (illustrations 3.12a — replace if normal/excessive finger wear or
broken/bent fingers are noted; 3.12b — check the friction surface for score marks, cracks,
overheating blue spots) — replace the pressure plate assembly if grooved/damaged; light
glazing can be removed with emery cloth or sandpaper; new or factory-rebuilt units are
available if replacement is indicated.

**Installation** (illustration 3.14): before installing, carefully wipe the flywheel and
pressure plate machined surfaces clean — no oil or grease should be on these surfaces or the
disc lining; handle these parts only with clean hands. Position the clutch disc and pressure
plate using a clutch alignment tool (illustration 3.14 — most replacement plates are marked
"flywheel side"; if unmarked, install with the damper springs/cushion toward the transaxle).
Tighten the pressure plate-to-flywheel bolts only finger-tight, working around the plate.
Center the disc by ensuring the alignment tool passes through the splined hub into the
crankshaft recess — wiggle the tool as needed to bottom it. Tighten the bolts a little at a
time in a criss-cross pattern to prevent cover distortion; once snug, tighten to the
Specifications torque; remove the alignment tool.

Using high-temperature grease, lubricate the inner groove of the release bearing (Section 4)
and the release lever contact areas and transaxle input shaft bearing retainer. Install the
release bearing (Section 4). Install the transaxle, slave cylinder, and all previously
removed components.

## 4. Clutch release bearing and fork - removal, inspection and installation (PDF p.188-189) <a id="p188"></a>

> **WARNING** — same asbestos precautions as Section 3.

**Removal** (illustration 4.3): disconnect the negative battery cable. Remove the transaxle
(Chapter 7). Remove the clutch release fork from the ball stud (reach behind the release
lever and disengage it from the ball stud by pulling on the retention spring), then remove
the bearing from the fork.

**Inspection** (illustration 4.4): hold the bearing by the outer race and rotate the inner
race while applying pressure — if it doesn't turn smoothly or is noisy, replace the
bearing/hub assembly. Wipe with a clean rag and inspect for damage/wear/cracks. *Don't
immerse the bearing in solvent — it's sealed for life and this would ruin it.* Also check
the release lever for cracks and bends.

**Installation** (illustrations 4.5, 4.6): fill the inner groove of the release bearing with
high-temperature grease; also apply a light coat to the transaxle input shaft splines and
front bearing retainer. Lubricate the release fork ball socket, fork ends, and release
cylinder pushrod socket with high-temperature grease. Attach the release bearing to the
release fork. Slide the release bearing onto the transaxle input shaft front bearing
retainer while passing the release fork end through the clutch housing opening — push the
fork onto the ball stud until firmly seated. Apply a light coat of high-temperature grease
to the face of the release bearing where it contacts the pressure plate diaphragm fingers.
The remainder of installation is the reverse of removal.

## 5. Clutch master cylinder - removal, overhaul and installation (PDF p.189-191) <a id="p189"></a>

*Note (as printed): before beginning, contact local parts stores and dealers concerning a
rebuild kit or a new master cylinder — availability/cost may dictate rebuild vs. replace. If
rebuilding, inspect the bore per Step 14 before purchasing parts.*

**Removal** (illustrations 5.2, 5.4): disconnect the negative battery cable. Under the
dashboard, disconnect the pushrod from the top of the clutch pedal (held by a clevis pin,
illustration 5.2). On 1988 and later models with the 4A-GE engine, remove the brake booster
(Chapter 9). On all 1988 and later models, disconnect the metal clutch line from the fluid
hose (illustration 5.4) — use a flare-nut wrench on the fitting to avoid rounding it off;
have rags handy for spilled fluid. *Caution: don't allow brake fluid to contact paint — it
will damage the finish.* On all models, disconnect the hydraulic line at the clutch master
cylinder (same flare-nut-wrench/rag precautions). From under the dash, remove the nut
securing the master cylinder to the firewall. Remove the master cylinder, careful not to
spill fluid.

**Overhaul** (illustrations 5.7a — components exploded view, 1989 and earlier models: 1
reservoir bolt, 2 reservoir and cap, 3 clutch line, 4 mounting nut, 5 piston, 6 pushrod, 7
clevis pin, 8 clip, 9 clevis, 10 boot, 11 snap-ring, 12 master cylinder; 5.7b, 5.8, 5.10,
5.19a, 5.19b): remove the reservoir cap and drain all fluid. On 1984-1989 models, remove the
hold-down bolt inside the reservoir and pull off the reservoir; on 1990 and later models, tap
out the retainer pin securing the reservoir, then lift it off. Pull back the dust cover on
the pushrod and remove the snap-ring (illustration 5.8). Remove the retaining washer and
pushrod from the cylinder. Tap the master cylinder on a block of wood to eject the piston
assembly (illustration 5.10). *Note: if the rebuild kit supplies a complete piston assembly,
skip the corresponding disassembly steps.* Separate the spring from the piston. Remove the
spring support, seal, and shim from the pushrod. Carefully remove the seal from the piston.

Inspect the bore for deep scratches, score marks, and ridges — must be smooth to the touch;
replace the master cylinder with a new or factory-rebuilt unit if not. If rebuilding, use
the rebuild kit's new parts per any included instructions; wash reused parts with brake
cleaner, denatured alcohol, or clean brake fluid — **do not use petroleum-based solvents.**
Attach the seal to the piston, lips facing away from the pushrod end. Assemble the shim,
spring support, and spring on the piston's other end. Lubricate the cylinder bore and seals
with plenty of fresh DOT 3 brake fluid. Apply lithium soap-base glycol grease (**not** chassis
grease) to the piston recesses (illustrations 5.19a, 5.19b). Carefully guide the piston
assembly into the bore, careful not to damage the seals — spring end installed first, pushrod
end of the piston closest to the opening. Position the pushrod and retaining washer in the
bore, compress the spring, install a new snap-ring. Install the reservoir with a new grommet.

**Installation:** on 1988 and later models, connect the metal fluid line to the fluid hose
(illustration 5.4). Position the master cylinder on the firewall, mounting nut finger-tight.
Connect the hydraulic line, moving the cylinder slightly as needed to thread the fitting
properly — don't cross-thread it. Tighten the mounting nuts securely, then the hydraulic line
fitting. Install the brake booster if removed (Chapter 9). Inside the vehicle, connect the
pushrod to the clutch pedal. Fill the reservoir with DOT 3 brake fluid and bleed the clutch
system (Section 7). Check the clutch pedal height and freeplay, adjust if necessary (Chapter
1).

## 6. Clutch release cylinder - removal, overhaul and installation (PDF p.191-192) <a id="p191"></a>

*Note (as printed): same rebuild-kit-vs-new sourcing advice as Section 5 — inspect the bore
per Step 8 (below) before purchasing parts if rebuilding.*

**Removal** (illustration 6.3): disconnect the negative battery cable. Raise the vehicle and
support securely on jackstands. Disconnect the hydraulic line at the release cylinder — use a
flare-nut wrench to avoid rounding the fitting; have a small can and rags handy for spilled
fluid. Remove the release cylinder mounting bolts. Remove the release cylinder.

**Overhaul** (illustration 6.6 — components exploded view: 5 piston/seal assembly, 6 spring,
7 mounting bolts, 8 hydraulic line): remove the pushrod and boot. Tap the cylinder on a block
of wood to eject the piston and seal; remove the spring from inside. Carefully inspect the
bore for deep scratches, score marks, ridges — must be smooth to the touch; replace the
release cylinder with a new one if any imperfections are found. Using the rebuild kit's new
parts, assemble with plenty of fresh brake fluid for lubrication — note the installed
direction of the spring and seal.

**Installation:** install the release cylinder on the clutch housing, pushrod seated in the
release fork pocket. Connect the hydraulic line, tighten securely. Fill the master cylinder
with DOT 3 brake fluid. Bleed the system (Section 7). Lower the vehicle and connect the
negative battery cable.

## 7. Clutch hydraulic system - bleeding (PDF p.192) <a id="p192"></a>

Bleed all air from the system whenever any part has been removed, or if the fluid level has
dropped so low that air was drawn into the master cylinder — the procedure is very similar to
bleeding a brake system. Fill the master cylinder with new DOT 3 brake fluid.

> *Caution: do not re-use any fluid coming from the system during bleeding, or fluid that's
> been in an open container for an extended time.*

Raise the vehicle and support securely on jackstands to access the release cylinder (left
side of the clutch housing). Remove the dust cap over the bleeder valve and push a length of
plastic hose over the valve; place the other end in a clear container with about two inches
of brake fluid, submerged. Have an assistant depress the clutch pedal and hold it. Open the
bleeder valve on the release cylinder, allowing fluid to flow through the hose; close the
valve when fluid stops flowing, then have the assistant release the pedal. Continue until all
air is evacuated — indicated by a full, solid stream ejected each time with no bubbles in the
hose or container. Keep a close watch on the master cylinder reservoir fluid level — if it
drops too low, air will be sucked back in and the process must restart. Install the dust cap
and lower the vehicle — check carefully for proper operation before returning the vehicle to
service.

## 8. Clutch start switch - check and adjustment (PDF p.192-193) <a id="p192"></a>

Check the pedal height, pedal freeplay, and pushrod play (Chapter 1). Verify the engine
won't start when the clutch pedal is released. Verify it will start when the pedal is
depressed all the way. Measure clearance "A" (illustration — distance "A" diagram) — it must
be as specified when the clutch pedal is depressed (see the switch clearance chart below);
adjust or replace the clutch start switch if not. Verify continuity between the switch
terminals when it's On (illustration 8.5 — there should be continuity when pushed in, none
when released; the switch is "On" when distance A is as specified or less — reference figure
printed: (1 mm); if it isn't, adjust the switch). Verify no continuity when the switch is Off.
Replace the switch if it fails either test — remove the nut nearest the plunger end and slide
it off the bracket, disconnect the wire harness; installation is the reverse of removal.

To adjust: measure the pedal stroke (illustration 8.8a — distance between the pedal at rest,
fully up/released, and fully depressed) and calculate switch clearance "A" using the chart
(illustration 8.8b — clearance "A" is inversely proportional to pedal stroke: the longer the
stroke, the smaller clearance "A"; printed reference values 0.079 in / 0.157 in / 0.236 in
correspond to points on the clearance-vs-stroke curve). Change the switch position if
necessary. Verify again that the engine doesn't start when the clutch pedal is released.

## 9. Driveaxles - general information and inspection (PDF p.193) <a id="p193"></a>

Power transmits from the transaxle to the wheels through a pair of driveaxles. Each
driveaxle's inner end connects to the transaxle by a side gear shaft (flanged stub axle)
splined to the differential — these shafts can be driven out to replace oil seals (Chapter
7A). The outer ends are splined to the axle hubs and locked in place by a large nut.

The splined inner ends have sliding tripod joints, capable of both angular (bending) and
axial (in-and-out) motion — each inner joint has a tripod bearing and a joint tulip
(housing) the tripod slides in-and-out of as the driveaxle moves with the wheel. Inner
joints are replaceable (Section 11). Each outer joint has ball bearings running between an
inner race and outer cage, capable of angular but not axial movement — outer joints are
neither rebuildable nor removable; a failed one requires a new driveaxle/outer joint
assembly.

Inspect the boots periodically for damage and leaking lubricant — damaged CV joint boots
must be replaced immediately or the joints can be damaged. Boot replacement involves
driveaxle removal (Section 10). *Note: some auto parts stores carry "split" type replacement
boots installable without removing the driveaxle — convenient, but the driveaxle should
still be removed and the CV joint disassembled/cleaned to ensure it's free of moisture/dirt
contaminants that accelerate wear.* The most common symptom of worn/damaged CV joints —
besides lubricant leaks — is a clicking noise in turns, a clunk when accelerating after
coasting, and vibration at highway speeds.

To check for CV joint/driveaxle shaft wear: grasp each axle (one at a time) and rotate it in
both directions while holding the CV joint housings, feeling for play indicating worn
splines or sloppy CV joints. Also check the shafts for cracks, dents, and distortion.

## 10. Driveaxles - removal and installation (PDF p.193-195) <a id="p193"></a>

Disconnect the cable from the negative battery terminal. Remove the cotter pin and bearing
nut lock from the driveaxle nut (illustration 10.2). Loosen the driveaxle nut (illustration
10.3 — with the wheel on the ground, use a long breaker bar and socket). Loosen, but don't
remove, the front wheel lug nuts. Raise the front and support securely on jackstands. Remove
the wheel. Drain the transaxle lubricant (Chapter 1). If the inner end of the driveaxle is
secured by nuts, have an assistant depress the brake pedal, remove the nuts attaching the
inner CV joint to the differential side gear shaft (illustration 10.8 — some models use six
nuts), and detach the tripod joint from the side gear shaft. Remove the disc brake caliper,
bracket, and disc (Chapter 9). Remove the control arm-to-balljoint bolts/nuts and detach the
arm from the balljoint (Chapter 10). Push the driveaxle out of the hub with a two-jaw puller
(illustration 10.11a) and remove the driveaxle. If the inner end is splined to the
differential (not nut-secured), separate it from the differential — on right-hand
driveaxles, tap gently against the inner joint with a hammer and brass drift punch;
left-hand driveaxles can be pried out with a large screwdriver (illustration 10.11b).
Confirmed against the p.191 page image (not p.194-195 as originally guessed).

*Note (as printed): it's not a good idea to move the vehicle with a driveaxle removed, but if
you must, first install a bolt and pair of washers as shown (illustration 10.12: 1 2-inch
O.D. washer, 2 1-3/4-inch O.D. washer, 3 9/16-inch nut, 4 lock washer, 5 9/16-inch bolt) and
tighten them securely.*

If you must move the vehicle while the driveaxle is out, secure the hub and bearing assembly
as shown (illustration 10.12). *Caution: failure to support the hub and bearing assembly
could damage it if subjected to vehicle weight.* If replacing a differential oil seal,
carefully tap the side gear shaft flange out with a hammer and punch and remove it (Chapter
7A). Installation is the reverse of removal — tighten the driveaxle/hub nut and the
driveaxle-to-differential side gear flange nuts to the Specifications torque.

## 11. Driveaxle boot replacement and CV joint overhaul (PDF p.194-196) <a id="p194"></a>

*Note (as printed): if CV joints must be overhauled (usually due to torn boots), explore all
options before starting — complete rebuilt driveaxles are available on an exchange basis,
saving much time/work. Check parts cost/availability before disassembling the vehicle either
way.*

Remove the driveaxle (Section 10). Make a pair of match marks on the joint tulip and the
driveaxle (illustration 11.2 — draw the marks, don't use a center-punch). On Toyota-type
driveaxles (used on all early models, some later ones), pry the outer (larger) clamps loose
with a small screwdriver (illustration 11.3a) and slide them off the driveaxle ends. On
Saginaw-type driveaxles (some later models), squeeze the clamp ends together with a boot
clamp tool (illustration 11.3b) to release the clamp. Cut the inner (smaller) clamps with
diagonal cutters and discard them. On all except Saginaw-type driveaxles, cut and discard the
damper clamp if the driveaxle has a damper. On Saginaw-type driveaxles, remove the damper
clamp with a screwdriver.

Separate the inner joint tulip from the tripod joint. Remove the tripod joint snap-ring with
snap-ring pliers (illustration 11.6a exploded view; 11.6b). Make match marks on the tripod
and driveaxle (illustration 11.7). Using a hammer and brass punch, drive the tripod joint off
the driveaxle (illustration 11.8 — careful not to damage the bearing surfaces or the shaft
splines). Slide the inner joint boot, damper (if equipped), and outer joint boot off the
driveaxle. Clean both CV joints with solvent and blow dry with compressed air if available.
*Note: since the outer joint can't be disassembled, it's difficult to wash away all the old
grease and rid the bearing of solvent once clean — but it's imperative the job be done
thoroughly, so take your time.*

Inspect the inner tripod joint for wear/damage — replace it and the tulip as an assembly if
obviously worn/damaged. Bend the outer CV joint housing at an angle to the driveaxle to
expose the bearings, inner race, and cage (illustration 11.12) — inspect bearing surfaces for
wear; replace the driveaxle if damaged/worn.

Slide the new outer boot and clamps onto the driveaxle — wrap vinyl tape around the shaft
splines first to prevent boot damage. Once positioned, add the specified amount of grease
(included in the boot replacement kit) to the outer joint and boot (pack the joint with as
much grease as it will hold, put the rest in the boot). Slide the boot the rest of the way
on. If the driveaxle has a damper, install it now with its clamp. Slide the inner boot and
clamps onto the driveaxle. Align the match marks made before removal and, using a brass punch
and hammer, tap the tripod onto the driveaxle. Install the snap-ring. Fill the inner joint
tulip with grease and install it over the tripod joint. Slide the boot into place and
equalize the pressure inside by inserting a small screwdriver between the boot and joint.

Set the driveaxle to the length in this Chapter's Specifications (illustrations 11.15a — on
models with flanged inner CV joints, measure between these points; 11.15b — on models with
splined inner CV joints, measure between the points indicated) and confirm the boot isn't
stretched, contracted, or distorted — equalize the pressure again as described above if
necessary. Tighten the boot clamps: (a) Toyota type — bend the tang down and secure it with
the tabs (illustrations 11.15c, 11.15d); (b) Saginaw type — install the clamp with a boot
clamp tool until the tangs engage the slots (illustration 11.3b). Install the driveaxle
(Section 10).

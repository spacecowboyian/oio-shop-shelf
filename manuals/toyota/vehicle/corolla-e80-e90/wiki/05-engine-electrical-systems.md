## 1. General information (PDF p.141) <a id="p141"></a>

Engine electrical systems cover all ignition, charging, and starting components — discussed
separately from chassis electrical devices (lights, instruments, etc., in Chapter 12).

> *Precautions (as printed):*
> a) *Be extremely careful when servicing engine electrical components — easily damaged if
> checked, connected, or handled improperly.*
> b) *Never leave the ignition switch on for long periods with the engine off.*
> c) *Don't disconnect the battery cables while the engine is running.*
> d) *Maintain correct polarity when connecting a battery cable from another vehicle during
> jump starting.*
> e) *Always disconnect the negative cable first and hook it up last, or the battery may be
> shorted by the tool used to loosen the cable clamps.*

Also review the safety-related information near the front of the manual before beginning
any operation in this Chapter.

## 2. Battery - removal and installation (PDF p.141) <a id="p141"></a>

Disconnect both cables from the battery terminals (illustration 2.1).

> **WARNING** — always disconnect the negative cable first and hook it up last, or the
> battery may be shorted by the tool used to loosen the cable clamps. Connecting the
> positive cable while the negative is already connected can cause a spark, resulting in a
> battery explosion.

Remove the battery hold-down clamp (illustration 2.2 — detach negative and positive cables,
then remove the strap bolt/nut and the strap). Lift out the battery — careful, it's heavy.
While it's out, inspect the carrier (tray) for corrosion (Chapter 1). If replacing the
battery, get an identical one — same dimensions, amperage rating, cold cranking rating,
etc. Installation is the reverse of removal.

## 4. Battery cables - check and replacement (PDF p.142) <a id="p142"></a>

Periodically inspect the entire length of each battery cable for damage, cracked/burned
insulation, and corrosion — poor cable connections cause starting problems and decreased
engine performance. Check the cable-to-terminal connections for cracks, loose wire strands,
and corrosion — white, fluffy deposits under the insulation at the terminal connection mean
the cable is corroded and should be replaced. Check the terminals for distortion, missing
mounting bolts, and corrosion.

> **WARNING** — when removing the cables, always disconnect the negative cable first and
> hook it up last, or the battery may be shorted by the tool used to loosen the cable
> clamps. Even if only the positive cable is being replaced, be sure to disconnect the
> negative cable from the battery first (see Chapter 1 for more on battery cable removal).

Disconnect the old cables from the battery, then trace each to its opposite end and detach
from the starter solenoid and ground terminals — note the routing of each cable for correct
reinstallation. If replacing either or both cables, take them along when buying new ones —
it's vitally important to replace with identical parts. Positive cables are usually red,
larger in cross-section, with a larger-diameter battery post clamp; ground cables are
usually black, smaller in cross-section, with a slightly smaller-diameter clamp for the
negative post.

Clean the threads of the solenoid or ground connection with a wire brush to remove
rust/corrosion; apply a light coat of battery terminal corrosion inhibitor or petroleum
jelly to the threads to prevent future corrosion. Attach the cable to the solenoid or ground
connection and tighten the mounting nut/bolt securely. Before connecting a new cable to the
battery, make sure it reaches the battery post without being stretched. Connect the
positive cable first, followed by the negative cable.

## 5. Ignition system - general information and precautions (PDF p.142-143) <a id="p142"></a>

The ignition system includes the ignition switch, battery, igniter, coil, primary (low
voltage) and secondary (high voltage) wiring circuits, the distributor, and the spark
plugs. On fuel-injected models, the ignition system is controlled by the Electronic Control
Unit (ECU) — using data from information sensors monitoring rpm, intake air volume, engine
temperature, etc., the ECU ensures perfectly timed spark under all conditions, a system
called Electronic Spark Advance (ESA). On carbureted models, spark advance is controlled by
vacuum and centrifugal advance units.

> *Precautions when working on the ignition system (as printed):*
> a) *Don't keep the ignition switch on for more than 10 seconds if the engine won't start.*
> b) *Always connect a tachometer per the manufacturer's instructions — idle speed
> procedures in Chapter 1 show correct tachometer connection methods; some tachometers may
> be incompatible with this ignition system — consult a dealer before buying one.*
> c) *Never allow the ignition coil terminals to touch ground — grounding the coil could
> damage the igniter and/or the coil.*
> d) *Do not disconnect the battery when the engine is running.*
> e) *Make sure the igniter is properly grounded.*

## 6. Ignition system - check (PDF p.143-144) <a id="p143"></a>

> **WARNING** — because of the high voltage generated by the ignition system, extreme care
> should be taken whenever an operation involves ignition components — including the
> igniter, coil, distributor, spark plug wires, plug connectors, tachometer, and other test
> equipment.

If the engine turns over but won't start, disconnect a spark plug wire from any plug and
attach it to a calibrated ignition tester (available at most auto parts stores). Connect the
tester's clip to a bolt or metal bracket on the engine (illustration 6.1). Without a
calibrated tester, remove a plug wire and, using an insulated tool, pull back the boot and
hold the wire end about 1/4-inch from a good ground. Crank the engine and watch for bright
blue, well-defined sparks (have an assistant crank if not using a calibrated tester).

If sparks occur, sufficient voltage is reaching the plug (repeat the check at the remaining
plug wires to verify the distributor cap and rotor are OK) — but the plugs themselves may be
fouled; remove and check them (Chapter 1). If no sparks or intermittent sparks occur, remove
the distributor cap and check the cap/rotor per Chapter 1 — if moisture is present, dry out
the cap/rotor, reinstall, and repeat the spark test. If still no spark, detach the coil
secondary wire from the distributor cap and hook it to the tester (reattach the plug wire to
the plug), repeat the check — or hold the wire end about 1/4-inch from a good ground without
a tester.

If sparks now occur, the distributor cap, rotor, or plug wire(s) may be defective. If no
sparks occur, check the primary wire connections at the coil for clean/tight fit; check for
voltage to the coil; check the coil (Section 7); make any necessary repairs and repeat the
check. If there's still no spark, the coil-to-cap wire may be bad (check resistance with an
ohmmeter, compare to Specifications) — if a known-good wire doesn't change the result, the
igniter may be defective.

## 7. Ignition coil - check and replacement (PDF p.143-145) <a id="p143"></a>

**Check:** detach the cable from the negative battery terminal.

**Distributor-mounted coil** (illustrations 7.3a, 7.3b): remove the distributor cap (Chapter
1). Using an ohmmeter: (a) measure resistance between the positive and negative terminals,
compare to the coil primary resistance Specifications; (b) measure resistance between the
positive terminal and the high-tension terminal, compare to the coil secondary resistance
Specifications. Replace the coil if either reading is outside spec.

**Remote-mounted coil** (illustrations 7.6a-7.6d): locate the coil in the engine
compartment. Using an ohmmeter: (a) measure resistance between the positive and negative
terminals, compare to primary resistance Specifications; (b) detach the high-tension lead
from the coil, measure resistance between the positive and high-tension terminals, compare
to secondary resistance Specifications. Replace the coil if either reading is outside spec.

**Replacement — distributor-mounted coil** (illustrations 7.11, 7.12): detach the cable from
the negative battery terminal. Remove the distributor (Section 8). Remove the coil dust
cover. Label and disconnect the electrical wires from the coil terminals (illustration
7.11). Remove the four coil mounting bolts and separate it from the distributor.
Installation is the reverse of removal.

**Replacement — remote-mounted coil:** label and remove the coil terminal connector or nuts
and wires. On 1987 models, unplug the connector to the igniter. Remove the coil bracket
bolts. On 1987 models, separate the coil and igniter, then attach the new coil to the old
igniter. Installation is the reverse of removal.

## 8. Distributor - removal and installation (PDF p.145-146) <a id="p145"></a>

**Removal** (illustrations 8.4, 8.6a, 8.6b): detach the cable from the negative battery
terminal. Unplug the electrical connectors from the distributor. If equipped, detach the
vacuum hose(s) from the advance unit. Look for a raised "1" on the distributor cap
(illustration 8.4) marking the No. 1 cylinder plug wire terminal — if unmarked, trace the
No. 1 spark plug wire back to its cap terminal. Remove the distributor cap (Chapter 1) and
turn the engine over until the rotor points toward the No. 1 spark plug terminal (see the
TDC-locating procedure in `02a-engines.md` Section 3). Make a mark on the distributor base
edge directly below and in line with the rotor tip; also mark the distributor base and
engine block to ensure correct reinstallation (illustrations 8.6a, 8.6b). Remove the
distributor hold-down bolt(s), then pull the distributor straight out.

> *Caution: DO NOT turn the crankshaft while the distributor is out of the engine, or the
> alignment marks will be useless.*

**Installation** (illustrations 8.9a, 8.9b, 8.10): *Note: if the crankshaft has been moved
while the distributor is out, locate TDC for the No. 1 piston (Chapter 2A) and position the
distributor/rotor accordingly.* Insert the distributor into the engine in exactly the same
relationship to the block as when removed. On gear-driven distributor models, align the
dimples on the driven gear and distributor housing (illustrations 8.9a — 4A-C, 8.9b —
4A-GE) — meshing the helical gears on the camshaft and distributor may require slightly
turning the rotor. On lug-driven distributor models, align the distributor match marks
(illustration 8.10 — 4A-F/4A-FE: the mark on the lug and the protrusion on the distributor
housing must align). If the distributor doesn't seat completely, recheck the alignment
marks between the distributor base and block to verify it's in the same position as before
removal; also check that the rotor aligns with the mark made on the distributor base edge.
Loosely install the distributor hold-down bolt(s). Installation is otherwise the reverse of
removal. Check the ignition timing (Section 9) and tighten the hold-down bolt securely.

## 9. Ignition timing - check and adjustment (PDF p.146-147) <a id="p146"></a>

*Note (as printed): the following procedure should apply to all vehicles covered by this
manual — but if the VECI label on your vehicle specifies a different procedure, use that
one instead.*

Warm up the engine to normal operating temperature. Hook up a timing light per the
manufacturer's instructions.

**4A-C (SOHC) engine** (illustration 9.4): detach the vacuum hose(s) from the vacuum advance
diaphragm on the distributor (both hoses on 1984-1985 models; just the advance hose on
1986-1987 models) and plug the disconnected hose(s). Locate the timing marks on the timing
cover and crankshaft pulley. Using the timing light, check ignition timing and compare to
the VECI label spec. If necessary, loosen the distributor hold-down bolts and turn the
distributor slightly to align the marks. Recheck to confirm the distributor didn't move when
the bolts were tightened. Reattach the vacuum hose(s). On 1986-1987 models, recheck timing
with the vacuum advance hose connected — should be 13° BTDC; repeat the procedure if it
deviates from spec.

**1987 4A-GE (DOHC) engine** (illustrations 9.11, 9.13): locate the check engine connector
near the windshield wiper motor; bridge the terminals with a jumper wire. Set the engine
idle speed (Chapter 1). Using a timing light, set the timing to the VECI label value with
the engine idling and the transmission in Neutral — if necessary, loosen the distributor
hold-down bolt and turn until the marks align, recheck timing after tightening. Remove the
jumper wire. Recheck timing with the engine idling, transmission in Neutral — automatic-
transmission models should show more than 16° BTDC, manual-transmission models more than
12° BTDC; repeat the procedure if necessary.

**1988 and later 4A-GE (DOHC) engine:** set the engine idle speed (Chapter 1). With the
ignition switch off, connect a timing light per manufacturer instructions. Locate the
diagnostic connector and insert a jumper wire between E1 and T terminals (illustration
9.24). Locate the timing marks on the timing cover and crankshaft pulley. Start the engine
and aim the timing light at the mark — it should be 10° BTDC; if necessary, loosen the
distributor and slowly align the marks, tighten and recheck. Remove the jumper wire and
recheck — should be 16° BTDC (1988-1990) or 9 to 19° BTDC (1991). Turn the engine off,
remove the jumper wire and timing light.

**1988 and 1989 4A-F (DOHC) engine** (illustration 9.16): locate the tachometer service
connector at the distributor and connect a tachometer. Set the engine idle speed (Chapter
1). Disconnect and plug both distributor vacuum hoses. Using a timing light, set the timing
to the VECI label value with the engine idling and transmission in Neutral — if necessary,
loosen the distributor hold-down bolt and turn until aligned, recheck after tightening.
Remove the jumper wire from the connector. Recheck timing with the engine idling,
transmission in Neutral — should vary between 10 and 16° BTDC; repeat if necessary.

**1989 and later 4A-FE (DOHC) engine** (illustrations 9.24, 9.27): set the engine idle speed
(Chapter 1). With the ignition switch off, connect a timing light. Locate the diagnostic
connector, insert a jumper wire between E1 and T terminals (illustration 9.24: 1 E1
terminal, 2 T terminal, 3 jumper wire, 4 diagnostic connector). Locate the timing marks on
the timing cover and crankshaft pulley. Start the engine and aim the timing light at the
mark; if necessary, loosen the distributor and slowly turn until the marks indicate the VECI
label value, tighten the hold-down bolt and recheck. Remove the jumper wire from the
diagnostic connector — on 1989 models only, also unplug the Air Control Valve (ACV)
connector (illustration 9.27). Recheck the timing: (a) 1989 — 10° BTDC; (b) 1990 — 5 to 15°
BTDC; (c) 1991 — 0 to 20° BTDC. Repeat the procedure if necessary. Turn the engine off,
reconnect the ACV connector (1989 models), remove the timing light.

## 10. Vacuum advance unit - check and replacement (PDF p.147-148) <a id="p147"></a>

**Check** (illustration 10.4): detach the negative battery cable. Remove the distributor cap
(Chapter 1). Disconnect the vacuum hoses from the advance unit and, using a T-fitting and
two hoses, connect a vacuum pump to both diaphragms. Apply vacuum and verify the advancer
moves. Replace the advancer if it doesn't work.

**Replacement** (illustration 10.10): remove the distributor (Section 8). Carefully clamp the
distributor in a bench vise with padded jaws — don't overtighten, the housing is an aluminum
casting that will crack if squeezed too hard. Pull the rotor off the shaft (Chapter 1).
Remove the vacuum unit mounting screws. Remove the controller link from the pin on the
breaker plate, then detach the vacuum advance unit.

## 12. Centrifugal advance mechanism - check and component replacement (PDF p.148-149) <a id="p148"></a>

Detach the cable from the negative battery terminal. Remove the distributor cap (Chapter
1).

**Check** (illustration 12.3): turn the rotor clockwise, release it, and verify it returns
slightly counterclockwise. Verify the rotor isn't excessively loose. Repair or replace as
necessary if it doesn't work as described.

**Replacement** (illustrations 12.9, 12.11, 12.13a, 12.13b, 12.14, 12.16): remove the
distributor (Section 8). Remove the coil (Section 7). Remove the igniter (Section 11).
Remove the signal rotor — carefully pry off with a screwdriver (illustration 12.9; it's
retained by a spring clip — be careful not to lose it). Remove the vacuum advance unit
(Section 10). Remove the breaker plate assembly (illustration 12.11 — two retaining screws).
Mark the governor springs for correct reinstallation; using needle-nose pliers, remove the
two governor springs. Mark the governor shaft and stopper for correct reinstallation
(illustration 12.13a); remove the grease stopper and the screw at the end of the governor
shaft, then remove the governor shaft. Using a small screwdriver, remove the clips and pull
out the two governor weights (illustration 12.14). Apply a very small amount of lightweight
grease to the pivot pins, then install the governor weights. Inspect the breaker plate
(illustration 12.16 — turn it and confirm a slight drag; if strong resistance or binding is
felt, replace the breaker plate and pick-up coil assembly). Reassembly is the reverse of
removal.

## 14. Charging system - general information and precautions (PDF p.150) <a id="p150"></a>

The charging system includes the alternator, an internal voltage regulator, a charge
indicator, the battery, a fusible link, and the connecting wiring — it supplies power for
the ignition system, lights, radio, etc. The alternator is driven by a drivebelt at the
front (right end) of the engine. The voltage regulator limits alternator voltage to a preset
value, preventing power surges/circuit overloads during peak output. The fusible link is a
short insulated wire, several gauges smaller than the circuit it protects, integral with the
engine compartment wiring harness — identified by flag color (Chapter 12 has more on
fusible links).

The charging system doesn't ordinarily need periodic maintenance, but the drivebelt,
battery, and wires/connections should be inspected at the Chapter 1 intervals. The dashboard
warning light should come on when the key is turned to Start, then go off immediately — if
it stays on, there's a charging system malfunction (Section 15). Some vehicles also have a
voltmeter — abnormally high/low readings also indicate a check is needed (Section 15).

> *Precautions when working on an alternator-equipped vehicle (as printed):*
> a) *When reconnecting wires to the alternator from the battery, note the polarity.*
> b) *Before arc welding any part of the vehicle, disconnect the wires from the alternator
> and battery terminals.*
> c) *Never start the engine with a battery charger connected.*
> d) *Always disconnect both battery leads before using a battery charger.*
> e) *The alternator is driven by an engine drivebelt which could cause serious injury if
> your hand, hair, or clothes become entangled in it with the engine running.*
> f) *Because the alternator is connected directly to the battery, it could arc or cause a
> fire if overloaded or shorted out.*
> g) *Wrap a plastic bag over the alternator and secure it with rubber bands before steam
> cleaning the engine.*

## 15. Charging system - check (PDF p.150-152) <a id="p150"></a>

If a charging circuit malfunction occurs, don't automatically assume the alternator is at
fault — first check: (a) drivebelt tension/condition, replace if worn/deteriorated; (b)
alternator mounting/adjustment bolts tight; (c) alternator wiring harness and connectors at
the alternator and voltage regulator, in good condition and tight; (d) the fusible link
(if equipped) between the starter solenoid and alternator — if burned, determine the cause,
repair the circuit, and replace the link (the vehicle won't start and/or accessories won't
work if the link is blown); (e) start the engine and check the alternator for abnormal
noises (a shrieking/squealing sound indicates a bad bushing); (f) check the battery
electrolyte specific gravity — charge the battery if low (doesn't apply to maintenance-free
batteries); (g) confirm the battery is fully charged (one bad cell can cause alternator
overcharging); (h) disconnect the battery cables (negative first, then positive), inspect
the posts and cable clamps for corrosion — clean thoroughly if necessary (Section 4, Chapter
1), reconnect the cable to the positive terminal first; with the key off, insert a test
light between the negative battery post and the disconnected negative cable clamp — if the
light doesn't come on, reattach the clamp and proceed; if it comes on, there's a short in
the vehicle's electrical system that must be repaired before the charging system can be
checked — disconnect the alternator wiring harness: if the light goes out, the alternator is
bad; if it stays on, pull each fuse until the light goes out (identifying the shorted
circuit).

Using a voltmeter, check battery voltage with the engine off — should be approximately 12
volts. Start the engine and check again — should now be approximately 13.5 to 15.1 volts.
Turn on the headlights — voltage should drop, then come back up, if the charging system is
working properly. If the reading is greater than the specified charging voltage, replace the
voltage regulator (Section 17). If less than standard, check the regulator and alternator:
ground terminal F, start the engine, check the voltage at terminal B (illustration 15.7) and
compare to standard voltage — (a) if greater than standard, replace the regulator; (b) if
less than standard, check the alternator or have it checked by a dealer/service station.

If you have an ammeter, hook it into the system as shown (illustration 15.8a) — or use an
inductive-type current indicator (illustration 15.8b), inexpensive and accurate enough for a
quick check; simply place it on the alternator output lead. With the engine running at 2,000
rpm, turn on the high beams, set the heater blower to HI, check the ammeter reading and
compare to standard amperage — repair or replace the alternator if the reading is less than
standard.

## 18. Starting system - general information and precautions (PDF p.153) <a id="p153"></a>

The starting system's sole function is to turn the engine over quickly enough to start —
consists of the battery, starter motor, starter solenoid (mounted directly on the starter
motor), and connecting wires. The solenoid/starter assembly is installed on the lower part
of the engine, next to the transaxle bellhousing. When the key is turned to Start, the
starter solenoid is actuated through the starter control circuit, connecting the battery to
the starter — the battery supplies electrical energy, and the starter motor does the actual
work of cranking. On a 1986-and-later vehicle with a manual transaxle, the starter can only
operate when the clutch pedal is depressed; with an automatic transaxle, only when the
selector lever is in Park or Neutral.

> *Precautions when working on the starting system (as printed):*
> a) *Excessive cranking can overheat the starter motor and cause serious damage — never
> operate it for more than 15 seconds at a time without pausing at least two minutes to
> cool.*
> b) *The starter is connected directly to the battery and could arc or cause a fire if
> mishandled, overloaded, or shorted out.*
> c) *Always detach the cable from the negative battery terminal before working on the
> starting system.*

## 19. Starter motor - testing in vehicle (PDF p.153-154) <a id="p153"></a>

*Note (as printed): before diagnosing starter problems, make sure the battery is fully
charged.*

If the starter doesn't turn at all when the switch is operated, confirm the shift lever is
in Neutral or Park (automatic) or the clutch pedal is depressed (manual). Confirm the
battery is charged and all cables at the battery and starter solenoid terminals are clean
and secure. If the starter spins but the engine isn't cranking, the overrunning clutch in
the starter is slipping and the starter must be replaced. If the starter doesn't operate at
all when the switch is actuated but the solenoid clicks, the problem is the battery, the
main solenoid contacts, or the starter motor itself (or the engine is seized). If the
solenoid plunger can't be heard when the switch is actuated, the battery is bad, the fusible
link is burned (open circuit), or the solenoid itself is defective.

To check the solenoid, connect a jumper lead between the battery (+) and the ignition switch
terminal (the small terminal) on the solenoid — if the starter now operates, the solenoid is
OK and the problem is in the ignition switch, Neutral start switch, or wiring. If the
starter still doesn't operate, remove the starter/solenoid assembly for disassembly, testing,
and repair.

If the starter cranks the engine at an abnormally slow speed, first confirm the battery is
charged and all terminal connections are tight — a partially seized engine or wrong-
viscosity oil will also crank slowly. Run the engine to normal operating temperature, then
shut it off. On vehicles with remote-mounted coils, disconnect the coil wire from the
distributor cap and ground it on the engine; on vehicles with integral coil/distributor
caps, disconnect the primary electrical connector from the distributor. Connect a voltmeter
positive lead to the battery positive post and the negative lead to the negative post. Crank
the engine and take the voltmeter reading as soon as it's steady — don't crank for more than
15 seconds at a time. A reading of nine volts or more, with the starter turning at normal
cranking speed, is normal. Nine volts or more but slow cranking means a faulty motor. Less
than nine volts and slow cranking means the solenoid contacts are probably burned, the
starter is bad, the battery is discharged, or there's a bad connection.

## 2. Electrical troubleshooting - general information (PDF p.246-247) <a id="p246"></a>

A typical circuit consists of an electrical component, any switches/relays/motors/fuses/
fusible links/circuit breakers related to it, and the wiring/connectors linking it to the
battery and chassis. Wiring diagrams are included at the end of this Chapter (Section 20) to
help pinpoint circuit problems — study the appropriate diagram first to understand what
makes up the circuit; narrow down trouble spots by noting whether other components on the
same circuit are operating (several simultaneous failures often point to a shared fuse or
ground connection).

Electrical problems usually stem from simple causes: loose/corroded connections, a blown
fuse, a melted fusible link, or a bad relay. Visually inspect all fuses, wires, and
connections in a problem circuit before troubleshooting further. If using test instruments,
use the diagrams to plan connection points ahead of time.

Basic troubleshooting tools: a circuit tester or voltmeter (a 12-volt bulb with test leads
also works), a continuity tester (bulb, battery, and test leads), and a jumper wire
(preferably with a built-in circuit breaker) to bypass components.

**Voltage checks:** connect one circuit-tester lead to the negative battery terminal or a
known good ground; connect the other to a connector in the circuit, preferably nearest the
battery/fuse. If the tester bulb lights, voltage is present and that part of the circuit is
problem-free — continue checking further along; where voltage stops being present, the
problem lies between that point and the last point with voltage.

**Continuity check:** determines if there are any breaks in a circuit, with the circuit off
(no power). Connect a self-powered continuity tester's leads to both circuit ends (or the
"power" end and a good ground) — the test light comes on if current passes properly; if not,
there's a break somewhere. The same procedure tests a switch, by connecting to the power-in
and power-out sides — the test light should come on with the switch On.

**Finding a short:** remove the fuse and connect a test light or voltmeter in its place to
the fuse terminals — there should be no voltage present. Move the wiring harness side to
side while watching the test light — if it lights, there's a short to ground somewhere in
that area, probably where insulation has rubbed through. The same test works on each
component in the circuit, even a switch.

**Ground check:** disconnect the battery, connect one lead of a self-powered continuity
tester to a known good ground, and the other to the wire or ground connection being tested
— the bulb lighting indicates a good ground; not lighting indicates a bad ground.

**Finding an open circuit:** often difficult to locate by sight since oxidation or terminal
misalignment is hidden by the connectors — merely wiggling a connector on a sensor or in the
harness may correct an open-circuit condition; keep this in mind when an open circuit is
indicated during troubleshooting. Intermittent problems may also be caused by oxidized or
loose connections.

*Note (as printed): keep in mind that some circuits receive voltage only when the ignition
key is in the Accessory or Run position.* Electrical troubleshooting is simple if you keep
in mind that all circuits are basically electricity running from the battery, through wires/
switches/relays/fuses/fusible links, to each electrical component, and to ground, from which
it's passed back to the battery — any electrical problem is an interruption in that flow.

## 3. Fuses - general information (PDF p.247-248) <a id="p247"></a>

Vehicle circuits are protected by a combination of fuses, circuit breakers, and fusible
links. Fuse blocks are located under the instrument panel on the left and right sides of the
dashboard (behind the driver's and passenger's kick panels) and next to the battery in the
engine compartment (illustration 3.1a — the fuse block in the left kick panel). Each fuse
protects a specific circuit, identified on the fuse panel itself. Miniaturized blade-terminal
fuses allow fingertip removal/replacement. If an electrical component fails, always check the
fuse first — a blown fuse is easily identified through the clear plastic body; visually
inspect the element for damage (illustration 3.1b). If a continuity check is called for, the
blade terminal tips are exposed in the fuse body.

Always replace blown fuses with the correct type — fuses of different ratings are physically
interchangeable, but only the proper rating should be used (replacing with a higher/lower
value than specified is not recommended). Each circuit needs a specific amount of
protection; the amperage value is molded into the fuse body. If a replacement fuse
immediately fails, don't replace it again until the cause is isolated and corrected — in most
cases this is a short circuit from a broken or deteriorated wire.

## 4. Fusible links - general information (PDF p.248) <a id="p248"></a>

Some circuits are protected by fusible links, used in circuits not ordinarily fused (such as
the ignition circuit). Fusible links on these models are similar to fuses in that they can be
visually checked for melting (illustration 4.2). To replace: disconnect the negative battery
cable, unplug the burned-out link, replace it with a new one (available from your dealer).
Always determine the cause of the overload that melted the link before installing a new one.

## 5. Circuit breakers - general information (PDF p.248) <a id="p248"></a>

Circuit breakers protect components such as power windows, power door locks, and headlights
— some are located in the fuse box. On some models, the circuit breaker resets itself
automatically — an overload causes the circuit to fail momentarily, then come back on; if it
doesn't come back on, check it immediately. Some circuit breakers must be reset manually —
once the underlying condition is corrected, the breaker resumes normal function. To reset a
manual circuit breaker: disconnect the cable from the negative battery terminal, remove the
circuit breaker, insert a pin into the reset hole and push in (illustration 5.3), reinstall
the circuit breaker.

## 6. Relays - general information (PDF p.248-249) <a id="p248"></a>

Several electrical accessories use relays to transmit the electrical signal to the
component — a defective relay means that component won't operate properly. Relays are
located in several locations throughout the vehicle (illustrations 6.2a — seat belt warning
relay location; 6.2b — typical engine compartment relay locations, 1984-1987 models, labeled:
defogger relay, door lock control relay, running light relay, CB [circuit breaker] defogger
30A, starter relay, heater main relay, panel relay, CB [circuit breaker] heater 30A, fuse A/C
7.5A, cruise control ECU or theft deterrent ECU, taillight control relay; 6.2c — typical
passenger compartment relay locations, 1988 and later models, labeled: integration relay
(seat belt warning system), power window main relay). If a faulty relay is suspected, it can
be removed and tested by a dealer or other qualified shop — defective relays must be replaced
as a unit.

## 7. Turn signal/hazard flashers - check and replacement (PDF p.249) <a id="p249"></a>

The turn signal/hazard flasher — a small canister-shaped unit behind the left kick panel
(1984-1987 models) or under the dash (1988 and later models, see illustrations 6.2a, 6.2c) —
flashes the turn signals. A functioning unit clicks audibly during operation. If turn signals
fail on one side and the flasher doesn't make its characteristic click, a faulty turn signal
bulb is indicated. If both turn signals fail to blink, the problem may be a blown fuse, a
faulty flasher unit, a broken switch, or a loose/open connection — if the fuse box shows the
turn signal fuse has blown, check the wiring for a short before installing a new fuse. To
replace the flasher, simply pull it out of the fuse block or wiring harness. Make sure the
replacement is identical to the original — compare old to new before installing. Installation
is the reverse of removal.

## 8. Steering column switches - removal and installation (PDF p.249-251) <a id="p249"></a>

**Combination switch** (illustrations 8.4, 8.5): disconnect the negative cable at the
battery. Remove the steering wheel (Chapter 10). Remove the lower finish panel and steering
column cover. Remove the combination switch retaining screws. Trace the wiring harness down
the steering column to the connector — release the wiring retainer clamp (if equipped),
unplug the connector, slide the switch up off the column. Installation is the reverse of
removal.

**Turn signal/headlight control switch** (illustration 8.8): remove the combination switch
(above). Remove the retaining screws and lift the switch off. Disconnect the switch wiring
terminals from the combination switch electrical connector (Steps 18-22, Electrical
connectors, below). Installation is the reverse of removal.

**Wiper/washer/cruise control switch** (illustration 8.11): remove the retaining screws,
detach the switch, lift it off the combination switch. Disconnect the switch wiring
terminals from the combination switch electrical connector (Steps 18-22 below). Installation
is the reverse of removal.

**Hazard warning flasher switch:** remove the turn signal/headlight control switch. Remove
the retaining screws, detach the switch, lift it off the combination switch (illustration
8.8 reference). Disconnect the switch wiring terminals from the combination switch electrical
connector (Steps 18-22 below). Installation is the reverse of removal.

**Electrical connectors** (illustrations 8.18, 8.19, 8.20a, 8.20b, 8.21): trace the wiring
harness from the switch being disconnected to the combination switch connector — may need to
detach or cut wiring ties/straps to separate a particular switch's wires from the harness

wires for the specific switch are disconnected. Use a small screwdriver or punch to pry the
combination switch cover up for terminal access (illustration 8.19). Insert a small
screwdriver or punch into the connector and pry the locking lug down (illustrations 8.20a,
8.20b), disconnect the terminal, pull it out to remove it. To connect a terminal: insert it
into the connector and push in with a small screwdriver until the locking lug snaps into
place (illustration 8.21). Snap the connector cover shut to lock the terminals.

## 12. Headlights - replacement (PDF p.253) <a id="p253"></a>

On 1988 and later coupes, raise the headlights. Disconnect the negative battery cable.

> **WARNING** — on 1988 and later coupes, this is important to prevent the headlights from
> accidentally closing and causing injury.

**Sealed-beam type** (illustration 12.5): on all except 1988 and later coupes, remove the
park and front sidemarker lens and remove the sidemarker assembly; remove the radiator
grille (Chapter 11). On all models, remove the headlight trim, then the headlight retainer
screws — don't disturb the adjustment screws. Pull the headlight out, unplug the connector,
remove the headlight assembly. Installation is the reverse of removal — look for a "this
side up" indicator on the bulb face and position it upward.

**Bulb type** (illustrations 12.8, 12.9, 12.10):

> **WARNING** — the halogen gas-filled bulbs on these models are under pressure and may
> shatter if the surface is scratched or the bulb is dropped. Wear eye protection and handle
> the bulbs carefully, grasping only the base whenever possible. Do not touch the bulb
> surface with your fingers — skin oil could cause it to overheat and fail prematurely. If
> you do touch the surface, clean it with rubbing alcohol.

Reach behind the headlight assembly and unplug the electrical connector (illustration 12.8 —
press down on the connector retaining tab, then gently pull the connector off). Turn the
bulb retaining ring counterclockwise (illustration 12.9) and remove the bulb assembly from
the housing (illustration 12.10 — to remove the bulb housing, press down and turn
counterclockwise). Insert the new bulb into the holder and turn it clockwise to seat it.
Install the bulb holder in the headlight assembly. Installation is the reverse of removal.

## 13. Headlights - adjustment (PDF p.253-254) <a id="p253"></a>

*Note (as printed): it is important that headlights be aimed correctly — if adjusted
incorrectly they could blind an oncoming driver and cause a serious accident, or seriously
reduce your ability to see the road. Headlights should be checked for proper aim every 12
months and any time a new headlight is installed or front-end body work is performed. The
following procedure is only an interim step providing temporary adjustment until the
headlights can be adjusted by a properly equipped shop.*

Headlights have two adjusting screws — one on top (up/down movement), one on the side
(left/right movement). The simplest adjustment method requires a blank wall 25 feet in front
of the vehicle and a level floor (it may be easier to position tape on the wall with the
vehicle parked only a few inches away). Position masking tape vertically in reference to the
vehicle centerline and both headlight centerlines. Position a horizontal tape line in
reference to the centerline of all headlights.

Adjustment should be made with the vehicle sitting level, the gas tank half-full, and no
unusually heavy load. Starting with the low beam: position the high intensity zone two inches
below the horizontal line and two inches to the right of the headlight vertical line —
sealed-beam models adjust via the vertical adjustment screw (illustration 13.6); the
horizontal adjustment screw moves the beam left/right the same way. On bulb-type headlights,
the adjusting screws move the headlight assembly to aim the beam.

With the high beams on, the high intensity zone should be vertically centered with the exact
center just below the horizontal line. *Note: it may not be possible to position the aim
exactly for both high and low beams — if a compromise must be made, keep in mind that low
beams are the most used and have the greatest effect on driver safety.* Have the headlights
adjusted by a dealer service department or other repair shop.

## 17. Cruise control system - description and check (PDF p.257) <a id="p257"></a>

The cruise control system maintains vehicle speed via a vacuum-actuated servo motor in the
engine compartment, connected to the throttle linkage by a cable. The system consists of the
servo motor, clutch switch (manual transmission models), stoplight switch, control switches,
a relay, and associated vacuum hoses.

*Note (as printed): given the complexity of the cruise control system and the special tools/
techniques required for diagnosis and repair, this should be left to a dealer or properly
equipped shop.* The home mechanic can, however, make simple checks for minor,
easily-repaired faults: (a) inspecting the cruise control actuating switches and wiring for
broken wires or loose connections; (b) checking the cruise control fuse; (c) checking the
hoses in the engine compartment for tight connections, cracked hoses, and obvious vacuum
leaks — *the system is vacuum-operated, so it's critical that all vacuum switches, hoses, and
connections be [tight and in good condition]*.

## 18. Power door lock system - description and check (PDF p.258) <a id="p258"></a>

The power door lock system operates the door lock actuators mounted in each door — the
system consists of the switches, actuators, and associated wiring. *Note (as printed):
special tools/techniques are required to fully diagnose this system, and this should be left
to a dealer or properly equipped shop.* The home mechanic can make simple checks for minor,
easily-repaired faults: (a) checking the system fuse and/or circuit breaker; (b) checking the
switch wiring for damage or loose connections; (c) checking the switches for continuity; (d)
removing the door panel(s) and checking the actuator wiring connections for looseness or
damage — inspect the actuator rods (if equipped) for bending, damage, or binding; the
actuator can be checked by applying battery power momentarily — a solid click indicates the
solenoid is operating properly.

## 19. Power window system - description and check (PDF p.258) <a id="p258"></a>

The power window system operates the electric motors mounted in the doors that lower and
raise the windows — consists of the control switches, motors (regulators), glass mechanisms,
and associated wiring. *Note (as printed): given the complexity of the power window system
and the special tools/techniques required, this should be left to a dealer or properly
equipped shop.* The home mechanic can make simple checks for minor, easily-repaired faults:
(a) inspecting the power window actuating switches and wiring for broken wires or loose
connections; (b) checking the power window fuse and/or circuit breaker; (c) removing the
door panel(s) and checking the power window motor wiring connections for looseness/damage,
and inspecting the glass mechanisms for damage that could cause binding.

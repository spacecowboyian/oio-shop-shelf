# Engines (Part A)

In-vehicle repair procedures for the 4A-C (SOHC), 4A-GE, 4A-F, and 4A-FE (DOHC) engines.
Engine removal/installation and block/cylinder-head overhaul are in Part B
(`02b-general-engine-overhaul-procedures.md`). "DOHC" in the source text refers
collectively to the 4A-GE, 4A-F, and 4A-FE engines. *Note (as printed): Specifications for
the 4A-F also apply to the 4A-FE (the FE is the fuel-injected version of the 4A-F).*

## 1. General information (PDF p.56-58) <a id="p56"></a>

Three different four-cylinder engines were installed in the Corolla across the years this
manual covers: the SOHC, two-valve-per-cylinder **4A-C**; and two DOHC, four-valve-per-
cylinder engines — one with dual camshaft timing belt sprockets (**4A-GE**), and one with a
single camshaft timing belt sprocket, called **4A-F** with a carburetor or **4A-FE** with
fuel injection. "DOHC" in the text means the procedure applies to 4A-GE, 4A-F, and 4A-FE
alike.

## 2. Repair operations possible with the engine in the vehicle (PDF p.58) <a id="p58"></a>

Many major repairs don't require engine removal. Degrease the engine compartment/exterior
before starting. Remove the hood if it helps access (Chapter 11); cover the fenders (special
pads, or an old bedspread/blanket). Repairable in-vehicle: intake/exhaust manifold gaskets,
oil pan gasket, crankshaft oil seals, cylinder head gasket (camshaft/valve servicing is
possible since the head can be removed without pulling the engine). Exterior components
removable for repair in place: intake/exhaust manifolds, oil pan, oil pump, water pump,
starter motor, alternator, distributor, fuel system components. Timing belt/sprocket
replacement is also possible in-vehicle. In extreme cases (lacking equipment), piston
ring/piston/connecting rod/rod bearing repair is *possible* in-vehicle but **not
recommended** given the cleaning/prep work involved.

## 3. Top Dead Center (TDC) for number one piston - locating (PDF p.58-60) <a id="p58"></a>

*Note (as printed): this procedure assumes the distributor is correctly installed — if
locating TDC to install the distributor, determine piston position by feeling for
compression at the No. 1 plug hole, then align the ignition timing marks per Step 8.*

TDC is the highest point each piston reaches; generally refers to the compression stroke.
Positioning at TDC is essential for camshaft/timing belt/sprocket removal and distributor
removal. Before starting: transaxle in Neutral, parking brake applied (or rear wheels
blocked); disable the ignition by detaching the coil wire from the distributor cap center
terminal and grounding it on the block (separate-coil vehicles), or disconnecting the
ignition primary wiring connector (integrated coil/distributor vehicles); remove the spark
plugs (Chapter 1).

To turn the crankshaft (viewed from the drivebelt end, normal rotation is clockwise): (a)
preferred — socket/ratchet on the bolt threaded into the crankshaft front; (b) a remote
starter switch, then finish with (a) once close to TDC; (c) an assistant bumping the
ignition switch to Start in short bursts (assistant must stay clear of the vehicle/ignition
switch), then finish with (a).

Detach the distributor cap and set aside (Chapter 1 if needed). Note the position of the
No. 1 spark plug wire terminal on the cap (follow the plug wire from the No. 1 cylinder if
unmarked). Mark the distributor body directly under that terminal's location with a
felt-tip pen or chalk. *Note: outside-cap wire positions don't correspond to inside
terminals — use the inside terminal position for rotor alignment.* Turn the crankshaft
until the crankshaft pulley notch aligns with the 0 on the timing plate (illustration 3.8).
The rotor should point directly at the mark made on the distributor body. If it's 180°
off, the No. 1 piston is at TDC on the **exhaust** stroke — turn one more complete
revolution (360°); the rotor should now point at the mark, and with the ignition timing
marks aligned, No. 1 is at TDC on the **compression** stroke. *Note: if the timing marks
can't be aligned while the rotor points at the distributor mark, the distributor may be
installed wrong, or the timing belt may have jumped/been installed incorrectly. On 4A-GE
engines, TDC can be verified by removing the oil filler cap (illustration 3.11) and
checking that a small dimple in the camshaft aligns with the straight edge at the base of
the filler neck.*

To locate TDC for the remaining pistons: turn the crankshaft following the firing order,
marking/numbering each remaining spark plug wire terminal location on the distributor body
to match cylinder numbers — as the crankshaft turns, the rotor turns with it; when it
points directly at a numbered mark, that cylinder's piston is at TDC on the compression
stroke.

## 4. Valve cover(s) - removal and installation (PDF p.59-61) <a id="p59"></a>

Disconnect the negative battery cable. On 4A-C/4A-F, remove the air cleaner assembly.
Detach the PCV valve and breather hose from the valve cover (illustration 4.3). On DOHC
engines, remove the spark plug wires from the plugs. Label/detach/move aside any wiring
harness in the way. On 4A-C engines, remove the timing belt upper cover and unclip the
throttle cable from it. On 4A-GE engines, remove the throttle cable (Chapter 4) and the
center cover/gasket (illustration 4.7). Remove the mounting nuts/bolts/sealing washers and
detach the cover(s)/gasket(s) — if stuck, bump the end with a block of wood and hammer, or
slip a flexible putty knife between head and cover to break the seal. *Caution: don't pry
at the cover-to-head joint — damage to the sealing surfaces can cause oil leaks after
reinstallation.* On 4A-F engines only, drive out the old spark plug tube seals and install
new ones (illustrations 4.10a, 4.10b).

**Installation:** mating surfaces must be clean — scrape all sealant/gasket material, then
clean with lacquer thinner or acetone (residue/oil on the surfaces when installed risks
leaks). On DOHC engines, apply a thin uniform layer of RTV sealant to the gasket mating
surfaces and seal joints (illustrations 4.11a, 4.11b). *Note: the cover must be installed
within 5 minutes or the sealer will dry out.* Position a new gasket/seals, install the
cover, sealing washers, and nuts; tighten to the Specifications torque in three equal
steps. Reinstall the remaining parts, run the engine, check for oil leaks.

## 5. Intake manifold - removal and installation (PDF p.61-62) <a id="p61"></a>

*Note (as printed): on 4A-C engines, intake and exhaust manifolds share a common gasket —
the exhaust manifold (Section 6) must be removed to change it.*

**Removal:** disconnect the negative battery cable. Drain the cooling system (Chapter 1).
Remove the air cleaner, carburetor or throttle body, fuel injectors and fuel rail if
equipped (Chapter 4). Remove the heat shield if equipped (illustration 5.4). Label and
detach all wire harnesses, control cables, tubes, hoses and switches still connected to
the intake manifold. Unbolt any remaining braces (illustration 5.6). Remove the nuts/bolts
and detach the manifold (illustrations 5.7a-5.7d) — tap with a rubber mallet, or a block of
wood and hammer, if stuck. On 4A-C engines, remove the exhaust manifold too (Section 6).

**Installation:** scrape all old gasket material/sealant from manifold and cylinder head
(illustration 5.8 — *the aluminum gouges very easily, be very careful*), clean mating
surfaces with lacquer thinner or acetone; if the old gasket was leaking, have the manifold
checked for warpage at a machine shop and resurfaced if needed. Install a new gasket,
position the manifold, install the nuts/bolts, tighten in three equal steps to the
Specifications torque, working from the center out to avoid warping. Reverse the rest of
removal. Before starting the engine, check throttle linkage for smooth operation. Run the
engine and check for coolant/vacuum leaks; road test and check accessory operation.

## 6. Exhaust manifold - removal and installation (PDF p.62-63) <a id="p62"></a>

> **WARNING** — the engine must be completely cool before beginning this procedure.

*Note: on 4A-C engines the intake and exhaust manifolds share the same gasket — the intake
manifold must come off first (Section 5) to replace it.*

**Removal:** disconnect the negative battery cable. Unplug the oxygen sensor connector
(transfer it to the new manifold if replacing — Chapter 6). Remove the heat shield(s)
(illustrations 6.3a, 6.3b). Apply penetrating oil to the manifold mounting nuts/bolts and
exhaust-pipe-to-manifold nuts. Disconnect the exhaust pipe from the manifold (illustration
6.5). Remove any manifold-to-block braces (illustration 6.6). Remove the nuts/bolts and
detach the manifold and gasket (illustrations 6.7a-6.7c).

**Installation:** scrape old gasket material/carbon deposits from manifold and head mating
surfaces — careful not to gouge the delicate aluminum surfaces; check for warpage at a
machine shop if the old gasket was leaking. Position a new gasket over the cylinder head
studs, install the manifold, thread the mounting nuts/bolts. Working from the center out,
tighten to the Specifications torque in three equal steps. Reverse the rest of removal. Run
the engine and check for exhaust leaks.

## 7. Timing belt and sprockets - removal, inspection and installation (PDF p.63-70) <a id="p63"></a>

**Removal:** the timing belt match marks can be checked without removing the covers
(illustration 7.1). Disconnect the negative battery cable. Block the rear wheels, set the
parking brake, loosen the right front wheel lug nuts, raise and support the front securely
on jackstands. Remove the right front wheel and right lower splash pan. Loosen the water
pump pulley bolts (Chapter 3). Remove the drivebelts (Chapter 1). Unbolt the cruise control
actuator if equipped and set aside; on 4A-FE/4A-GE models remove the windshield washer
reservoir; on 4A-GE models with power steering, remove the power steering fluid reservoir
and detach hose clamps. Support the engine and remove the engine mount (Section 19). *Note:
if also removing the oil pan, support the engine with a hoist from above (Chapter 2B).*
Remove the water pump pulley bolts and pulley (may require removing the transaxle mount
bolts, Chapter 7, and raising the engine slightly for clearance). Remove the upper (No. 1)
timing belt cover.

Position the No. 1 piston at TDC on the compression stroke (Section 3). On 4A-C/4A-F(/4A-
FE) engines, confirm the small hole in the camshaft sprocket aligns with the TDC mark on
the cam bearing cap (illustration 7.11a); on 4A-GE engines, align the sprocket marks with
the rear cover marks (illustration 7.11b). Keep the crankshaft from turning with a large
screwdriver or pry bar (illustration 17.4 reference). Remove the crankshaft pulley bolt
and detach the pulley (illustrations 7.12a, 7.12b) — sometimes removable by hand. *Caution:
do not use a jaw-type puller — it will damage the pulley; use a puller that applies force
to the hub instead.* Remove the lower timing belt cover(s) and gaskets — may require
removing the A/C idler pulley (illustration 7.13a). Slip the belt guide off the crankshaft,
noting its installed orientation (cupped edge faces out, illustration 7.13b). If reusing
the belt, paint match marks between each sprocket and the belt, plus an arrow for direction
of travel (illustrations 7.14a, 7.14b).

Loosen the timing belt idler pulley set bolt, push the pulley down against spring tension
as far as it goes, and temporarily tighten it (illustration 7.15); slip the belt off the
sprockets. If a camshaft sprocket is worn/damaged, or replacing the camshaft oil seal(s):
remove the valve cover, hold the camshaft with a large wrench, remove the bolt, detach the
sprocket (illustration 7.16a) — *DO NOT use timing belt tension to keep the camshaft from
turning.* Note knock-pin/index-mark locations for reinstallation (illustrations 7.16b-
7.16d). On 4A-GE engines, the rear cover bolts become accessible once the camshaft
sprockets are off (illustration 7.17 — 7 bolts total, the 7th just below/right of the
engine mounting bracket, which must be disconnected to remove the cover). The crankshaft
sprocket and oil pump housing match marks must align (illustration 7.18a) — remove the
sprocket from the crankshaft if worn/damaged or to access the oil pump/crankshaft front
seal (illustration 7.18b — usually pried off with a small pry bar; use a puller if stuck).

> *Caution: do not bend, twist, or turn the timing belt inside out; keep it away from
> oil/coolant/fuel. Do not use timing belt tension to hold the camshaft or crankshaft from
> turning when installing sprocket bolt(s). Do not turn the crankshaft or camshaft more
> than a few degrees (needed for tooth alignment) with the timing belt removed.*

**Inspection** (illustrations 7.19a-7.24): remove the idler pulley (bolt + spring,
illustration 7.19a) and check bearings for smooth operation/excessive play; inspect the
spring for damage and compare free length to Specifications (illustration 7.19b). If the
belt broke during operation, valves and/or pistons may be damaged. Cracked or pulled-off
teeth (illustration 7.21) suggest the distributor, water pump, oil pump, or camshaft(s) may
have seized. Noticeable wear/cracks: check for nicks/burrs on the sprockets; wear/damage on
only one side: check the belt guide and sprocket/idler-pulley alignment. Replace the belt
if wear/damage is noted or the least bit questionable. *Note: 4A-C, 4A-F and 4A-FE engines
have square belt teeth; 4A-GE engines have rounded belt teeth (illustration 7.24) — get the
correct type for the replacement.* Correct anything that contributed to belt failure before
installing a new one.

**Installation** (illustrations 7.35a, 7.35b): remove all dirt/oil from the timing belt
area. If removed, install the idler pulley and tension spring, pulled back against spring
tension as far as possible, bolt temporarily tightened. Recheck the camshaft and crankshaft
timing marks. Install the belt on the crankshaft/camshaft sprockets and idler pulley,
aligning the original removal marks if reusing the belt. Slip the belt guide onto the
crankshaft, cupped side facing out. Keep tension on the side nearest the front of the
vehicle, aligning original marks if reusing. Loosen the idler pulley bolt 1/2-turn to let
the spring apply pressure, then tighten the idler pulley mounting bolt to the Specifications
torque. Slowly turn the crankshaft clockwise by hand two complete revolutions (720°) — if
resistance is felt, stop and recheck your work (don't force it: pistons may hit open
valves if valve timing is wrong). Recheck the timing match marks; repeat the installation
if not exactly aligned.

> **CAUTION** — DO NOT start the engine until absolutely certain the timing belt is
> installed correctly. Serious and costly engine damage could occur if the belt is
> installed wrong.

Measure the timing belt deflection (illustration 7.35a) and compare to Specifications —
repeat the adjustment if too loose/tight, then recheck. Reverse the rest of removal. Run
the engine and check for proper operation.

## 10. Camshaft and rocker arms - removal, inspection and installation (4A-C engine only) (PDF p.70-73) <a id="p70"></a>

**Rocker arm removal** (illustrations 10.5, 10.6): remove the negative battery cable, set
the engine at TDC (Section 3), remove the valve cover (Section 4). Loosen the locknuts and
back off the adjustment screws until valve tension is released. Remove the rocker arm shaft
by loosening each pedestal bolt a little at a time, in sequence (illustration 10.5). Leave
the bolts in place to hold the rocker arm assembly together as you lift it off as a unit
(illustration 10.6).

**Rocker arm and shaft inspection** (illustrations 10.11a, 10.11b): move the rocker arms to
check clearance — little or no movement should be felt; if movement is felt, disassemble
the shaft assembly (lay parts out in removal order). Visually inspect the rocker
arm-to-camshaft contact surface; replace excessively worn rockers. Measure the oil
clearance: inside-micrometer/dial-indicator the rocker arm bore, outside-micrometer the
shaft diameter where the rocker rides, subtract, compare to Specifications; replace as
necessary.

**Camshaft inspection** (illustrations 10.12-10.15b): visually inspect journals, lobes, and
distributor gear for score marks, pitting, wear, or overheating discoloration — replace if
excessive. Micrometer the lobe height, compare to Specifications — replace the camshaft if
below the minimum. Micrometer each journal diameter, compare to Specifications — replace if
worn/damaged. Check oil clearance per journal using Plastigage: clean bearing caps and
journals with lacquer thinner/acetone; lay the camshaft in place without lubrication; lay a
Plastigage strip on each journal (illustration 10.15a); install the bearing caps with
arrows pointing toward the front (timing belt end) of the engine; tighten the bolts to
Specifications torque in 1/4-turn increments (*note: don't turn the camshaft while the
Plastigage is in place*); remove the bolts/caps; compare the crushed Plastigage width
(widest point) to the envelope scale (illustration 10.15b) — replace the camshaft and/or
head if clearance exceeds spec; scrape off the Plastigage with a fingernail or credit card
edge, careful not to scratch/nick the journals or caps.

**Camshaft installation** (illustrations 10.17-10.18c): basically the reverse of removal.
Apply engine assembly lube or moly-base grease to camshaft lobes/journals, rocker arms, and
oil seal lips. Slip a new oil seal over the camshaft end, spring side facing in. Place the
camshaft in the head with the seal correctly positioned; apply RTV sealant dabs at the No.
1 cam bearing cap-to-head joint (illustration 10.18a). Install the bearing caps in original
locations and tighten in three steps, in sequence (illustration 10.18b), to Specifications
torque — confirm the valve cover studs are in their correct places (illustration 10.18c).

**Rocker arm installation** (illustrations 10.19, 10.20): if disassembled, install
components on the shaft in original locations with engine assembly lube/moly-base grease on
moving surfaces; before installing the shaft, confirm the oil holes point left, right, and
down as shown. Install and tighten the mounting bolts gradually, in three steps, following
the sequence shown, to Specifications torque. Adjust the valve clearances (Chapter 1).
Reinstall the remaining components in reverse order.

## 11. Camshafts and valve lifters - removal, inspection and installation (DOHC engines only) (PDF p.72-77) <a id="p72"></a>

**Camshaft removal (4A-GE)** (illustrations 11.4, 11.5): remove the valve covers (Section
4). Remove the timing belt, camshaft sprockets, and rear timing belt cover (Section 7).
Remove the distributor (Chapter 5). Measure camshaft endplay with a dial indicator
(illustration 11.4) — replace the camshaft and/or head if it exceeds the Specifications
limit. Check bearing caps for numbers, mark if needed for reinstallation. Loosen the
camshaft bearing cap bolts in sequence (illustration 11.5); remove the caps and lift each
camshaft out.

**Camshaft removal (4A-F)** (illustrations 11.10-11.19): *Note: obtain three 6 x 1.0 mm
service bolts, 16-20 mm long, before starting.* Remove the valve cover (Section 4).
Remove the distributor (Chapter 5). Remove the timing belt and camshaft sprocket (Section
7). Measure camshaft thrust clearance/endplay with a dial indicator (illustration 11.4
reference) — replace as needed if over spec.

*Intake camshaft:* position the service bolt hole of the intake camshaft gear at the top
(illustration 11.10). Alternately loosen the No. 1 intake and exhaust bearing cap bolts a
little at a time (illustration 11.11) until removable. Secure the intake camshaft sub-gear
to the main gear with a service bolt (illustration 11.12). *Caution: when removing the
camshaft, ensure the sub-gear's torsional spring force has been eliminated by this step.*
Uniformly loosen the intake camshaft bearing cap bolts in 1/4-turn increments, in sequence
(illustration 11.13), until removable by hand; lift off the caps and remove the camshaft.
*Caution: as the bolts loosen, ensure the camshaft moves up evenly — if one end stops and
the cam cocks, start over by retightening evenly.*

To disassemble the intake camshaft: mount it in a vise gripping the shaft's large hex.
Install the two remaining service bolts in the unthreaded sub-gear holes; using a
screwdriver against them, rotate the sub-gear clockwise and remove the No. 3 service bolt
(installed in the earlier step, illustration 11.14). Remove the sub-gear snap-ring
(illustration 11.15). The wave washer, sub-gear, camshaft gear spring, and oil seal can now
be removed (illustration 11.16 exploded view: 1 wave washer, 2 camshaft sub-gear, 3
camshaft gear spring, 4 camshaft gear, 5 camshaft). Measure the spring free length
(illustration) and compare to Specifications — replace if out of spec.

*Exhaust camshaft:* turn until the knock pin is near the bottom (illustration). Loosen the
bearing cap bolts in 1/4-turn increments, in sequence (illustration 11.19) — same
even-movement caution as above. Detach the caps and lift the camshaft out.

**Inspection:** camshaft — refer to Section 10's inspection/lobe-height/oil-clearance
procedures. On 4A-FE engines, temporarily install the camshafts without lifters or the
exhaust camshaft sub-gear and measure gear backlash with a dial indicator (illustration
11.21) — compare to Specifications, replace the camshafts if it exceeds spec. Lifters
(illustrations 11.22-11.24b): carefully label, then remove the valve lifters and shims
(mark intakes "I" and exhausts "E" — a magnetic tool works well for removal); inspect each
for scuffing/score marks; micrometer the outside diameter and the corresponding bore
diameter, subtract for oil clearance, compare to Specifications — a new head and/or lifters
are needed if clearance is excessive. Store lifters in a labeled, separated box; keep shims
with their lifters.

**Camshaft installation (4A-GE)** (illustrations 11.28a-11.28c): basically the reverse of
removal. Apply engine assembly lube/moly-base grease to lobes/journals, lifter sides, and
lifter shims; install lifters/shims in their original positions. Lubricate a new oil seal
lip, slip over the camshaft end (spring side in). Place the camshaft in the head with the
seal and knock pin correctly positioned (illustration 11.28a); install the bearing caps in
original locations (illustration 11.28b: I=intake, E=exhaust, front mark; cap numbers for
bearings 2-5); tighten the bolts in three steps, in sequence (illustration 11.28c), to
Specifications torque.

**Camshaft installation (4A-FE)** (illustrations 11.33-11.39): apply moly-base
grease/assembly lube to the lifters, install in original positions with valve adjustment
shims in place. Position the exhaust camshaft in the head with the knock pin near the
bottom. Apply a thin coat of RTV sealant to the head front at the bearing cap mating
surface (illustration 11.33). *Note: the cap must be installed immediately or the sealant
will dry prematurely.* Install the bearing caps (except the timing-belt-end one) in
numerical order, arrows toward the timing belt end (illustration 11.34); tighten in
1/4-turn increments in the factory sequence (illustration 11.35), to Specifications torque.
Rotate the exhaust camshaft until the knock pin is just above 9 o'clock (illustration
11.36); mesh the intake camshaft gear with the exhaust gear, aligning the installing marks
(illustration 11.37); roll the intake camshaft down into position, working the exhaust
camshaft back and forth until the intake camshaft sits evenly. Install the remaining
bearing caps in numerical order, arrows toward the timing belt end (illustration 11.39);
tighten in 1/4-turn increments, factory sequence, to Specifications torque. Remove the
service bolt from the camshaft gear. Install the remaining bearing cap and tighten to
Specifications. Install the timing belt and sprocket (Section 7). Reverse the rest of
removal.

## 12. Valve springs, retainers and seals - replacement (4A-C engine only) (PDF p.77-79) <a id="p77"></a>

*Note (as printed): broken valve springs and defective valve stem seals can be replaced
without removing the cylinder head. Two special tools and a compressed air source are
normally required — read through this Section and rent/buy the tools before beginning. If
compressed air isn't available, a length of nylon rope can substitute to keep valves from
falling into the cylinder.*

Refer to Section 10 and remove the rocker arm assembly. Remove the spark plug from the
affected cylinder (all plugs if replacing all valve stem seals). Turn the crankshaft until
that cylinder's piston is at TDC on the compression stroke (Section 3). If replacing all
seals, work cylinder-by-cylinder following the firing order (1-3-4-2). Thread an air hose
adapter into the spark plug hole (illustration 12.4) and connect a compressed air hose.
*Note: many compression gauges have a screw-in fitting that may work with a quick-disconnect
air hose fitting.*

Apply compressed air to the cylinder.

> **WARNING** — the piston may be forced down by compressed air, causing the crankshaft to
> turn suddenly. If a wrench used to position the No. 1 piston at TDC is still attached to
> the crankshaft nose bolt, it could cause damage or injury when the crankshaft moves.

The valves should be held in place by air pressure; if faces/seats are in poor condition,
leaks may prevent this — use the rope alternative below. Without compressed air: position
the piston just before TDC on the compression stroke, feed a long nylon rope through the
spark plug hole to fill the combustion chamber (leave the end hanging out for easy
removal); use a large ratchet/socket to rotate the crankshaft in normal rotation direction
(clockwise from the drivebelt end) until slight resistance is felt. Stuff shop rags into
the cylinder head holes above/below the valves to prevent parts/tools falling in; use a
valve spring compressor to compress the spring; remove the keepers with small needle-nose
pliers or a magnet (illustration 12.9). *Note: different compressor tool types exist —
lower-spring-coil-gripping type and camshaft-lever type; both work well.*

Remove the spring retainer, valve spring, then the stem oil seal. *Note: if air pressure
fails to hold the valve closed, the valve face and/or seat is probably damaged and the head
will need removal for repair.* Wrap a rubber band/tape around the top of the valve stem so
it won't fall into the combustion chamber, then release air pressure (or, if rope was used,
turn the crankshaft slightly opposite normal rotation). Inspect the valve stem for damage;
rotate the valve in the guide checking for eccentric movement (indicates a bent valve); move
the valve up/down in the guide checking for binding (bent valve or damaged guide either
way — head removal needed for repair). Reapply air pressure to retain the valve closed,
then remove the tape/band (or, with rope, rotate the crankshaft to slight resistance in
normal direction).

Lubricate the valve stem with engine oil, install a new oil seal (illustration 12.15).
Install the spring (closely wound coils next to the head) and retainer. Compress the
spring, position the keepers in the groove — a small dab of grease on each keeper's inside
holds it in place if needed (illustration 12.17). Release the compressor, confirm the
keepers are seated. Disconnect the air hose/adapter (or pull the rope out). Refer to
Section 10 and install the rocker arm assembly. Install the spark plug(s) and connect the
wire(s). Start and run the engine, check for oil leaks and unusual sounds from the valve
cover area.

## 13. Cylinder head - removal and installation (PDF p.78-81) <a id="p78"></a>

**Removal:** disconnect the negative battery cable. Drain the coolant from the block and

plugs. Remove the carburetor or throttle body and fuel injectors/rail if equipped (Chapter
4). Remove the intake and exhaust manifolds (Sections 5, 6). *Note: on 4A-C and 4A-F
engines, the manifolds may be left on the cylinder head until after the head is removed —
but this makes the head harder to lift.* Remove the valve cover(s) (Section 4). Remove the
timing belt and camshaft sprockets (Section 7) — on 4A-GE, also remove the rear timing belt
cover. On 4A-C engines, remove the camshaft and rocker arms (Section 10); on 4A-GE/4A-F,
remove the camshafts and lifters (Section 11). Remove the alternator and distributor
(Chapter 5). Unbolt the power steering pump if equipped and set aside without disconnecting
the hoses (Chapter 10 — note: source cross-reference as printed, though power steering pump
mounting is more typically covered elsewhere; treat this as the manual's own reference).
Unbolt the engine mount bracket from the engine (below the camshaft sprocket). Label and
remove any remaining coolant lines/tubes/cables/hoses/wires (illustration 13.13). Using the
new head gasket, outline the cylinders and head bolt pattern on cardboard (illustration
13.14a) — mark front/rear of engine, punch holes at bolt locations. Loosen the head bolts
in 1/4-turn increments until removable by hand, following the recommended sequence
(illustration 13.14b) to avoid warping the head. *On 4A-F engines, use a 10 mm 12-point
socket.* Store the bolts in the cardboard holder in their removal order/location — essential
for correct reinstallation. Lift the head off the block — if stuck, very carefully pry on a
casting protrusion beyond the gasket surface (illustration 13.15). Remove all external
components from the head for cleaning/inspection (see Chapter 2B for head servicing
procedures).

**Installation:** mating surfaces must be perfectly clean. Scrape all carbon and old gasket
material (illustration 13.18), clean with lacquer thinner or acetone — oil on the surfaces
risks a non-sealing gasket and leaks. Stuff the cylinders with clean shop rags while working
on the block; vacuum out debris that falls in. Check block/head mating surfaces for nicks,
deep scratches, other damage — a file can remove slight damage; excessive damage may
require machining.

Use a correctly-sized tap to chase the threads in the head bolt holes, then clean with
compressed air (make sure nothing remains in the holes).

> **WARNING** — wear eye protection.

Mount each bolt in a vise and run a die down the threads to remove corrosion and restore
them (dirt/corrosion/sealant/damaged threads affect torque readings). Reinstall the
components removed from the head. Position the new gasket over the block dowel pins
(illustration) — look for "Top" or "This side up" markings. Carefully set the head on the
block without disturbing the gasket. Apply a small amount of clean engine oil to the head
bolt threads before installing. Install the bolts in their original locations and tighten
finger-tight. Following the recommended sequence (illustration), tighten in several steps
to the Specifications torque (and rotation angle, on 4A-GE engines — see the multi-step
torque-plus-angle spec in this chapter's Specifications table above). *Note: on 4A-GE
engines, the exhaust-side bolts are 4-1/4 inches long and the intake-side bolts are 3-1/2
inches long — don't mix them up.* Reverse the rest of removal. Check/adjust the valves
(Chapter 1). Refill the cooling system, install a new oil filter, add oil (Chapter 1). Run
the engine and check for leaks; set the ignition timing and road test.

## 14. Oil pan - removal and installation (PDF p.80-82) <a id="p80"></a>

Disconnect the negative battery cable. Set the parking brake, block the rear wheels. Raise
the front and support securely on jackstands. Remove the splash shields from under the oil
pan. Drain the engine oil and remove the oil filter (Chapter 1). On DOHC engines, remove the
front exhaust pipe (and bracket) from the manifold and catalytic converter. Using an engine
hoist, support the engine from above; remove any mounts/crossmembers that would interfere
with oil pan removal. Remove the bolts (illustration 14.8a) and detach the oil pan (and
baffle plate on 4A-GE engines) — tap loose with a soft-face hammer if stuck (illustration
14.8b). *Don't pry at the gasket mating surface — may damage the surfaces and cause leaks.*

Scrape all old gasket material/sealant from block and pan, clean mating surfaces with
lacquer thinner or acetone. Confirm the block's threaded bolt holes are clean. Check the
pan flange for distortion, especially around bolt holes — if needed, flatten/restore the
gasket surface on a block of wood with a hammer. Inspect the oil pickup tube assembly for
cracks or a blocked strainer (illustration 14.12); remove and replace if necessary. If the
pickup was removed, reinstall now with a new gasket, torqued to Specifications.

If the pan originally used a gasket, use a new one, held in place with sealant. If
originally sealant-only (no gasket), apply a bead of RTV sealant to the pan flange
(illustration 14.15). *Note: the pan must be installed within 5 minutes of applying the
sealer.* Position the pan on the block and install the bolts; working from the center out,
tighten to Specifications torque in three steps. Reverse the rest of removal — add oil and
install a new oil filter. Run the engine and check for oil pressure and leaks.

## 15. Oil pump - removal and installation (PDF p.81-83) <a id="p81"></a>

**Removal:** remove the oil pan, oil pick-up tube assembly (and baffle plate on 4A-GE) per
Section 14. *Note: with the pan removed, the engine must be supported securely from above
for the remaining steps.* Remove the timing belt and crankshaft sprocket (Section 7) — on
4A-C engines, remove the timing belt idler pulley; on DOHC engines, temporarily move the
idler pulley aside and retighten the adjustment bolt. Remove the dipstick tube
(illustration 15.3). Remove the bolts and detach the oil pump body from the engine — may
require carefully prying between the front main bearing cap and the pump case with a
screwdriver (illustration 15.4c). Scrape all sealant/gasket material from the pump case and
block, clean with lacquer thinner or acetone.

**Installation:** pry the old oil seal out with a screwdriver; drive a new seal into place
with a deep socket and hammer, applying clean engine oil or multi-purpose grease to the
seal lip (illustrations 15.6a-15.6c). Apply a light coat of RTV sealant to the gasket
mating surfaces (illustration 15.7); place a new gasket on the block (dowel pins hold it in
place). Position the pump against the block (illustration 15.8 — make sure the drive gear
splines engage the crankshaft flats) and install the mounting bolts. Tighten to
Specifications torque in three steps, following a criss-cross pattern to avoid warping the
case. Reverse the rest of removal. Add oil, start the engine, and check for oil pressure and
leaks. Recheck the engine oil level.

## 17. Flywheel/driveplate - removal and installation (PDF p.83-85) <a id="p83"></a>

Raise the vehicle and support securely on jackstands; refer to Chapter 7 and remove the
transaxle. *If leaking, this is a good time to replace the front pump seal
(automatic-transaxle only).* Remove the pressure plate and clutch disc (Chapter 8, manual-
transaxle vehicles) — a good time to check/replace clutch components. Use a center punch or
paint to make match marks on the flywheel/driveplate and crankshaft for correct
reinstallation alignment (illustration 17.3). Remove the bolts securing the
flywheel/driveplate to the crankshaft — if the crankshaft turns, wedge a screwdriver in the
ring gear teeth to hold it (illustration 17.4). Remove the flywheel/driveplate — it's
fairly heavy, support it while removing the last bolt. *Automatic-transaxle vehicles have
spacers on both sides of the driveplate (illustration 17.5).*

Clean the flywheel of grease/oil; inspect for cracks, rivet grooves, burned areas, score
marks — light scoring removable with emery cloth; check for cracked/broken ring gear teeth;
use a straightedge on a flat surface to check for warpage. Clean and inspect the
flywheel/driveplate and crankshaft mating surfaces; if the crankshaft rear seal is leaking,
replace it (Section 18) before reinstalling.

Position the flywheel/driveplate against the crankshaft, aligning the removal marks (some
engines have an alignment dowel or staggered bolt holes for correct orientation). Apply
thread locking compound to the bolt threads before installing. Wedge a screwdriver in the
ring gear teeth to prevent turning while tightening the bolts to the Specifications torque,
following the recommended sequence (illustration 17.9), working up to final torque in three
steps. Reverse the rest of removal.

## 18. Crankshaft rear oil seal - replacement (PDF p.84-85) <a id="p84"></a>

The flywheel/driveplate must be removed for this procedure (Section 17). The seal *can* be
replaced without removing the oil pan or the seal retainer, but this quick method **isn't
recommended** — the seal lip is quite stiff and it's possible to cock the seal in the
retainer bore or damage it. If attempted anyway: pry out the old seal with a screwdriver
(illustration 18.2), apply engine oil or multi-purpose grease to the crankshaft journal and
new seal lip, and carefully push the new seal into place — work it onto the journal with a
smooth object like the end of an extension as you tap it in (don't rush, or you may damage
the seal).

**Recommended method** (requires removing the oil pan, Section 14, and the seal retainer):
after the oil pan is off, remove the bolts, detach the seal retainer (illustrations 18.4a),
and peel off all old gasket material. Position the seal/retainer assembly on wood blocks on
a workbench and drive the old seal out from the back side with a screwdriver (illustration
18.5). Drive the new seal into the retainer with a block of wood (illustration 18.6) or a
pipe section slightly smaller than the seal's OD. Lubricate the crankshaft seal journal and
new seal lip with engine oil or multi-purpose grease. Position a new gasket on the block.
Slowly and carefully push the seal onto the crankshaft (the lip is stiff — work it on with
a smooth object like an extension end as you push the retainer against the block). Install
and tighten the retainer bolts to the Specifications torque. Reverse the rest of removal.

## 19. Engine mount - check and replacement (PDF p.85) <a id="p85"></a>

The engine mount seldom requires attention, but a broken/deteriorated mount should be
replaced immediately — added driveline strain can cause damage/wear.

**Check:** the engine must be raised slightly to remove weight from the mount. Raise and
support the vehicle securely on jackstands, position a jack under the engine oil pan with a
large block of wood between the jack head and the pan, and carefully raise the engine just
enough to unload the mount.

> **WARNING** — DO NOT place any part of your body under the engine when it's supported
> only by a jack!

Check the mount rubber for cracking, hardening, or separation from the metal plates
(sometimes splits right down the center). Check for relative movement between the mount
plates and engine/frame (use a large screwdriver or pry bar to attempt to move it) — if
movement is noted, lower the engine and tighten the mount fasteners. Apply rubber
preservative to slow deterioration.

**Replacement:** disconnect the negative battery cable, raise and support the vehicle
securely on jackstands (if not already done), support the engine as in the Check procedure.
Remove the through-bolt from the frame bracket (illustration 19.8a); on models so equipped,
remove the engine mount brace (illustration 19.8b); remove the washer reservoir if it
obstructs access. Remove the two mount-to-engine-bracket nuts and detach the mount
(illustration 19.9). Installation is the reverse of removal — use thread locking compound
on the mount bolts/nuts, tighten securely. See Chapter 7 for transaxle mount replacement.

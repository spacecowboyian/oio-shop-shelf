## Introduction to the Toyota Corolla (PDF p.5) <a id="p5"></a>

Available in two- and four-door hatchback and sedan, two-door coupe, and four-door
station wagon body styles. Transversely-mounted inline four-cylinder engines, equipped
with either a carburetor or fuel injection. The engine drives the front wheels through a
five-speed manual or a three- or four-speed automatic transaxle via independent
driveaxles. Independent suspension (coil springs and struts) at all four wheels;
rack-and-pinion steering mounted behind the engine. Brakes on most models are disc
front / drum rear with power assist standard; some models have front and rear disc
brakes.

## Vehicle identification numbers (PDF p.5-6) <a id="p5"></a>

- **Vehicle Identification Number (VIN):** stamped on a plate attached to the left side of
  the dashboard, just inside the windshield on the driver's side — visible from outside the
  vehicle through the windshield. Also appears on the Vehicle Certificate of Title and
  Registration (where/when manufactured, model year, body style).
- **Vehicle Certification Plate (VC label):** attached to the left front door pillar. Contains
  manufacturer name, month/year of production, Gross Vehicle Weight Rating (GVWR), and
  certification statement.
- **Engine serial number:** stamped into the front of the block at the driver's end.
- **Transaxle ID number:** located on top of the transaxle housing (manual); a separate
  illustration shows the automatic transaxle ID number location.
- VIN plate decode diagram shows last digit of manufacturing year (example: `6 = 1985`)
  and manufacturing month code (`A` = Jan, `B` = Feb, `C` = Mar, ... `A` through `M`, `I` not
  used).
- Automatic transaxle ID plate shows two alternate year-code schemes used across the
  production run: a last-digit-of-year numeric code (example: `5 = 1985`) and, for later
  years, a letter code (`E` = 1985, `F` = 1986, `G` = 1987, ...). Confirmed against the p.6
  page image.

## Buying parts (PDF p.6) <a id="p6"></a>

Two general sources: **authorized dealer parts departments** (best source for
vehicle-unique parts — major engine parts, transmission parts, trim pieces) and
**independent retail auto parts stores** (good for frequently-worn components: clutch
components, exhaust systems, brake parts, tune-up parts; also general maintenance items
oil/grease/filters/spark plugs/belts/bulbs — often cheaper, more convenient hours).
Have engine and chassis numbers available, and bring old parts for positive ID. Check
that replacement parts don't invalidate a still-active warranty.

## Maintenance techniques, tools and working facilities (PDF p.6-11) <a id="p6"></a>

### Fasteners

Nuts, bolts, studs, screws. Most use a locking device (lockwasher, locknut, locking tab,
or thread adhesive). Special locknuts with nylon/fiber inserts can only be used once.
Rusted fasteners: treat with penetrating fluid before loosening. Badly rusted fasteners
may need chiseling, sawing, or a nut breaker. A broken bolt/stud can be drilled and
removed with a specialty tool, or the hole re-tapped to next oversize.

**Fastener sizes** — standard (SAE/USS) vs. metric hardware are not interchangeable even
when nearly identical dimensionally (example given: standard 1/2-13 x 1 bolt vs. metric
M12-1.75 x 25 bolt). Standard bolt heads measure across-flats in inches; metric in mm.
Standard bolts show grade via radiating slashes on the head (more slashes = higher grade;
Grades 0 through 5 common on automobiles). Metric bolts show a property-class number
molded into the head (higher number = stronger; classes 8.8, 9.8, 10.9 common). Standard
hex nuts show grade via dots (more dots = stronger); metric nuts via a number.

**Tightening sequences:** fasteners laid out in a pattern (cylinder head bolts, oil pan
bolts, differential cover bolts) must be loosened/tightened in sequence to avoid warping.
If no specific pattern is given: assemble finger-tight, then tighten each one full turn in
a criss-cross/diagonal pattern, then each one-half turn following the same pattern, then
each one-quarter turn at a time until proper torque. Reverse for loosening.

**Component disassembly:** keep track of removal sequence and orientation-sensitive parts
(e.g. grooved thrust washers). Lay parts out in removal order; sketch or photograph before
removal. A compartmented box (e.g. a muffin tin) helps keep fasteners sorted by location.

**Gasket sealing surfaces:** avoid prying with a screwdriver/bar (mars sealing surfaces);
use an old broom handle if prying is necessary. Scrape old gasket material carefully;
a flattened/sharpened copper-tubing scraper is recommended (copper is softer than the
surfaces being scraped).

> **WARNING** — if the vehicle is equipped with air conditioning, do not disconnect any of
> the A/C hoses without first having the system depressurized by a dealer service
> department or a service station.

**Hose removal:** loosen hose clamps, then work the hose free by rotating/working it back
and forth at the spigot — don't scratch/gouge the mating surface (especially radiator
hoses). Silicone lubricant eases removal. As a last resort on a hose being replaced anyway,
it can be slit and peeled off (avoid damaging the metal connection). Replace (don't reuse)
broken/damaged clamps; prefer screw-type clamps over wire-type when replacing.

### How to repair damaged threads (PDF p.11-12) <a id="p11"></a>

Internal threads (nut or bolt hole) can strip, usually from overtightening — most common
in aluminum parts. Partially-stripped threads often clean up with a tap/die. For badly
damaged threads, three approaches:

1. Drill and tap the hole to the next suitable oversize; install a larger-diameter
   bolt/screw/stud.
2. Drill and tap the hole to accept a threaded plug (or buy one pre-threaded to the
   original size), then drill/tap the plug to the original screw size, thread it in with a
   bolt and jam nut, then remove the jam nut/bolt.
3. Use a thread-repair kit (e.g. Heli-Coil or Slimsert) — drill the hole, tap with the
   kit's special tap, install the insert; the hole returns to original diameter/pitch.
   Works for straight-through and blind holes.

## Booster battery (jump) starting (PDF p.12) <a id="p12"></a>

> **Precautions:**
> a) Ignition switch OFF before connecting the booster battery.
> b) Turn off lights, heater, other electrical loads.
> c) Shield your eyes — safety goggles recommended.
> d) Booster battery must be the same voltage as the dead one.
> e) The two vehicles MUST NOT TOUCH each other.
> f) Transmission in Neutral (manual) or Park (automatic).
> g) If the booster battery isn't maintenance-free, remove vent caps and lay a cloth over
>    the vent holes.

Connect the red jumper cable to the positive (+) terminals of each battery. Connect one
end of the black jumper cable to the negative (-) terminal of the booster battery; connect
the other end to a good ground on the vehicle to be started (e.g. a bolt/bracket on the
engine block) — **not** to the dead battery's negative terminal — keeping the cable clear
of the fan, drivebelts, and other moving parts. Start the engine, then, at idle,
disconnect the jumper cables in reverse order of connection.

## Jacking and towing (PDF p.13-14) <a id="p13"></a>

> **WARNING** — the jack supplied with the vehicle should only be used for raising the
> vehicle when changing a tire or placing jackstands under the frame. Never work under the
> vehicle or start the engine while the jack is the only means of support.

**Jacking:** vehicle on a level surface, wheels blocked, transaxle in Park (automatic) or
Reverse (manual); apply the parking brake if the front is being raised; make sure no one
is in the vehicle while it's being raised. Remove jack/lug wrench/spare from the vehicle.

> **WARNING** — wheel covers may have sharp edges; be careful not to cut yourself.

Loosen lug nuts one-half turn (leave in place until the tire is off the ground). Position
the jack at the indicated jacking point (front and rear jacking points on each side — see
illustration). Raise until the tire clears the ground, remove lug nuts, swap in the spare,
install lug nuts with beveled edges facing in, snug them — don't fully tighten until the
vehicle is lowered (it could slip off the jack). Lower, remove jack, tighten lug nuts in a
criss-cross pattern (torque wrench per Chapter 1 figures if available, otherwise have them
checked by a shop soon after). Stow jack/tire/wrench, unblock wheels.

<!-- DIAGRAM CANDIDATE: p.14 — "Jacking points" illustration (front and rear jacking point locations per side), kind: chart, depth: mono. -->

**Towing:**

- **Manual transaxle vehicles:** tow with front (drive) wheels off the ground as a general
  rule; release the parking brake. If towed with front wheels on the ground, place the
  transaxle in Neutral and the ignition key in the ACC position (the steering lock isn't
  strong enough to hold the wheels straight otherwise).
- **Automatic transaxle vehicles:**
  > **CAUTION** — never tow an automatic-transaxle vehicle from the rear with the front
  > wheels on the ground. If towed from the rear, put the front wheels on a towing dolly.

  Can only be towed from the rear with front wheels off the ground. Can be towed with all
  four wheels on the ground, from the front only, if **both**: (a) transmission fluid level
  is up to the HOT mark on the dipstick (Chapter 1 for checking procedure), and (b) max
  speed 30 mph, max distance 50 miles — if either condition can't be confirmed, tow with
  front wheels off the ground (or use a towing dolly) instead, since severe transmission
  damage could otherwise occur. The parking brake must be released during towing.
- **All vehicles:** use equipment specifically designed for towing, attached to the main
  structural members (not bumpers/brackets). A safety chain must be used at all times.
  Power steering and brakes won't work with the engine off. Obey all applicable state/local
  towing laws.

## Automotive chemicals and lubricants (PDF p.14-15) <a id="p14"></a>

**Cleaners:** carburetor/choke cleaner (strong solvent for gum/varnish/carbon; leaves a
dry film — not recommended on electrical components); brake system cleaner (removes
grease/brake fluid, no residue, can eliminate brake squeal from contaminants); electrical
cleaner (removes oxidation/corrosion/carbon deposits, restores current flow — for
electrical contacts, spark plugs, carburetor jets, voltage regulators); demoisturants
(remove water/moisture from alternators, voltage regulators, connectors, fuse blocks —
non-conductive, non-corrosive, non-flammable); degreasers (heavy-duty solvents for the
outside of the engine/chassis; rinse with water or solvent depending on type).

**Lubricants:** motor oil (weights 5 to 80; multi-viscosity oils span 5W-20 to 20W-50);
gear oil (differentials, manual transmissions, high-temp areas); chassis/wheel-bearing
grease (heavy grease for wheel bearings, balljoints, tie-rod ends, universal joints);
high-temperature wheel bearing grease (disc-brake-equipped vehicles; usually contains
moly); white grease (metal-to-metal, water-resistant, stays soft roughly -100°F to
+190°F); assembly lube (extreme-pressure, moly-based, for initial engine start-up on
high-load parts like main/rod bearings and cam lobes); silicone lubricants (rubber,
plastic, vinyl, nylon parts); graphite lubricants (where oil can't be used due to
contamination, e.g. locks — electrically conductive, won't foul contacts); moly
penetrants (loosen/lubricate frozen, rusted, corroded fasteners); heat-sink grease
(electrically non-conductive; for mounting electronic ignition modules where heat must be
carried away).

**Sealants:** RTV sealant (silicone, air-curing; widely used supplementary sealer with
most low/medium-temp gaskets); anaerobic sealant (cures only in the absence of air, i.e.
after assembly — vs. RTV which cures on air exposure); thread and pipe sealant (Teflon-
based, for hydraulic/pneumatic fittings and vacuum lines; spray, paint-on liquid, or
wrap-around tape).

**Chemicals:** anti-seize compound (prevents seizing/galling/cold welding/rust/corrosion
in fasteners; high-temp anti-seize, usually copper/graphite-based, for exhaust system and
manifold bolts); anaerobic locking compounds (keep fasteners from vibrating loose,
medium-strength for smaller/removable fasteners, high-strength for large nuts/bolts/
studs not regularly removed); oil additives (viscosity-index improvers, friction-reducing
treatments — most oil manufacturers caution against use with their oils); gas additives
(dissolve gum/varnish, break down combustion-chamber carbon deposits, upper-cylinder
lubricants, moisture removal from the tank).

**Miscellaneous:** brake fluid (withstands brake-system heat/pressure — keep away from
paint/plastic, reseal opened containers); weatherstrip adhesive; undercoating (petroleum-
based, tar-like, protects underside metal from corrosion, also sound-deadening); waxes
and polishes (protect painted/plated surfaces; some polishes are mildly abrasive to remove
oxidized paint; many modern non-wax polishes use polymers/silicones).

## Safety first! (PDF p.16-17) <a id="p16"></a>

**Essential DOs and DON'Ts:**

- DON'T rely on a jack when working under the vehicle — always use approved jackstands
  under the recommended lift/support points.
- DON'T attempt to loosen extremely tight fasteners (e.g. wheel lug nuts) while the
  vehicle is on a jack — it may fall.
- DON'T start the engine without confirming the transmission is in Neutral (or Park) and
  the parking brake is set.
- DON'T remove the radiator cap from a hot cooling system — let it cool, or cover it with
  a cloth and release the pressure gradually.
- DON'T attempt to drain engine oil until it has cooled enough not to burn you.
- DON'T touch any part of the engine or exhaust system until it has cooled sufficiently.
- DON'T siphon toxic liquids (gasoline, antifreeze, brake fluid) by mouth, or allow them
  on your skin.
- DON'T inhale brake lining dust — potentially hazardous (see Asbestos below).
- DON'T allow spilled oil/grease to remain on the floor — wipe it up.
- DON'T use loose-fitting wrenches or other tools that may slip and cause injury.
- DON'T push on wrenches when loosening/tightening — pull toward you; if pushing is
  unavoidable, push with an open hand to avoid scraped knuckles if it slips.
- DON'T attempt to lift a heavy component alone — get help.
- DON'T rush or take unsafe shortcuts.
- DON'T allow children or animals in or around the vehicle while working on it.
- DO wear eye protection with power tools (drill, sander, bench grinder, etc.) and when
  working under a vehicle.
- DO keep loose clothing and long hair clear of moving parts.
- DO make sure any hoist used has an adequate safe working load rating.
- DO have someone check on you periodically when working alone.
- DO carry out work in a logical sequence and make sure everything is correctly assembled
  and tightened.
- DO keep chemicals/fluids tightly capped and out of reach of children and pets.
- DO remember your vehicle's safety affects yourself and others — get professional advice
  if in doubt.

**Asbestos:** certain friction, insulating, sealing products (brake linings, brake bands,
clutch linings, torque converters, gaskets, etc.) may contain asbestos. Avoid inhaling
dust from such products — hazardous to health. If in doubt, assume they contain asbestos.

**Fire:** gasoline is highly flammable — no smoking or open flame near a vehicle being
worked on. Sparks (electrical short, metal-to-metal contact, static electricity) can
ignite gasoline vapors in a confined space. Never use gasoline for cleaning parts — use an
approved safety solvent. Always disconnect the battery ground (-) cable before working on
the fuel or electrical system. Never risk spilling fuel on a hot engine/exhaust component.
Keep a fire extinguisher suitable for fuel/electrical fires on hand; never use water on a
fuel or electrical fire.

**Fumes:** gasoline vapor and some cleaning-solvent vapors are highly toxic — can cause
unconsciousness or death if inhaled. Drain/pour volatile fluids only in a well-ventilated
area; read container instructions and never use unmarked containers. Never run the engine
in an enclosed space (garage) — exhaust contains carbon monoxide, extremely poisonous; if
you must run the engine, do so in open air or with the rear of the vehicle outside the
work area. Never drain/pour gasoline or run the engine over an open inspection pit —
fumes are heavier than air and concentrate in the pit with possibly lethal results.

**The battery:** never create a spark or allow a bare light bulb near a battery — batteries
give off hydrogen gas, which is highly explosive. Always disconnect the battery ground (-)
cable before working on the fuel or electrical systems. Loosen filler caps (non-sealed/
non-maintenance-free batteries only) when charging from an external source; don't charge
at an excessive rate or the battery may burst. Electrolyte is corrosive even when diluted
— avoid contact with clothing/skin; wear eye protection when cleaning the battery.

**Household current:** when using electric power tools/inspection lights on household
current, ensure correct plug connection and proper grounding; don't use in damp
conditions; avoid sparks or excessive heat near fuel/fuel vapor.

**Secondary ignition system voltage:** a severe electric shock can result from touching
ignition-system parts (e.g. spark plug wires) while the engine is running or being
cranked, particularly if components are damp or insulation is defective. On an electronic
ignition system, secondary voltage is much higher — could prove fatal.

## Troubleshooting (PDF p.17-22) <a id="p17"></a>

An easy-reference symptom guide, grouped by system, cross-referenced to Chapter/Section.
Method: work by elimination, start with the simplest possibility, don't overlook the
obvious (empty gas tank, lights left on), determine *why* a problem happened (not just
fix the symptom) — e.g. if a fuse blows repeatedly, find the cause rather than just
replacing fuses.

### Symptom index (Section numbers as printed)

**Engine:** 1 Engine will not rotate when attempting to start · 2 Engine rotates but will
not start · 3 Starter motor operates without rotating engine · 4 Engine hard to start when
cold · 5 Engine hard to start when hot · 6 Starter motor noisy or excessively rough in
engagement · 7 Engine starts but stops immediately · 8 Engine lopes while idling or idles
erratically · 9 Engine misses at idle speed · 10 Engine misses throughout driving speed
range · 11 Engine stalls · 12 Engine lacks power · 13 Engine backfires · 14 Pinging or
knocking engine sounds during acceleration or uphill · 15 Engine diesels (continues to
run) after switching off.

**Engine electrical system:** 16 Battery will not hold a charge · 17 Ignition light fails
to go out · 18 Ignition light fails to come on when key is turned on.

**Fuel system:** 19 Excessive fuel consumption · 20 Fuel leakage and/or fuel odor.

**Cooling system:** 21 Overheating · 22 Overcooling · 23 External coolant leakage · 24
Internal coolant leakage · 25 Coolant loss · 26 Poor coolant circulation.

**Clutch:** 27 Fails to release (pedal pressed to the floor - shift lever does not move
freely in and out of Reverse) · 28 Clutch slips (engine speed increases with no increase
in vehicle speed) · 29 Grabbing (chattering) as clutch is engaged · 30 Squeal or rumble
with clutch fully disengaged (pedal depressed) · 31 Clutch pedal stays on floor when
disengaged.

**Manual transaxle:** 32 Noisy in Neutral with engine running · 33 Noisy in all gears · 34
Noisy in one particular gear · 35 Slips out of high gear · 36 Difficulty in engaging
gears · 37 Oil leakage.

**Automatic transaxle** *(note: home-mechanic diagnosis is limited due to complexity —
for problems other than the following, see a dealer or transmission shop)*: 38 General
shift mechanism problems · 39 Transaxle will not downshift with accelerator pedal pressed
to the floor · 40 Transaxle slips, shifts rough, is noisy or has no drive in forward or
reverse gears · 41 Fluid leakage.

**Driveaxles:** 42 Clicking noise in turns · 43 Knock or clunk when accelerating after
coasting · 44 Shudder or vibration during acceleration.

**Rear axle:** 45 Noise.

### Full symptom → cause tables (PDF p.18-22) <a id="p18"></a>

**1. Engine will not rotate when attempting to start:** (1) Battery terminal connections
loose or corroded — check/tighten/de-corrode. (2) Battery discharged or faulty — check by
switching on headlights/wipers with key On; if they fail to function, battery is
discharged. (3) Automatic transaxle not completely in Park/Neutral, or clutch pedal not
completely depressed. (4) Broken/loose/disconnected wiring in the starting circuit —
inspect wiring/connectors at battery, starter solenoid, ignition switch. (5) Starter
motor pinion jammed in flywheel ring gear — if manual transaxle, put it in gear and rock
the vehicle to manually turn the engine; remove starter and inspect pinion/flywheel at
earliest convenience (Ch. 5). (6) Starter solenoid faulty (Ch. 5). (7) Starter motor
faulty (Ch. 5). (8) Ignition switch faulty (Ch. 12).

**2. Engine rotates but will not start:** (1) Fuel tank empty. (2) Fault in carburetor or
fuel injection system (Ch. 4). (3) Battery discharged (engine rotates slowly) — check
electrical components per Section 1. (4) Battery terminal connections loose/corroded. (5)
Fuel pump faulty (Ch. 4). (6) Excessive moisture on, or damage to, ignition components
(Ch. 5). (7) Worn, faulty or incorrectly gapped spark plugs (Ch. 1). (8) Broken/loose/
disconnected wiring in starting circuit. (9) Distributor loose, causing ignition timing
to change — reposition to start, then reset timing (Ch. 1) ASAP. (10) Broken/loose/
disconnected wires at the ignition coil, or faulty coil (Ch. 5).

**3. Starter motor operates without rotating engine:** (1) Starter pinion sticking —
remove starter (Ch. 5) and inspect. (2) Starter pinion or flywheel teeth worn/broken —
remove flywheel/driveplate access cover and inspect.

**4. Engine hard to start when cold:** (1) Battery discharged or low — check per Section
1. (2) Fault in fuel or electrical systems (Ch. 4, 5). (3) Carburetor in need of overhaul
(carbureted models) (Ch. 4). (4) Distributor rotor carbon-tracked and/or damaged (Ch. 1,
5). (5) Choke control stuck or inoperative (carbureted models) (Ch. 1, 4).

**5. Engine hard to start when hot:** (1) Air filter clogged (Ch. 1). (2) Fault in fuel or
electrical systems (Ch. 4, 5). (3) Fuel not reaching the carburetor (carburetor-equipped
models) (see Section 2). (4) Vacuum leak at intake manifold or carburetor/throttle body
gasket surfaces — check mounting bolts/nuts tight, vacuum hoses connected/positioned/in
good condition.

**6. Starter motor noisy or excessively rough in engagement:** (1) Pinion or flywheel gear
teeth worn or broken — remove the cover at the rear of the engine (if so equipped) and
inspect. (2) Starter motor mounting bolts loose or missing.

**7. Engine starts but stops immediately:** (1) Loose or faulty electrical connections at
distributor, coil, or alternator. (2) Fault in fuel or electrical systems (Ch. 4, 5). (3)
Insufficient fuel reaching the carburetor (carburetor-equipped models) — check fuel pump
(Ch. 4). (4) Leaking EGR valve or plugged PCV valve (Ch. 1, 6).

**8. Engine lopes while idling or idles erratically:** (1) Vacuum leakage — check mounting
bolts/nuts at carburetor/throttle body and intake manifold; check vacuum hoses connected
and in good condition. A stethoscope or fuel hose held to your ear can detect the hissing
of a vacuum leak; soapy water solution also detects leaks. (2) Fault in fuel or electrical
systems (Ch. 4, 5). (3) Air filter clogged (Ch. 1). (4) Fuel pump not delivering
sufficient fuel to the carburetor (carbureted models) (Ch. 4). (5) Carburetor out of
adjustment (Ch. 4). (6) Leaking head gasket — perform a compression check (Ch. 2). (7)
Camshaft lobes worn (Ch. 2).

**9. Engine misses at idle speed:** (1) Spark plugs worn or not gapped properly (Ch. 1).
(2) Fault in fuel or electrical systems (Ch. 4, 5). (3) Faulty spark plug wires (Ch. 1).

**10. Engine misses throughout driving speed range:** (1) Fuel filter clogged and/or
impurities in the fuel system (Ch. 1). (2) Faulty or incorrectly gapped spark plugs (Ch.
1). (3) Fault in fuel or electrical systems (Ch. 4, 5). (4) Incorrect ignition timing (Ch.
1). (5) Cracked distributor cap, disconnected distributor wires, damaged distributor
components (Ch. 1). (6) Defective spark plug wires (Ch. 1). (7) Faulty emissions system
components (Ch. 6). (8) Low or uneven cylinder compression pressures — remove spark plugs
and test with a compression gauge (Ch. 2). (9) Weak or faulty ignition system (Ch. 5). (10)
Vacuum leaks at carburetor/throttle body, intake manifold or vacuum hoses (see Section 8).

**11. Engine stalls:** (1) Idle speed incorrect — refer to VECI label and Ch. 1. (2) Fuel
filter clogged and/or water and impurities in the fuel system (Ch. 1). (3) Distributor
components damp or damaged (Ch. 5). (4) Fault in fuel system or sensors (Ch. 4, 6). (5)
Faulty emissions system components (Ch. 6). (6) Faulty or incorrectly gapped spark plugs
(Ch. 1), including spark plug wires. (7) Vacuum leak at carburetor/throttle body, intake
manifold or vacuum hoses — see Section 8.

**12. Engine lacks power:** (1) Incorrect ignition timing (Ch. 1). (2) Fault in fuel or
electrical systems (Ch. 4, 5). (3) Excessive play in the distributor shaft — also check
for damaged rotor, faulty distributor cap/wires (Ch. 1, 5). (4) Faulty or incorrectly
gapped spark plugs (Ch. 1). (5) Carburetor not adjusted properly or excessively worn
(carbureted models) (Ch. 4). (6) Faulty coil (Ch. 5). (7) Brakes binding (Ch. 1). (8)
Automatic transaxle fluid level incorrect (Ch. 1). (9) Clutch slipping (Ch. 8). (10) Fuel
filter clogged and/or impurities in the fuel system (Ch. 1). (11) Emissions control system
not functioning properly (Ch. 6). (12) Substandard fuel — fill with proper octane. (13)
Low or uneven cylinder compression pressures — test with a compression tester (detects
leaking valves and/or a blown head gasket) (Ch. 2).

**13. Engine backfires:** (1) Emissions system not functioning properly (Ch. 6). (2) Fault
in fuel or electrical systems (Ch. 4, 5). (3) Ignition timing incorrect (Ch. 1). (4) Faulty
secondary ignition system — cracked spark plug insulator, faulty plug wires, distributor
cap and/or rotor (Ch. 1, 5). (5) Carburetor or fuel injection system in need of adjustment
or worn excessively (Ch. 4). (6) Vacuum leak at carburetor/throttle body, intake manifold
or vacuum hoses (see Section 8). (7) Valves sticking (Ch. 2).

**14. Pinging or knocking engine sounds during acceleration or uphill:** (1) Incorrect
grade of fuel — fill with proper octane rating. (2) Fault in fuel or electrical systems
(Ch. 4, 5). (3) Ignition timing incorrect (Ch. 1). (4) Carburetor in need of adjustment
(carbureted models) (Ch. 4). (5) Improper spark plugs — check plug type against the VECI
label in the engine compartment; also check plugs and wires for damage (Ch. 1). (6) Worn
or damaged distributor components (Ch. 5).

**15. Engine diesels (continues to run) after switching off:** (1) Idle speed too high —
refer to Ch. 1. (2) Fault in fuel or electrical systems (Ch. 4, 5). (3) Ignition timing
incorrectly adjusted (Ch. 1). (4) Thermo-controlled air cleaner heat valve not operating
properly (Ch. 1, 6). (5) Excessive engine operating temperature — probable causes:
malfunctioning thermostat, clogged radiator, faulty water pump (Ch. 3).

**16. Battery will not hold a charge:** (1) Alternator drivebelt defective or not adjusted
properly (Ch. 1). (2) Electrolyte level low or battery discharged (Ch. 1). (3) Battery
terminals loose or corroded (Ch. 1). (4) Alternator not charging properly (Ch. 5). (5)
Loose, broken or faulty wiring in the charging circuit (Ch. 5). (6) Short in the vehicle
wiring causing a continual drain on the battery (see Ch. 12 and the Wiring Diagrams). (7)
Battery defective internally.

**17. Ignition light fails to go out:** (1) Fault in the alternator or charging circuit
(Ch. 5). (2) Alternator drivebelt defective or not properly adjusted (Ch. 1).

**18. Ignition light fails to come on when key is turned on:** (1) Instrument cluster
warning light bulb defective (Ch. 12). (2) Alternator faulty (Ch. 5). (3) Fault in the
instrument cluster printed circuit, dashboard wiring or bulb holder (Ch. 12).

**19. Excessive fuel consumption:** (1) Dirty or clogged air filter element (Ch. 1). (2)
Incorrectly set ignition timing (Ch. 1). (3) Choke sticking or improperly adjusted
(carbureted models) (Ch. 1). (4) Emissions system not functioning properly (Ch. 6). (5)
Fault in fuel or electrical systems (Ch. 4, 5). (6) Carburetor or fuel injection system
internal parts excessively worn or damaged (Ch. 4). (7) Low tire pressure or incorrect
tire size (Ch. 1).

**20. Fuel leakage and/or fuel odor:** (1) Leak in a fuel feed or vent line (Ch. 4). (2)
Tank overfilled — fill only to automatic shut-off. (3) Evaporative emissions system
canister clogged (Ch. 6). (4) Vapor leaks from system lines (Ch. 4). (5) Carburetor or
fuel injection system internal parts excessively worn or out of adjustment (Ch. 4).

**21. Overheating:** (1) Insufficient coolant in the system (Ch. 1). (2) Water pump
drivebelt defective or not adjusted properly (Ch. 1). (3) Radiator core blocked or
radiator grille dirty/restricted (Ch. 3). (4) Thermostat faulty (Ch. 3). (5) Fan blades
broken or cracked (Ch. 3). (6) Radiator cap not maintaining proper pressure — have the cap
pressure tested by a gas station or repair shop. (7) Ignition timing incorrect (Ch. 1).

**22. Overcooling:** Thermostat faulty (Ch. 3).

**23. External coolant leakage:** (1) Deteriorated or damaged hoses, or loose clamps —
replace hoses and/or tighten clamps at hose connections (Ch. 1). (2) Water pump seals
defective — water drips from the weep hole in the water pump body (Ch. 3). (3) Leakage
from radiator core or header tank — requires professional radiator repair (see Ch. 3 for
removal procedures). (4) Engine drain plug leaking (Ch. 1), or water jacket core plugs
leaking (see Ch. 2).

**24. Internal coolant leakage:** *Note: internal coolant leaks can usually be detected by
examining the oil — check the dipstick and inside of the cylinder head cover for water
deposits and a milkshake-like oil consistency.* (1) Leaking cylinder head gasket — have
the cooling system pressure tested. (2) Cracked cylinder bore or cylinder head — dismantle
the engine and inspect (Ch. 2).

**25. Coolant loss:** (1) Too much coolant in the system (Ch. 1). (2) Coolant boiling away
due to overheating (see Section 21).

**26. Poor coolant circulation:** (1) Inoperative water pump — quick test: pinch the top
radiator hose closed with the engine idling, then release; you should feel the surge of
coolant if the pump is working (Ch. 1). (2) Restriction in the cooling system — drain,
flush, refill (Ch. 1); if necessary, remove the radiator (Ch. 3) and have it reverse
flushed. (3) Water pump drivebelt defective or not adjusted properly (Ch. 1). (4)
Thermostat sticking (Ch. 3).

**27. Fails to release (pedal pressed to the floor - shift lever does not move freely in
and out of Reverse):** (1) Leak in the clutch hydraulic system — check master cylinder,
slave cylinder, lines (Ch. 8). (2) Clutch plate warped or damaged (Ch. 8). (3) Worn or dry
clutch release shaft bushing (Ch. 8).

**28. Clutch slips (engine speed increases with no increase in vehicle speed):** (1)
Linkage out of adjustment (Ch. 8). (2) Clutch plate oil-soaked or lining worn — remove
clutch (Ch. 8) and inspect. (3) Clutch plate not seated — may take 30-40 normal starts for
a new one to seat.

**29. Grabbing (chattering) as clutch is engaged:** (1) Oil on clutch plate lining —
remove (Ch. 8) and inspect; correct any leakage source. (2) Worn or loose engine or
transaxle mounts — these move slightly when the clutch is released; inspect mounts and
bolts (Ch. 2). (3) Worn splines on clutch plate hub — remove clutch components (Ch. 8) and
inspect. (4) Warped pressure plate or flywheel — remove clutch components and inspect. (5)
Weak diaphragm spring, allowing pressure plate to bounce on engagement — remove clutch
components and inspect.

**30. Squeal or rumble with clutch fully disengaged (pedal depressed):** (1) Worn,
defective or broken release bearing (Ch. 8). (2) Worn or broken pressure plate springs (or
diaphragm fingers) (Ch. 8).

**31. Clutch pedal stays on floor when disengaged:** Linkage or release bearing binding —
inspect the linkage or remove the clutch components as necessary.

**32. Noisy in Neutral with engine running:** (1) Input shaft bearing worn. (2) Damaged
main drive gear bearing. (3) Worn countershaft bearings. (4) Worn or damaged countershaft
end-play shims.

**33. Noisy in all gears:** (1) Any of the above (Section 32), and/or: (2) Insufficient
lubricant (see checking procedures in Ch. 1).

**34. Noisy in one particular gear:** (1) Worn, damaged or chipped gear teeth for that
gear. (2) Worn or damaged synchronizer for that gear.

**35. Slips out of high gear:** (1) Transaxle loose on clutch housing (Ch. 7). (2) Shift
cables interfering with the engine mounts or clutch lever (Ch. 7). (3) Shift cables not
working freely (Ch. 7). (4) Dirt between the transaxle case and engine, or transaxle
misalignment (Ch. 7). (5) Worn or improperly adjusted linkage (Ch. 7).

**36. Difficulty in engaging gears:** (1) Clutch not releasing completely (see clutch
adjustment, Ch. 1). (2) Loose, damaged or out-of-adjustment shift linkage — make a
thorough inspection, replace parts as necessary (Ch. 7).

**37. Oil leakage:** (1) Excessive amount of lubricant in the transaxle (see Ch. 1 for
correct checking procedures) — drain as required. (2) Driveaxle oil seal or speedometer
oil seal in need of replacement (Ch. 7).

**38. General shift mechanism problems:** (1) Ch. 7 covers checking/adjusting the shift
linkage on automatic transaxles. Common problems attributable to poorly adjusted linkage:
engine starting in gears other than Park or Neutral; shifter indicator pointing to a gear
other than the one actually selected. (2) Vehicle moves when in Park — refer to Ch. 7 to
adjust the linkage.

**39. Transaxle will not downshift with accelerator pedal pressed to the floor:** (1) Ch.
7 covers adjusting the throttle valve (downshift) cable to enable the transaxle to
downshift properly.

**40. Transaxle slips, shifts roughly, is noisy or has no drive in forward or reverse
gears:** *Note: many probable causes exist, but the home mechanic should be concerned with
only one possibility — fluid level.* (1) Before taking the vehicle to a repair shop, check
fluid level/condition (Ch. 1); correct level, or change fluid/filter, as needed. If the
problem persists, have a professional diagnose the cause.

**41. Fluid leakage:** *Note: automatic transaxle fluid is deep red when new but darkens
with age — distinguish from engine oil (which can be blown onto the transaxle by airflow)
by comparing a drop of each on a clean lint-free paper towel.* (1) To pinpoint a leak,
degrease/steam-clean the underside, drive at low speed (so airflow doesn't blow the leak
far from its source), then raise the vehicle and locate the leak. Common areas: (a) Pan —
tighten mounting bolts and/or replace the pan gasket (Ch. 7); (b) Filler pipe — replace
the rubber seal where the pipe enters the transaxle case; (c) Transaxle oil lines —
tighten connectors where lines enter the case and/or replace the lines; (d) Vent pipe —
transaxle overfilled and/or water in fluid (see checking procedures, Ch. 1); (e)
Speedometer connector — replace the O-ring where the speedometer cable enters the
transaxle case (Ch. 7).

**42. Clicking noise in turns:** Worn or damaged outer joint — check for cut or damaged
seals; repair as necessary (Ch. 8).

**43. Knock or clunk when accelerating after coasting:** Worn or damaged inner joint —
check for cut or damaged seals; repair as necessary (Ch. 8).

**44. Shudder or vibration during acceleration:** (1) Excessive joint angle — have checked
and corrected as necessary (Ch. 8). (2) Worn or damaged CV joints — repair or replace (Ch.
8). (3) Sticking CV joint assembly — correct or replace as necessary (Ch. 8). Confirmed
against the p.22 page image: all three items in this section cite Chapter 8.

**45. Noise (rear axle):** (1) Road noise — no corrective procedures available. (2) Tire
noise — inspect tires, check pressures (Ch. 1). (3) Rear wheel bearings loose, worn or
damaged (Ch. 1).

**46. Vehicle pulls to one side during braking:** (1) Defective, damaged or oil-
contaminated disc brake pads on one side — inspect (Ch. 9). (2) Excessive wear of brake
pad material or disc on one side — inspect and correct. (3) Loose or disconnected front
suspension components — inspect, tighten all bolts to specified torque (Ch. 10). (4)
Defective caliper assembly — remove and inspect for a stuck piston or other damage (Ch.
9).

**47. Noise (high-pitched squeal with the brakes applied):** (1) Disc brake pads worn out
— noise comes from the wear sensor rubbing the disc (not on all vehicles) or the pad
backing plate itself once material is fully worn — replace pads immediately (Ch. 9); if
pad material is completely worn away, inspect the brake discs for damage (Ch. 9).

**48. Excessive brake pedal travel:** (1) Partial brake system failure — inspect the
entire system (Ch. 9) and correct. (2) Insufficient fluid in the master cylinder — check
(Ch. 1), add fluid, bleed if necessary. (3) Rear brakes not adjusting properly — make a
series of starts/stops in Reverse; if that doesn't correct it, remove the drums and
inspect the self-adjusters (Ch. 9).

**49. Brake pedal feels spongy when depressed:** (1) Air in the hydraulic lines — bleed
the brake system (Ch. 9). (2) Faulty flexible hoses — inspect all system hoses/lines,
replace as necessary. (3) Master cylinder mounting bolts/nuts loose. (4) Master cylinder
defective (Ch. 9).

**50. Excessive effort required to stop vehicle:** (1) Power brake booster not operating
properly (Ch. 9). (2) Excessively worn linings or pads — inspect and replace (Ch. 9). (3)
One or more caliper pistons or wheel cylinders seized or sticking — inspect/rebuild (Ch.
9). (4) Brake linings or pads contaminated with oil or grease — inspect and replace (Ch.
9). (5) New pads or shoes installed and not yet seated — takes a while to seat against
the drum/disc.

**51. Pedal travels to the floor with little resistance:** Little or no fluid in the
master cylinder reservoir, caused by leaking wheel cylinder(s), leaking caliper piston(s),
or loose/damaged/disconnected brake lines — inspect the entire system and correct.

**52. Brake pedal pulsates during brake application:** (1) Caliper improperly installed —
remove and inspect (Ch. 9). (2) Disc or drum defective — remove (Ch. 9), check for
excessive lateral runout, and either resurface or replace with a new one.

**53. Vehicle pulls to one side (general):** (1) Tire pressures uneven (Ch. 1). (2)
Defective tire (Ch. 1). (3) Excessive wear in suspension or steering components (Ch. 10).
(4) Front end in need of alignment. (5) Front brakes dragging — inspect brakes (Ch. 9).

**54. Shimmy, shake or vibration:** (1) Tire or wheel out-of-balance or out-of-round —
have professionally balanced. (2) Loose, worn or out-of-adjustment rear wheel bearings
(Ch. 1). (3) Shock absorbers and/or suspension components worn or damaged (Ch. 10).

**55. Excessive pitching and/or rolling around corners or during braking:** (1) Defective
shock absorbers — replace as a set (Ch. 10). (2) Broken or weak springs and/or suspension
components — inspect (Ch. 10).

**56. Excessively stiff steering:** (1) Lack of fluid in power steering fluid reservoir
(Ch. 1). (2) Incorrect tire pressures (Ch. 1). (3) Front end out of alignment. (4)
Suspension or steering components excessively worn (Ch. 10).

**57. Excessive play in steering:** (1) Excessive wear in suspension or steering
components (Ch. 10). (2) Steering gearbox damaged (Ch. 10).

**58. Lack of power assistance:** (1) Steering pump drivebelt faulty or not adjusted
properly (Ch. 1). (2) Fluid level low (Ch. 1).

**59. Excessive tire wear (not specific to one area):** (1) Incorrect tire pressures (Ch.
1). (2) Tires out-of-balance — have professionally balanced. (3) Wheels damaged — inspect
and replace as necessary.

**60. Excessive tire wear on outside edge:** (1) Inflation pressures incorrect (Ch. 1).
(2) Excessive speed in turns. (3) Front end alignment incorrect (excessive toe-in) — have
professionally aligned.

**61. Excessive tire wear on inside edge:** (1) Inflation pressures incorrect (Ch. 1). (2)
Front end alignment incorrect — have professionally aligned. (3) Loose or damaged
steering components (Ch. 10).

**62. Tire tread worn in one place:** (1) Tires out-of-balance. (2) Damaged or buckled
wheel — inspect and replace if necessary. (3) Defective tire (Ch. 1).

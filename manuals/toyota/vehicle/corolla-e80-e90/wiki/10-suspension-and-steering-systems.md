## 1. General information (PDF p.215-216) <a id="p215"></a>

The front suspension is a Macpherson strut design — the upper end of each strut attaches to
the vehicle body's strut support; the lower end connects to the upper end of the steering
knuckle. The steering knuckle attaches to a balljoint mounted on the outer end of the
suspension control arm. A stabilizer bar connects the control arms to minimize body lean
during cornering (illustration 1.1: 1 control arm, 2 tie-rod, 3 balljoint, 4 center beam).

The rear suspension also uses Macpherson struts — the upper end of each strut attaches to the
body via a strut rod; the lower end attaches to a knuckle, located by a pair of suspension
arms on each side and a longitudinally-mounted strut rod between the body and each knuckle.

The rack-and-pinion steering gear is behind the engine/transaxle assembly on the firewall and
actuates the tie-rods, attached to the steering knuckles. Most vehicles have power steering.
The steering column is designed to collapse in the event of an accident.

*Frozen fastener advice (as printed): underside fasteners subjected to water/road grime/mud
can become rusted or "frozen." Use lots of penetrating oil and let it soak in; a wire brush
on exposed threads also helps. A hammer and punch can break the bond between nut and bolt
threads (be careful the punch doesn't slip and ruin the threads). Heating with a torch
sometimes helps but isn't recommended given fire risk. Long breaker bars and extension
("cheater") pipes increase leverage — never use an extension pipe on a ratchet, it could
damage the ratcheting mechanism. Sometimes tightening the nut/bolt first helps break it
loose. Fasteners requiring drastic removal measures should always be replaced with new
ones.*

Since most procedures involve jacking up the vehicle and working underneath, good jackstands
are needed; a hydraulic floor jack is preferred for lifting and can also support components
during operations.

> **WARNING** — never, under any circumstances, rely on a jack to support the vehicle while
> working on it. Whenever any suspension or steering fastener is loosened/removed, it must
> be inspected and, if necessary, replaced with a new one of the same part number or
> original-equipment quality/design. Torque specifications must be followed for proper
> reassembly and component retention. Never attempt to heat or straighten any suspension or
> steering component — instead, replace any bent or damaged part with a new one.

## 2. Stabilizer bar (front) - removal and installation (PDF p.216-217) <a id="p216"></a>

Raise the front and support securely on jackstands (block the vehicle to keep it from rolling
off the stands). Detach the stabilizer bar retainer or link from the control arms. Detach
both stabilizer bar brackets from the vehicle floor pan. Disconnect the exhaust pipe from the
exhaust manifold (Chapter 4). Remove the stabilizer bar. While it's off, slide the bracket
bushings off and inspect them — replace if cracked/worn/deteriorated. Inspect the link
bushings too, replace if necessary. Clean the bushing area of the stabilizer bar with a stiff
wire brush to remove rust/dirt. *Note: do not remove any factory paint marks from the
stabilizer bar while cleaning.* Lubricate the inside/outside of the new bushings with
vegetable oil (cooking oil) to simplify reassembly.

> *Caution: don't use petroleum or mineral-based lubricants or brake fluid on the bushings —
> they will cause deterioration.*

Installation is the reverse of removal. On 1988 and later models, align the bushings with the
paint marks on the stabilizer bar, bushing arrows at the bottom pointing forward (illustration
2.9). Be sure to tighten the exhaust pipe-to-exhaust manifold nuts, the stabilizer bar
bracket bolts, and the stabilizer bar-to-control arm retainer nuts or stabilizer link nuts to
the Specifications torque.

**Front suspension exploded view** (illustration 2.2, labeled): stabilizer bar bracket,
stabilizer bar, cushion, retainer, suspension support, dust seal, spring seat, upper
insulator, retainer, cushion, coil spring, spring bumper, lower insulator, suspension
crossmember, stabilizer bar link, shock absorber (4A-GE engine variant shown; lower arm
front bolt, lower arm, rear bracket labeled separately; non-reusable parts marked).

## 3. Strut assembly (front) - removal, inspection and installation (PDF p.217-219) <a id="p217"></a>

**Removal** (illustrations 3.3, 3.5, 3.6, 3.7, 3.8): loosen the wheel lug nuts, raise and
support the vehicle securely on jackstands, remove the wheel. Detach the brake hose banjo
fitting from the caliper (Chapter 9) — have rags and a container for brake fluid. Detach the
brake hose clip from the strut bracket (illustration 3.3), pull the hose through the opening
in the strut bracket, tape or cap the hose and caliper to keep out dirt. Remove the brake
caliper (Chapter 9) and hang it from the strut spring with wire — not the brake hose; don't
disconnect the hose from the caliper.

If working on a 1984-1987 model, mark the strut's relationship to the camber adjusting cam
and steering knuckle (illustration 3.5). Remove the strut-to-knuckle nuts and knock the bolts
out with a hammer and punch (illustration 3.6). On 1984-1987 models, remove the camber
adjusting cam. Separate the strut from the steering knuckle (illustration 3.7) — careful not
to damage the outer CV joint boot, and don't over-extend the inner CV joint (one way to
prevent this: wire the top of the steering knuckle to the body). Mark the relationship of the
upper strut nuts to the body, remove them, then detach the strut assembly from the vehicle
(illustration 3.8: the upper end is fastened to the shock tower with three or four nuts — a
plastic plug fills the extra hole on three-nut models).

**Inspection:** check the strut body for leaking fluid, dents, cracks, other damage warranting
repair/replacement. Check the coil spring for chips/cracks in the coating (causes premature
spring failure from corrosion). Inspect the spring seat for damage, hardness, general
deterioration. If any undesirable condition exists, proceed to Section 4 for strut
disassembly.

**Installation:** guide the strut assembly up into the fender well and insert the upper
mounting studs through the shock tower holes — once they protrude, install the nuts so the
strut won't fall back through (an assistant helps — the strut is heavy and awkward). On
1984-1987 models, insert the camber adjuster into the upper hole in the steering knuckle
(illustration 3.13 — don't forget to install it). Slide the steering knuckle into the strut
flange, insert the two bolts, install the nuts, align the camber adjuster marks (1984-1987
models) and tighten the nuts to the Specifications torque. Install the brake caliper and
brake hose (Chapter 9). Install the wheel, lower the vehicle, tighten lug nuts to Chapter 1
Specifications. Tighten the three upper mounting nuts to the Specifications torque. Drive the
vehicle to an alignment shop to have front-end alignment checked and, if necessary, adjusted.

## 4. Strut/spring assembly - replacement (PDF p.218-220) <a id="p218"></a>

*Note (as printed): you'll need a spring compressor for this procedure — available on daily
rental at most auto parts stores or equipment yards. If the struts or coil springs show
telltale wear signs (leaking fluid, loss of damping, chipped/sagging/cracked coil springs),
explore all options first — the strut/shock absorber assemblies are not serviceable and must
be replaced if a problem develops. Complete strut assemblies with springs may be available on
an exchange basis, saving time/work — check cost/availability before disassembling.*

> **WARNING** — disassembling a strut assembly is potentially dangerous — utmost attention
> must be directed to the job at hand, or serious bodily injury may result. Use only a
> high-quality spring compressor and carefully follow the manufacturer's instructions
> furnished with the tool. After removing the coil spring from the strut assembly, set it
> aside in a safe, isolated area (a steel cabinet is preferred).

Remove the strut and spring assembly (Section 3 or Section 10). Mount the strut assembly in
a vise, jaws lined with wood or rags to prevent damage — don't overtighten the vise. Install
the spring compressor per the manufacturer's instructions (illustration 4.4) and compress the
spring until you can wiggle the strut (suspension) support. Loosen the damper shaft nut with
a socket wrench (illustration 4.5) — wedge a screwdriver or pry bar between one of the upper
mounting studs and the socket to prevent the suspension support and damper shaft from
turning. Remove the nut and suspension support (illustration 4.6) — inspect the bearing for
smooth operation, replace the suspension support if it doesn't turn smoothly; check the
rubber portion for cracking/deterioration, replace if the rubber shows any separation. Lift
the spring seat and upper insulator from the damper shaft (illustration 4.7) — check the
rubber spring seat for cracking/hardness, replace if necessary. Carefully lift the compressed
spring from the assembly (illustration 4.8) and set it in a safe place, such as a steel
cabinet.

> **WARNING** — keep the ends of the spring pointed away from your body! Never place your
> head near the end of the spring!

Slide the rubber bumper and dust boot off the damper shaft. Check the lower insulator for
wear/cracking/hardness, replace if necessary. Assemble the strut beginning with the dust boot
and rubber bumper — extend the damper rod as far as it will go and slide the boot/bumper down
to the strut body. If replacing the lower insulator, set it into position with the dropped
portion seated in the lowest part of the seat. Carefully place the coil spring onto the lower
insulator, spring end resting in the lowest part of the insulator (illustration 4.13 — make
sure the end fits into the recessed portion of the lower seat). Install the upper insulator
and spring seat, making sure the flats in the seat hole match the flats on the damper shaft
(illustration 4.14a) — also confirm the "OUT" marking on the spring seat faces toward the
lower bracket where the steering knuckle fits (illustration 4.14b). Install the dust seal and
suspension support onto the damper shaft. Install the nut and tighten to the specified
torque. Install the strut/shock absorber and coil spring assembly (Section 3).

## 5. Control arm (front) - removal, inspection and installation (PDF p.220-221) <a id="p220"></a>

**Removal** (illustrations 5.3, 5.4a, 5.4b, 5.4c, 5.5a, 5.5b): loosen the wheel lug nuts,
raise the front, support securely on jackstands, remove the wheel. Detach the stabilizer bar
if equipped (Section 2). Remove the balljoint-to-control arm bolt and nuts (illustration 5.3).
On all but the left-side control arm on 1988-and-later models with automatic transaxles,
remove the control arm front and rear mounting bolts (illustrations 5.4a — nut and
through-bolt; 5.4b — bracket bolts at the rear pivot, 1987 and earlier). Remove the control
arm.

If removing the left-side control arm on a 1988-or-later automatic-transaxle model: support
the suspension crossmember with a jack, unbolt the crossmember (illustration 5.5a), lower the
crossmember and control arms and take them out from beneath the vehicle, remove the pivot
bolt (illustration 5.5b) to separate the control arm from the crossmember.

**Inspection** (illustrations 5.7, 5.9): check the control arm for distortion and the
bushings for wear/damage/deterioration — replace a damaged/bent control arm with a new one.
If the pivot bushing is worn/deteriorated, note its installed position before removal
(illustration 5.7). Secure the control arm in a vise with padded jaws. Remove the bushing
nut, retainer, and bushing (illustration 5.9). Install the new bushing and retainer, tighten
the nut to the Specifications torque.

**Installation:** install the control arm pivot and bracket bolts, tighten slightly. If
installing the left-side control arm on a 1988-or-later automatic-transaxle model: attach the
control arm to the crossmember with the pivot bolt, tighten slightly; raise the crossmember
and bolt it to the body, tightening to the Specifications torque. Attach the control arm to
the steering knuckle, tighten the bolts/nuts to the Specifications torque. Attach the
stabilizer bar to the control arm (Section 2). Install the wheels, remove jackstands, lower
the vehicle, tighten the lug nuts to Chapter 1 Specifications. Bounce the car several times to
settle the suspension, then tighten the control arm pivot and bracket bolts to the
Specifications torque. Have the front wheel alignment checked and, if necessary, adjusted.

## 6. Balljoint (front) - replacement (PDF p.221) <a id="p221"></a>

Loosen the wheel lug nuts, raise and support securely on jackstands, remove the wheel. Remove
the cotter pin and loosen the castellated nut on the balljoint stud a few turns. Separate the
control arm from the knuckle (Section 5). Using a picklefork or a puller, separate the
balljoint stud from the knuckle. Remove the nut and detach the balljoint from the knuckle.
Installation is the reverse of removal — tighten the castellated nut and the
balljoint-to-control arm nuts to the Specifications torque.

## 7. Steering knuckle and hub - removal and installation (PDF p.221-222) <a id="p221"></a>

> **WARNING** — dust from the brake system may contain asbestos, harmful to your health.
> Never blow it out with compressed air and don't inhale it. Do not, under any circumstances,
> use petroleum-based solvents to clean brake parts — use brake cleaner or denatured alcohol
> only.

Loosen the hub nut (Chapter 8). Loosen the wheel lug nuts, raise and support securely on
jackstands, remove the wheel. Remove the brake caliper and support it with wire. Separate the
brake disc from the hub (Chapter 9). On 1984-1987 models, mark the relationship of the strut
to the steering knuckle and camber adjuster (Section 3). Separate the tie-rod end from the
steering knuckle arm (Section 18). Remove the strut-to-knuckle bolts (Section 3). Remove the
balljoint-to-control arm bolt and nuts (Section 5). Push the driveaxle from the hub (Chapter
8) — support the driveaxle end with wire. Carefully separate the steering knuckle from the
strut and balljoint. Separate the balljoint from the steering knuckle and hub assembly
(Section 6). Installation is the reverse of removal — tighten all suspension fasteners to
this Chapter's Specifications; tighten the hub nut to the Chapter 8 Specifications.

## 9. Stabilizer bar (rear) - removal and installation (PDF p.222) <a id="p222"></a>

Raise the vehicle and support securely on jackstands. Disconnect the stabilizer bar link from
the suspension control arm (illustration 9.2) — note the order of the bushings and spacers.
Remove the stabilizer bar bracket-to-body bolts, remove the brackets, remove the bar. Check
the bracket bushings for cracking/hardness/deterioration; check the link bushings for wear.
Clean bushing-contact areas with a wire brush. Replace worn parts as necessary. Installation
is the reverse of removal — a light coat of vegetable oil eases bushing/U-bracket
installation (don't use petroleum-based lubricants or brake fluid — they cause premature
bushing failure).

**Typical rear stabilizer bar components** (illustration 9.2 exploded view): 1 retainers, 2
cushions, 3 stabilizer link, 4 bushing, 5 collar, 6 stabilizer bar, 7 bushing, 8 bracket.

## 11. Suspension arms (rear) - removal and installation (PDF p.224-225) <a id="p224"></a>

Raise the rear, support securely on jackstands, block the front wheels. Remove the suspension
arm-to-spindle bolt and nut — use a back-up wrench on the nut (illustration 11.2). If one of
the rear (No. 2) suspension arms is being removed, mark the relationship of the toe adjuster
cam to the suspension arm mounting bracket (illustration 11.3 — this ensures the toe
adjustment returns to the same setting). Remove the suspension arm-to-body mounting bolt and
nut while supporting the suspension arm (illustration 11.4). Remove the control arm from the
vehicle.

Install the control arm and tighten its fasteners slightly. Install the wheels, lower the
vehicle, tighten the wheel nuts to Chapter 1 Specifications. Bounce the vehicle several times
to settle the suspension, then tighten the suspension fasteners to this Chapter's
Specifications. Have the rear wheel alignment checked by a dealer or alignment shop.

## 14. Axle carrier (rear) - removal and installation (PDF p.226-227) <a id="p226"></a>

## 15. Steering system - general information (PDF p.227) <a id="p227"></a>

All models have rack-and-pinion steering, some power-assisted. The rack-and-pinion assembly
bolts to the firewall and operates the steering arms via tie-rods. The tie-rod inner ends are
protected by rubber boots — inspect periodically for secure attachment, tears, and leaking
lubricant. The power assist system consists of a belt-driven pump and associated lines/
hoses — check the reservoir fluid level periodically (Chapter 1). The steering wheel operates
the steering shaft, which actuates the steering gear through universal joints. Looseness in
the steering can result from wear in the steering shaft universal joints, the steering gear,
the tie-rod ends, or loose retaining bolts.

## 17. Steering gear - removal and installation (PDF p.227-229) <a id="p227"></a>

## 18. Tie-rod ends - removal and installation (PDF p.228-229) <a id="p228"></a>

**Removal** (illustrations 18.2, 18.4): loosen the wheel lug nuts, raise the front, support
securely, block the rear wheels, set the parking brake, remove the front wheel. Loosen the
jam nut enough to mark the position of the tie-rod end relative to the threads (illustration
18.2). Remove the cotter pin and loosen the nut on the tie-rod end stud. Disconnect the
tie-rod from the steering knuckle arm with a puller (illustration 18.4 — a two-jaw puller
works well; note the nut isn't removed first, to prevent the two components separating
violently). Remove the nut and detach the tie-rod end. Unscrew the tie-rod end from the tie-
rod.

**Installation:** thread the tie-rod end onto the marked position, insert the stud into the
steering knuckle arm, tighten the jam nut securely. Install the castellated nut on the stud
and tighten to this Chapter's Specifications, install a new cotter pin. Install the wheel and
lug nuts, lower the vehicle, tighten to Chapter 1 Specifications. Have the alignment checked
by a dealer or alignment shop.

## 19. Steering gear boots - replacement (PDF p.229) <a id="p229"></a>

Loosen the lug nuts, raise and support securely on jackstands, remove the wheel. Remove the

## 20. Power steering pump - removal and installation (PDF p.229-230) <a id="p229"></a>

**Removal** (illustrations 20.4, 20.5a, 20.5b): disconnect the cable from the negative
battery terminal. On some models, the air cleaner assembly obstructs removal — remove it if
so (Chapter 4). Using a large syringe or suction gun, suck as much fluid out of the power
steering reservoir as possible; place a drain pan under the vehicle for spillage when the
hoses are disconnected. Loosen the clamp and disconnect the fluid return hose from the pump
(illustration 20.4). Remove the adjuster bolt or loosen the idler pulley nut and adjusting
bolt (illustrations 20.5a — models with 4A-C and 4A-FE engines, 20.5b). *Note: on some models
it may be necessary to remove the pump pulley to remove the adjuster bolt — hold the pump
pulley with the drivebelt and loosen the pulley nut.* Loosen the right front wheel lug nuts,
raise and support securely on jackstands, remove the right front wheel.

On some models, it may be necessary to remove the bolt from the right engine mount (Chapter
2A), place a padded jack below the engine, and raise the engine about two inches to access the
lower power steering pump through-bolt.

> **WARNING** — don't place any part of your body under the engine when it's supported only
> by a jack!

Remove the pressure line-to-pump banjo bolt or flare-nut fitting and separate the line from
the pump — discard the sealing washers on each side of the banjo fitting and replace them.
Remove the through-bolt and remove the pump.

**Installation:** reverse of removal. Adjust the drivebelt tension (Chapter 1). Top up the
reservoir fluid level and bleed the system (Section 21).

## 21. Power steering system - bleeding (PDF p.230) <a id="p230"></a>

Following any operation in which the power steering fluid lines were disconnected, the system
must be bled to remove air and obtain proper steering performance. With the front wheels
straight ahead, check the fluid level and, if low, add fluid to the Cold mark on the
dipstick. Start the engine, let it run at fast idle (below 1,000 rpm), recheck and top up to
the Cold mark if necessary. Bleed by turning the wheels side to side without hitting the
stops — hold the steering wheel 2-3 seconds in each direction to work air out of the system,
keeping the reservoir full while doing this. When the air is worked out, return the wheels to
straight-ahead and leave the vehicle running for several more minutes before shutting off.
Check the fluid level with the engine running, then after shutdown — the level should not
rise more than 7/32 in (5.5 mm); if it does, repeat the bleeding step. Road test the vehicle
to confirm the steering functions normally and noise-free. Recheck the fluid level to be sure
it's up to the Hot mark on the dipstick while the engine is at normal operating temperature.

## 22. Wheels and tires - general information (PDF p.230-231) <a id="p230"></a>

All vehicles use metric-sized fiberglass- or steel-belted radial tires (illustration 22.1:
metric tire size code diagram — example "P185/80R13": tire type P=passenger/T=temporary/
C=commercial, section width in millimeters, aspect ratio (section height), construction type
R=radial/B=bias-belted/D=diagonal (bias), rim diameter in inches). Using other tire size/type
may affect ride and handling. Don't mix different tire types (e.g. radials and bias-belted)
on the same vehicle — handling may be seriously affected. Tires should be replaced in pairs
on the same axle; if replacing only one, be sure it matches size, structure, and tread design.

Tire pressure has a substantial effect on handling and wear — check monthly or before
extended trips (Chapter 1). Wheels must be replaced if bent, dented, leaking air, having
elongated bolt holes, heavily rusted, out of vertical symmetry, or if the lug nuts won't stay
tight — welding or peening wheel repairs are not recommended. Tire and wheel balance affects
handling, braking, and performance — whenever a tire is installed on a wheel, the tire and
wheel should be balanced by a shop with the proper equipment.

## 23. Wheel alignment - general information (PDF p.231) <a id="p231"></a>

A wheel alignment refers to adjustments made so the wheels are in proper angular relationship
to the suspension and ground. Misaligned wheels affect vehicle control and increase tire
wear. The front-end angles normally measured are camber, caster, and toe-in (illustration
23.1). Camber and toe-in are adjustable on 1984-1987 models; toe-in is the only adjustable
angle on 1988 and later models. The only rear adjustment possible is toe-in — other rear
angles should be measured to check for bent or worn suspension parts.

*Note (as printed): getting proper wheel alignment is an exacting process requiring
complicated, expensive machines — have a technician with proper equipment perform this work.
This section gives a basic idea of what's involved so you can understand the process and deal
intelligently with the shop doing the work.*

**Toe-in** is the turning-in of the wheels — its purpose is to ensure parallel rolling. In a
vehicle with zero toe-in, the distance between the front edges of the wheels equals the
distance between the rear edges. The actual amount is normally only a fraction of an inch. On
the front end, toe-in is controlled by the tie-rod end position on the tie-rod; on the rear,
by a cam on the inner end of the rear (No. 2) suspension arm. Incorrect toe-in causes
improper tire wear by making the tires scrub against the road surface.

**Camber** is the tilting of the wheels from vertical when viewed from one end of the
vehicle — tilting out at the top is positive camber, tilting in at the top is negative
camber. The amount of tilt, measured in degrees from vertical, is the camber angle — it
affects tire tread contact and compensates for suspension geometry changes during cornering
or over undulating surfaces.

**Caster** is the tilting of the front steering axis from vertical — a tilt toward the rear
is positive caster, toward the front is negative caster.

## 1. General information (PDF p.112-113) <a id="p112"></a>

**Engine cooling system:** all vehicles use a pressurized system with thermostatically
controlled coolant circulation. An impeller-type water pump on the drivebelt end of the
block pumps coolant through the engine, around each cylinder, toward the transaxle end.
Cast-in passages direct coolant around the intake/exhaust ports, near the spark plug areas,
and close to the exhaust valve guides. A wax-pellet thermostat sits in a housing near the
transaxle end. During warm-up, the closed thermostat blocks radiator circulation; as the
engine nears operating temperature, it opens to let hot coolant reach the radiator. The
system is sealed by a pressure-type radiator cap, raising the boiling point and increasing
radiator cooling efficiency — excess pressure forces the spring-loaded valve off its seat,
venting through the overflow tube into a coolant reservoir; as the system cools, coolant is
drawn back in automatically. The reservoir doubles as both the fresh-coolant fill point and
the overflow holding tank — this is a "closed" design because escaped coolant is saved and
reused.

**Heating system:** blower fan and heater core in the heater box, hoses connecting the core
to the engine cooling system, and the heater/A-C control head on the dashboard. Hot engine
coolant circulates through the core; when heat mode is activated, a flap door opens to
expose the heater box to the cabin, and a fan switch on the control head activates the
blower motor, forcing air through the core.

**Air conditioning system:** condenser (front of the radiator), evaporator (adjacent to the
heater core), compressor (engine-mounted), a filter-drier (contains a high-pressure relief
valve), and connecting plumbing. A blower fan forces cabin air through the evaporator core
(a "radiator in reverse"), transferring heat from the air to the refrigerant — the liquid
refrigerant boils off into low-pressure vapor, taking the heat with it as it leaves the
evaporator.

## 2. Antifreeze - general information (PDF p.113) <a id="p113"></a>

> **WARNING** — do not allow antifreeze to contact skin or painted surfaces — rinse spills
> immediately with plenty of water. Antifreeze is highly toxic if ingested — never leave it
> in an open container or puddle; check with local authorities on disposal.

Fill with a water/ethylene-glycol antifreeze solution, protecting to at least -20°F (lower
if the local climate requires); it also protects against corrosion and raises the coolant
boiling point. Drain/flush/refill at the specified intervals (Chapter 1) — old or
contaminated solutions cause damage and encourage corrosion/scale; use distilled water with
the antifreeze. Check all hose connections before adding antifreeze (it leaks through very
minute openings). Engines don't normally consume coolant — if the level drops, find and fix
the cause. Mixture should be at least 50% antifreeze but never more than 70% — consult the
ratio chart on the antifreeze container. Hydrometers (available at auto parts stores) test
the coolant. Use antifreeze meeting the vehicle manufacturer's specifications.

## 3. Thermostat - check and replacement (PDF p.113-115) <a id="p113"></a>

> **WARNING** — do not remove the radiator cap, drain the coolant, or replace the thermostat
> until the engine has cooled completely.

**Check:** before blaming the thermostat, check coolant level, drivebelt tension (Chapter
1), and temperature gauge operation. If the engine takes a long time to warm up (heater
output or gauge), the thermostat is probably stuck open — replace it. If the engine runs
hot, feel the upper radiator hose — if it isn't hot but the engine is, the thermostat is
probably stuck closed, trapping coolant in the engine — replace it. *Caution: don't drive
the vehicle without a thermostat — lack of one causes coolant to bypass the radiator, and
the computer may stay in open loop, hurting emissions and fuel economy.* If the upper
radiator hose is hot, coolant is flowing and the thermostat is open — consult the
Troubleshooting section at the front of the manual for other causes.

**Replacement** (illustrations 3.10, 3.11a, 3.11b, 3.13, 3.14): disconnect the negative
battery cable. Drain the cooling system (Chapter 1) — save/reuse the coolant if relatively
new/in good condition. Follow the lower radiator hose to the engine to locate the
thermostat housing. Loosen the hose clamp, detach the hose from the fitting (grasp near the
end with adjustable pliers and twist to break the seal if stuck; cut off and replace if old/
deteriorated). *Note: if the outer surface of the large hose-mating fitting is
deteriorated, hose removal may damage it further — the thermostat housing cover will need
replacement if so.* Unplug the engine cooling fan switch connector (illustration 3.10: 1
upper radiator hose, 2 cooling fan switch connector, 3 mounting bolts/nuts). Remove the
bolts/nuts and detach the housing cover — tap with a soft-face hammer if stuck; be prepared
for coolant spillage as the gasket seal breaks. Note the installed orientation (which end
faces the engine), then remove the thermostat. Thoroughly clean the sealing surfaces.

Install a new gasket over the thermostat (illustration 3.13). Install the new thermostat
(illustration 3.11b — 1 thermostat, 2 gasket, 3 water housing, 4 retaining nut) with the air
bleed facing up and the spring end directed into the engine (illustration 3.14: the air
bleed valve must be near the top — 1 air bleed valve, 2 cylinder head, 3 thermostat).
Install the cover and bolts, tighten to Specifications torque. Reattach the hose to the
fitting and tighten the clamp securely. Refill the cooling system (Chapter 1). Start the
engine, let it reach normal operating temperature, check for leaks and proper thermostat
operation (Steps 2-4 above).

## 4. Engine cooling fan - check and replacement (PDF p.114-115) <a id="p114"></a>

**Check:** the cooling fan is controlled by a temperature switch mounted in the thermostat
cover — when coolant reaches a predetermined temperature, the switch opens the ground return
for the fan motor relay, completing the circuit. First check the fuses (Chapter 12). To
test the fan motor, unplug the motor connector and use fused jumper wires to connect the fan
directly to the battery (illustration 4.3) — if it still doesn't work, replace the motor. If
the motor tested OK, the fault is in the coolant temperature switch, the relay, or the
wiring harness (Chapter 12). Test the temperature switch by unplugging the connector
(illustration 4.5: 1 connector, 2 temperature switch) with the ignition switch on. If the fan
doesn't operate, check the wiring (Chapter 12).

**Replacement:** unplug the wires, detach the hoses, unbolt the fan shroud (illustrations
4.7a — 1 hose, 2 mounting bolt upper; 4.7b HD option — 1 mounting bolts upper, 2 electric
connector, 3 hose) — remove the lower shroud bolts too. *Note: drain the coolant (Chapter 1)
before removing the upper radiator hose.* Remove the nut and detach the fan blade assembly
from the motor shaft. Take out the screws holding the fan motor to the bracket and detach
the motor. Installation is the reverse of removal. *Note: A/C-equipped vehicles have an
additional fan on the right side of the radiator — check per Steps 2, 3, 6 above; replace
per Steps 7-10.*

**Cooling fan components** (illustration 4.8 exploded view): 1 radiator, 2 fan retaining
nut, 3 cooling fan, 4 shroud, 5 shroud retaining bolt, 6 cooling fan motor, 7 motor
retaining bolt.

## 5. Radiator - removal and installation (PDF p.115-116) <a id="p115"></a>

> **WARNING** — wait until the engine is completely cool before beginning this procedure.

**Removal:** disconnect the negative battery cable. Raise the front and support securely on
jackstands. Remove the lower splash pans. Drain the cooling system (Chapter 1) — save/reuse
if new/good condition. Loosen the hose clamps, detach the radiator hoses (grasp near the
end with adjustable pliers and twist to break the seal if stuck — careful not to damage the
radiator fittings; cut off and replace old/deteriorated hoses). Disconnect the coolant
reservoir hose from the radiator filler neck. Remove the cooling fan(s) (Section 4). On
automatic-transaxle vehicles, disconnect the cooler lines (illustration 5.7 — bottom of the
radiator, viewed from below) and plug the lines/fittings. Remove the radiator mounting bolts
(illustration 5.8 — bolt and upper mounting bracket from each side) and lift out the
radiator — don't spill coolant.

**Installation:** reverse of removal — confirm the rubber cushions are seated properly and
the air space "A" isn't compressed (illustration 5.12). Fill with the proper
antifreeze/water mixture (Chapter 1). Start the engine, check for leaks; let it reach normal
operating temperature (upper hose becomes hot), recheck coolant level. On automatic
transaxle vehicles, check/add fluid as needed.

With the radiator removed, it can be inspected for leaks/damage — a radiator shop or dealer
handles repair (special techniques required). Bugs/dirt can be cleaned off with a garden
hose or soft brush — don't bend the cooling fins.

## 7. Water pump - check (PDF p.116) <a id="p116"></a>

A failed water pump can cause serious overheating damage — replace with new/rebuilt if
defective. Three checks while installed: (1) with the engine running at normal operating
temperature, squeeze the upper radiator hose — a pressure surge should be felt as it's
released if the pump is working properly.

> **WARNING** — keep your hands away from the fan blades!

(2) Water pumps have a weep/vent hole — a failed seal leaks coolant from it; a flashlight
usually helps spot it from underneath (illustration 7.4). (3) If the shaft bearings fail,
there may be a howling sound at the drivebelt end while running — don't confuse this with
drivebelt slippage (a squealing sound). Shaft wear can be felt by rocking the water pump
pulley up and down (illustration 7.5) — don't mistake loose pulley bolts for a worn shaft.

## 8. Water pump - removal and installation (PDF p.116-118) <a id="p116"></a>

> **WARNING** — wait until the engine is completely cool before beginning this procedure.

**4A-C and 4A-GE engines:** disconnect the negative battery cable. Drain the cooling system
(Chapter 1) — save/reuse if good condition. Loosen the water pump pulley bolts (illustration
8.3 — push on the drivebelt to keep the pulley from turning). Remove the alternator (and
power steering drivebelts if equipped) per Chapter 1; remove the pulley at the end of the
water pump shaft. *Note: on some models the pulley may not have sufficient clearance for
removal — follow the 4A-F/4A-FE engine-jacking procedure below instead.* Remove the power
steering pump adjustment bracket if equipped and move the pump aside. Set the parking brake,
block the rear wheels, raise and support the front securely on jackstands. Remove the lower
splash pans. Detach the coolant hose(s) from the coolant inlet pipe and unbolt the pipe
(illustration 8.7: 1 pipe retaining brace, 2 water inlet pipe, 3 pipe retaining nuts). On
4A-C engines, remove the upper timing belt cover; on 4A-GE, remove the lower and middle
timing belt covers. Remove the mounting bolt for the dipstick tube and pull the tube out —
plug the hole to keep dirt/coolant out of the oil. Remove the bolts (illustration 8.9) and
detach the pump from the engine — note the lengths and diameters of the various bolts as
removed, for correct reinstallation.

Clean the bolt threads and threaded holes to remove corrosion/sealant. Compare the new pump
to the old to confirm they're identical. Remove all traces of old O-rings and gasket
material from the sealing surfaces. Install new O-rings on the dipstick tube, in the engine
block (illustration 8.14: 1 engine, 2 O-ring), and behind the water pump — a dab of RTV
sealant holds the pump O-rings in place; wet the dipstick O-ring with engine oil. Carefully
mate the pump to the engine; slip a couple of bolts through the mounting holes to hold the
O-rings in place. Install the remaining bolts, tighten to Specifications torque in 1/4-turn
increments — don't overtighten or the pump may be damaged. Reinstall all parts removed for
access. Refill the cooling system, check drivebelt tension (Chapter 1). Run the engine and
check for leaks.

**4A-F, 4A-FE engines:** disconnect the negative battery cable. Drain the cooling system —
save/reuse if good condition. Set the parking brake, block the rear wheels, raise and
support the front on jackstands. Remove the lower splash pans. Remove the two nuts from the
rear engine mount. Remove the coolant reservoir (Section 6). Remove the cruise control
module and bracket if equipped. Support the engine with a floor jack, block of wood on the
jack pad to protect the oil pan. Remove the right engine mount through-bolt. Raise the
engine slightly for access to the water pump pulley bolts. Loosen the water pump pulley
bolts (illustration 8.10). Remove the alternator drivebelt (and power steering belt if
equipped). Lower the engine to its normal level. Remove the upper timing belt covers. Follow
Steps 9-18 from the 4A-C/4A-GE procedure above to complete. *Note: on some 4A-F/4A-FE
vehicles, it may be necessary to remove the cooling fan (Section 4).*

**Water pump components** (illustration 8.10 exploded view): 1 water pump pulley (without
power steering), 2 O-rings, 3 water pump assembly, 4 water pump pulley (with power
steering).

## 9. Coolant temperature sending unit - check and replacement (PDF p.117) <a id="p117"></a>

> **WARNING** — the engine must be completely cool before removing the sending unit.

**Check:** if the gauge is inoperative, check the fuses first (Chapter 12). If the indicator
shows excessive temperature after running a while, see the Troubleshooting section at the
front of the manual. If the gauge indicates Hot shortly after a cold start, disconnect the
wire at the sending unit (illustration 9.3 — located in the cylinder head at the drivebelt
end, one wire) — if the reading drops, replace the sending unit; if it stays high, the wire
to the gauge may be shorted to ground or the gauge is faulty. If the gauge fails to indicate
after the engine has been warmed up (~10 minutes) and the fuses check out okay, shut off the
engine, disconnect the wire at the sending unit, and using a jumper wire connect it to a
3.4-watt bulb, connecting the bulb to a clean ground on the engine. *Caution: the bulb
provides electrical resistance to protect the gauge — don't connect the wire directly to
ground.* Turn on the ignition without starting the engine — if the gauge now indicates Hot,
replace the sending unit. If the gauge still doesn't work, the circuit may be open or the
gauge faulty (Chapter 12).

**Replacement:** with the engine completely cool, remove the radiator cap to release
pressure, then reinstall it (reduces coolant loss during replacement). Disconnect the
electrical connector from the sending unit. Prepare the new unit with Teflon tape or a light
sealant coat on the threads. Unscrew the old unit and quickly install the new one to
minimize coolant loss. Tighten securely and plug in the connector. Refill the cooling
system, run the engine, check for leaks and proper gauge operation.

## 10. Blower motor and circuit - check (PDF p.117-119) <a id="p117"></a>

**Blower motor resistor** (illustrations 10.1, 10.2): locate the resistor on the blower
motor. Connect an ohmmeter from terminal 1 to terminals 2, 3, and 4 in turn — there should be
resistance in each case; zero or infinite resistance in any case means replace the resistor.

**Heater switch** (illustrations 10.4a, 10.4b): disconnect the negative battery cable. The
continuity diagrams show which terminals should show continuity in a given switch position
(example given: on early models in position III, the ohmmeter should show continuity between
terminals 2, 3, and 6). Check continuity of all terminals shown in the diagrams — replace
the switch if not as specified.

**Heater relay test** (illustration 10.7): locate the relay on the passenger-side kick panel
and remove it. Connect an ohmmeter between relay terminals 1 and 3, then 2 and 4 — there
should be continuity in both cases. Connect the ohmmeter between terminals 4 and 5 — there
should NOT be continuity. Apply battery voltage to terminals 1 and 3 with jumper wires —
there should now be continuity between 4 and 5, but not between 2 and 4. Replace the relay if
it doesn't perform as described.

**Blower motor test:** unplug the electrical connector and, using jumper wires, connect a
12-volt battery to the motor terminals — the motor should run; replace it if not.

## 12. Heater core - removal and installation (PDF p.120-121) <a id="p120"></a>

Disconnect the negative battery cable. Drain the cooling system (Chapter 1). Working in the
engine compartment, disconnect the heater hoses where they enter the firewall (illustration

Remove the screws and clips and separate the two halves of the heater assembly (illustration
12.6a — various clips/screws/wiring; 12.6b under-dash heater components exploded view: blower

Reassemble the heater unit and check the operation of the control flaps — correct any
binding before installation. Reinstall the remaining parts in reverse order. Refill the
cooling system, reconnect the battery, run the engine — check for leaks and proper system
operation.

## 13. Air conditioner and heater control assembly - removal, installation and cable adjustment (PDF p.121-122) <a id="p121"></a>

secure the clip (13.9h).

## 14. Air conditioning system - check and maintenance (PDF p.122-123) <a id="p122"></a>

> **WARNING** — the air conditioning system is under high pressure. Do not loosen any hose
> fittings or remove any components until after the system has been discharged by a dealer
> service department or service station. Always wear eye protection when disconnecting A/C
> system fittings.

*Note (as printed): given the complexity of the A/C system and special equipment needed for
service, in-depth troubleshooting and repairs aren't included in this manual (refer to the
Haynes Automotive Heating and Air Conditioning Manual) — but simple checks and component
replacement procedures are covered in this Chapter.*

**Check — regular maintenance:** (a) check the compressor drivebelt, replace if worn/
deteriorated (Chapter 1); (b) check drivebelt tension, adjust if necessary (Chapter 1); (c)
check system hoses for cracks/bubbles/hard spots/deterioration, inspect hoses/fittings for
oil bubbles/seepage — replace if worn/damaged/leaking; (d) inspect condenser fins for
leaves/bugs/debris — clean with a "fin comb" or compressed air; (e) confirm correct
refrigerant charge; (f) check the evaporator housing drain tube for blockage. It's a good
idea to run the system ~10 minutes at least once a month, particularly in winter — long-term
non-use can harden and eventually fail the seals.

The most common cause of poor cooling is simply a low refrigerant charge. If cool air output
noticeably drops, some quick checks help determine if the refrigerant level is low: warm the
engine to normal operating temperature; set the A/C temperature selector to coldest and the
blower to highest; open the doors (so the system doesn't cycle off as soon as it cools the
cabin). When the compressor engages, the clutch makes an audible click and the clutch center
rotates — inspect the sight glass (illustrations 14.7a — early models, under the coolant
reservoir, pull off the plastic cap; 14.7b — later models, between the grille and radiator;
14.7c — sight glass mounted in the top of the receiver-drier) — foamy refrigerant means it's
low; charge the system as described below.

**Adding refrigerant:** buy an automotive charging kit (14-oz refrigerant can, tap valve,
short hose section connecting the tap valve to the system low-side service valve). Since one
can may not be enough, buy an additional can — make sure the first can contains red
refrigerant dye, so a leak's location can be pinpointed by the dye leaking out with the
refrigerant.

> **WARNING** — never exceed the system's capacity when adding refrigerant.

Connect the charging kit per the manufacturer's instructions. On 1984-1987 carbureted models,
the charging port is on the right side of the engine compartment (illustration 14.9 — the
low-pressure port, identifiable by larger-diameter tubing than the high-pressure side); on
fuel-injected models, the port is on the firewall side.

> **WARNING** — DO NOT connect the charging kit hose to the system high side! Wear eye
> protection.

Warm up the engine and turn on the A/C. Keep the charging kit hose away from the fan and
other moving parts. Place a thermometer in the dashboard vent nearest the evaporator
(illustration 14.11) and add refrigerant until the indicated temperature is around 40 to
45°F.

## 15. Air conditioning receiver/drier - removal and installation (PDF p.123-124) <a id="p123"></a>

> **WARNING** — same A/C high-pressure precautions as Section 14.

**Removal:** the receiver/drier (reservoir and filter for the refrigerant) is in the left
front corner of the engine compartment — below the coolant reservoir on 1984-1987 models,
between the grille and radiator on 1988 and later models. On 1984-1987 models, remove the
coolant reservoir for access (Section 6); on 1988 and later, remove the grille (Chapter 11).
Unplug the electrical connector from the pressure switch (illustration 15.3). Detach the two
refrigerant lines from the receiver/drier. Immediately cap the open fittings to keep out
dirt/moisture. Unbolt the receiver/drier (illustrations 15.6a — early model, bracket bolts,
viewed from below; 15.6b — 1988 and later, receiver-drier/condenser/fan mounting details: 1
condenser fan, 2 condenser, 3 horn, 4 receiver/drier) and lift it out.

**Installation:** install new O-rings on the lines, lubricated with clean refrigerant oil.
Reverse of removal. *Note: don't remove the sealing caps until ready to reconnect the lines.
Don't mistake the inlet (marked IN) and outlet (marked OUT) connections.* If a new
receiver/drier is installed, add 0.7 US fluid ounces (20 cc) of refrigerant oil to the
system. Have the system evacuated, charged, and leak tested by the shop that discharged it.

## 16. Air conditioning compressor - removal and installation (PDF p.124-125) <a id="p124"></a>

> **WARNING** — same A/C high-pressure precautions as Section 14.

Disconnect the negative battery cable. Set the parking brake, block the rear tires. Raise
the front and support securely on jackstands. Unbolt the lower splash shield and remove the
compressor drivebelt (Chapter 1); detach the fan shroud if necessary. Disconnect the clutch
wire from the compressor. Detach the refrigerant lines from the back of the compressor
(illustration 16.6) and immediately cap the open fittings. Remove the mounting bolts
(illustrations 16.7a — 4A-C, 16.7b — 4A-GE, 16.7c — [4A-FE/4A-F] compressor mounting
details, exploded views) and lower the compressor from the engine compartment. *Note: keep
the compressor level during handling/storage. If the compressor seized or metal particles are
found in the refrigerant lines, the system must be flushed by an A/C technician and the
receiver/drier must be replaced.*

Prior to installation, turn the clutch center six times to disperse any oil collected in the
head. Install the compressor in reverse order of removal. If installing a new compressor,
refer to the manufacturer's instructions for adding refrigerant oil. Have the system
evacuated, charged, and leak tested by the shop that discharged it.

## 17. Air conditioning condenser - removal and installation (PDF p.125-126) <a id="p125"></a>

> **WARNING** — same A/C high-pressure precautions as Section 14.

Remove the grille (Chapter 11) and the bumper on 1988 and later models. Remove the hood
latch and vertical brace (illustration 17.2 — don't forget the bottom bolt). Remove the horn
if it obstructs condenser removal. On 4A-GE engines, remove the oil cooler without
disconnecting the lines. Remove the condenser fan. Disconnect the refrigerant lines from the
condenser — use a back-up wrench to avoid twisting the lines (illustrations 17.6a, 17.6b).
Immediately cap the open fittings. Unbolt the condenser (illustration 17.8) and lift it out —
store it upright to prevent oil loss. Installation is the reverse of removal. If a new
condenser was installed, add 1.4 to 1.7 ounces (40 to 50 cc) of refrigerant oil to the
system. Have the system evacuated, charged, and leak tested by the shop that discharged it.

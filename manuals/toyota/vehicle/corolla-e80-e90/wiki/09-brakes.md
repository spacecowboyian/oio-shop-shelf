## 1. General information (PDF p.197) <a id="p197"></a>

Hydraulically operated front and rear brakes — front brakes are disc type; rear brakes are
drum or disc type depending on the model. Both front and rear are self-adjusting: discs
compensate automatically for pad wear; drums have an adjustment mechanism activated when the
parking brake is applied.

**Hydraulic system:** two separate circuits — the master cylinder has separate reservoirs
for each, so a leak/failure in one circuit leaves the other operative. A dual proportioning
valve on the firewall balances front/rear brake pressure.

**Power brake booster:** uses engine manifold vacuum and atmospheric pressure to assist the
hydraulic brakes — mounted on the firewall in the engine compartment.

**Parking brake:** operates the rear brakes only, via cable actuation, activated by a lever
in the center console.

**Service:** after any operation involving brake system disassembly, always test drive to
check braking performance before resuming normal driving — test on a clean, dry, flat
surface (other conditions can give inaccurate results). Test at various speeds with both
light and heavy pedal pressure — the vehicle should stop evenly without pulling to either
side. Avoid locking the brakes — this slides the tires and diminishes braking efficiency and
vehicle control. Tires, vehicle load, and front-end alignment also affect braking
performance.

## 2. Disc brake pads - replacement (PDF p.197-198) <a id="p197"></a>

> **WARNING** — disc brake pads must be replaced on both front or both rear wheels at the
> same time — never replace pads on only one wheel. Dust created by the brake system may
> contain asbestos, harmful to your health. Never blow it out with compressed air and don't
> inhale it — wear an approved filtering mask when working on the brakes. Do not, under any
> circumstances, use petroleum-based solvents to clean brake parts — use brake cleaner or
> denatured alcohol only! When servicing disc brakes, use only high-quality, nationally
> recognized brand-name pads.

*Note: this procedure applies to both front and rear disc brakes.*

Remove the cap from the brake fluid reservoir. Loosen the wheel lug nuts, raise the front or
rear of the vehicle, support it securely on jackstands, block the wheels at the opposite
end. Remove the wheels — work on one brake assembly at a time, using the assembled brake for
reference. Inspect the brake disc carefully per Section 4 — if machining is necessary,
follow that Section to remove the disc, at which point the pads can also come off the
calipers.

On front calipers only, push the piston back into its bore to make room for the new pads —
a C-clamp works (illustration 2.5, one end on the flat area near the brake hose fitting, the
other pressing the outer pad). As the piston is depressed, fluid in the master cylinder will
rise — make sure it doesn't overflow; siphon off some fluid if necessary. Follow the
photo sequence beginning with illustration 2.6a for the actual pad replacement procedure,
staying in order and reading each caption:

- 2.6a: remove the one (rear caliper) or two (front caliper) mounting bolts.
- 2.6b: on front calipers, pull the caliper straight up and off the disc; on rear calipers,
  pivot the caliper up off the pads and support it in a raised position with a piece of wire
  attached to the underbody.
- 2.6c: once removed from the torque plate, hang the caliper from the coil spring with a
  piece of wire — **DON'T let it hang by the brake hose!**
- 2.6d: remove the anti-squeal shims from the pads (note their positioning — some models have
  only one shim per pad, others use two on the outer pad and one on the inner pad).
- 2.6e: remove the anti-rattle springs from the brake pads.
- 2.6f: to remove the pads, slide them to the side, then straight out of the torque plate.
- 2.6g: remove the pad support plates from the torque plate — replace them with new ones if
  distorted in any way.
- 2.6h: pry the wear indicators off the brake pads and transfer them to the new pads — replace
  them if bent.

On rear calipers, engage the piston notches with a special tool and rotate the piston
clockwise while pressing in (illustration 2.7) — you'll feel the piston lock in place. If a
special tool isn't available, needle-nose pliers can substitute.

When reinstalling the caliper, tighten the mounting bolts to the Specifications torque.
After the job is complete, firmly depress the brake pedal a few times to bring the pads into
contact with the disc. Check the brake fluid level, adding some if necessary. Check the
operation of the brakes carefully before placing the vehicle into normal service.

## 3. Disc brake caliper - removal, overhaul and installation (PDF p.198-200) <a id="p198"></a>

> **WARNING** — dust may contain asbestos, harmful to your health. Never blow it out with
> compressed air and don't inhale it — wear an approved filtering mask. Do not use
> petroleum-based solvents on brake parts — use brake cleaner or denatured alcohol only!

*Note (as printed): if an overhaul is indicated (usually fluid leakage), explore all options
first — new and factory-rebuilt calipers are available on an exchange basis, making this job
easier. If rebuilding, confirm a rebuild kit is available first. Always rebuild calipers in
pairs — never just one. Due to the relatively complex design of the rear brake
caliper/parking brake actuator assembly, all service procedures requiring disassembly/
reassembly should be left to a professional mechanic — the home mechanic can remove the
caliper and take it to a shop/dealer for repair, saving the removal/installation cost.*

**Removal** (illustration 3.1): disconnect the brake line from the caliper and plug it to
keep contaminants out and limit fluid loss — not necessary to disconnect the hose if just
removing the caliper for access to another component. Refer to Section 2 for front/rear
caliper removal procedures (part of the pad replacement procedure). If working on a rear
caliper, disconnect the parking brake cable from the parking brake crank on the underside of
the caliper.

**Overhaul** (illustrations 3.4a, 3.4b — rear brake caliper components exploded view: 1 brake
hose, 2 banjo fitting bolt, 3 sealing washer, 4 main pin bolt, 5 torque plate bolt, 6 caliper
bolt, 7 sliding bushing, 8 brake disc, 9 caliper bracket, 10 parking brake strut, 11 adjust
bolt, 12 adjusting bolt spring plate, 13 spring, 14 snap-ring, 15 piston seal, 16 retaining
ring, 17 piston dust boot, 18 piston, 19 adjusting bolt spring retainer, 20 adjusting bolt
stopper, 21 O-ring, 22 anti-rattle spring, 23 anti-squeal shim, 24 outer brake pad, 25 pad
guide, 26 inner brake pad, 27 anti-squeal shim, 28 dust boot, 29 sliding bushing, 30 stopper
pin, 31 parking brake spring, 32 parking brake lever, 33 brake caliper housing, 34 caliper
mounting bolt, 35 cable support bracket, 36 cable support bracket bolt, 37 bleeder screw;
3.4c, 3.5, 3.7, 3.8a, 3.8b): remove the piston dust boot retaining ring and dust boot. Before
removing the piston, place a wood block between the piston and caliper to prevent damage.

To remove the piston, apply compressed air to the brake fluid hose connection on the caliper
body — use only enough pressure to ease it out.

> **WARNING** — be careful not to place your fingers between the piston and caliper — the
> piston may come out with some force.

Inspect the mating surfaces of the piston and caliper bore wall — if there's any scoring,
rust, pitting, or bright areas, replace the complete caliper unit with a new one. If the
components are in good condition, remove the piston seal from the bore using a wooden or
plastic tool (illustration 3.7 — a pencil will do) — metal tools may damage the bore. Push
the sliding bushings out of the caliper ears and remove the dust boots from both ends; slide
the bushing sleeves out of the caliper ears (illustrations 3.8a, 3.8b). Wash all components
in clean brake fluid or brake cleaner.

To reassemble, use the correct rebuild kit for your vehicle. *Note: during reassembly, apply
silicone-based grease (supplied with the kit) between the sliding bushing and the bushing
sleeve.* Submerge the new piston seal and piston in brake fluid and install them into the
bore — don't force the piston in, but make sure it's squarely in place, then apply firm (not
excessive) pressure to install it. Install the new piston dust boot and retaining ring.
Lubricate the sliding bushings and sleeves with silicone-based grease (supplied in the kit)
and push them into the caliper ears; install the dust boots.

**Installation:** install the caliper by reversing the removal procedure — replace the
sealing washer on the brake line union bolt (comes with the rebuild kit). If installing the
rear caliper, don't forget to reattach the parking brake cable. Bleed the brake circuit per
Section 9.

## 4. Brake disc - inspection, removal and installation (PDF p.199-201) <a id="p199"></a>

*Note: this procedure applies to both front and rear brake discs.*

**Inspection** (illustrations 4.2, 4.3, 4.4a, 4.4b, 4.5a, 4.5b): loosen the wheel lug nuts,
raise and support the vehicle securely on jackstands. Remove the wheel and install the lug
nuts to hold the disc in place. If working on the rear disc, release the parking brake.
*Note: make sure the disc is mounted securely — if not tight on the hub, be sure the lug
nuts hold it securely so it doesn't wobble during the runout check; use washers on the wheel
studs to fill the space left by the removed wheel if necessary.* Remove the brake caliper
(Section 3) — not necessary to disconnect the brake hose; after removing the caliper bolts,
suspend it out of the way with wire (illustration 2.6c). Remove the two torque plate-to-
steering knuckle bolts (illustration 4.2) and detach the torque plate.

Visually inspect the disc surface for score marks and other damage — light scratches and
shallow grooves are normal and may not always be detrimental, but deep scoring over
0.015-inch (0.38 mm) requires disc removal and refinishing by a machine shop. Check both
sides of the disc (illustration 4.3). Suspect disc runout if pulsating has been noticed
during braking.

To check runout: first confirm hub bearing play is within specifications (Chapter 10). Place
a dial indicator about 1/2-inch from the outer edge of the disc (illustration 4.4a), zero it,
and turn the disc — the reading should not exceed the specified allowable runout limit; if it
does, the disc should be refinished by a machine shop. *Note (as printed): the discs should
be resurfaced regardless of the dial indicator reading — this imparts a smooth finish and a
perfectly flat surface, eliminating pedal pulsation or other symptoms related to questionable
discs. At the very least, if you elect not to resurface, remove the glaze from the surface
with sandpaper or emery cloth using a swirling motion (illustration 4.4b).*

It's absolutely critical that the disc not be machined to a thickness under the minimum
allowable refinish thickness. The minimum wear (discard) thickness is cast into the inside
of the disc (illustration 4.5a). Check disc thickness with a micrometer (illustration 4.5b).

**Removal** (illustration 4.6): remove the lug nuts put on earlier to hold the disc in place
and remove the disc from the hub. If stuck, thread bolts into the holes provided
(illustration 4.6) and tighten them — alternate between the bolts, turning 1/4-turn at a
time, until the disc is free.

**Installation:** place the disc in position over the threaded studs. Install the torque
plate and caliper assembly over the disc, position on the steering knuckle, tighten the
torque plate bolts to the Specifications torque. Install the wheel, lower the vehicle,
depress the brake pedal a few times to bring the pads into contact with the disc. Bleeding
won't be necessary unless the brake hose was disconnected from the caliper. Check the
operation of the brakes carefully before driving the vehicle.

## 5. Drum brake shoes - replacement (PDF p.201-204) <a id="p201"></a>

> **WARNING** — drum brake shoes must be replaced on both wheels at the same time — never
> replace shoes on only one wheel. Dust may contain asbestos, harmful to your health. Never
> blow it out with compressed air and don't inhale it — wear an approved filtering mask. Do
> not use petroleum-based solvents on brake parts — use brake cleaner or denatured alcohol
> only!

> *Caution: whenever brake shoes are replaced, the return and hold-down springs should also
> be replaced — they lose tension over time from continuous heating/cooling cycles and may
> allow the shoes to drag on the drum, wearing at a much faster rate than normal. Use only
> high-quality, nationally recognized brand-name parts.*

Loosen the wheel lug nuts, raise the rear, support securely on jackstands. Block the front
wheels. Release the parking brake. Remove the wheel. *Note: all four rear brake shoes must
be replaced at the same time, but to avoid mixing up parts, work on only one brake assembly
at a time.* Follow the illustration sequence 5.4a through 5.4x for the shoe replacement
procedure, staying in order and reading each caption:

- 5.4a: mark the relationship between the drum and hub so balance is retained.
- 5.4b: if the shoes are hanging up on the drum (excessive wear), insert two screwdrivers
  through the backing plate hole to push the adjuster lever off the star wheel, then turn the
  star wheel to retract the shoes.
- 5.4c: exploded view of the drum brake assembly (labeled: wheel cylinder, automatic
  adjusting lever, adjusting shim, rear shoe, return spring, parking brake shoe lever,
  C-washer, drum, inspection hole plug, strut, anchor spring, front shoe, adjusting lever
  spring, shoe hold-down cup, shoe hold-down spring).
- 5.4d: before removing anything, clean the assembly with brake cleaner and let it dry —
  position a drain pan under the brake to catch fluid/residue. **DO NOT USE COMPRESSED AIR TO
  BLOW THE DUST OFF THE PARTS!**
- 5.4e: unhook the return spring from the front shoe — locking pliers can stretch the spring
  and pull the end out of the hole in the shoe.
- 5.4f: depress the hold-down spring and turn the retainer 90°, then release it — pliers work,
  but a special hold-down spring removal tool (available at most auto parts stores,
  inexpensive) makes it much easier.
- 5.4g: remove the front shoe from the backing plate and unhook the anchor spring from the
  shoe end.
- 5.4h: remove the hold-down spring from the rear shoe.
- 5.4i: remove the rear shoe and adjuster assembly from the backing plate.
- 5.4j: pull back the spring on the parking brake cable and detach the cable from the lever.
- 5.4k: remove the adjuster lever spring [and the return spring from the adjuster, and spring
  off].
- 5.4m: pry the C-washer apart and remove it to separate the parking brake lever and
  adjusting lever from the rear shoe.
- 5.4n: attach the parking brake lever and adjuster lever to the new rear shoe and crimp the
  C-washer closed with pliers.
- 5.4o: lubricate the moving parts of the adjuster screw with a light coat of high-temperature
  grease.
- 5.4p: install the adjuster assembly on the rear shoe — make sure the end fits properly into
  the slot in the shoe and hook the spring into the opening in the shoe.
- 5.4q: install the adjuster lever spring.
- 5.4r: lubricate the brake shoe contact areas on the backing plate with high-temperature
  grease.
- 5.4s: pull the parking brake cable spring back and hold it with pliers, then place the cable
  in the hooked end of the parking brake lever.
- 5.4t: position the rear shoe assembly against the backing plate and push the hold-down
  spring pin through the shoe — install the retainers (one on each side of the spring) and
  lock the outer retainer on the pin by turning it 90° after the spring has been compressed.
- 5.4u: connect the anchor spring to the bottom of each shoe and mount the front shoe to the
  backing plate — install the hold-down spring into the hole of the front shoe.
- 5.4v: using a screwdriver, stretch the return spring into the hole in the front shoe.
- 5.4w: pry the parking brake lever forward and check that the return spring didn't come
  unhooked from the rear shoe.
- 5.4x: wiggle the brake shoe assembly to make sure it's seated properly against the backing
  plate.

*Note: if the brake drum can't be easily pulled off the axle/shoe assembly, make sure the
parking brake is completely released. If it still won't pull off, the shoes must be
retracted: remove the plug from the backing plate, push the lever off the adjusting star
wheel with a narrow screwdriver while turning the adjusting wheel with another screwdriver,
moving the shoes away from the drum (illustration 5.4b) — the drum should now come off.*

Before reinstalling the drum, check it for cracks, score marks, deep scratches, and hard
spots (discolored areas). If hard spots can't be removed with sandpaper/emery cloth, or any
other listed condition exists, the drum must go to a machine shop to be turned. *Note
(as printed): professionals recommend resurfacing the drums each time a brake job is done —
this eliminates out-of-round drums. If drums are worn so much they can't be resurfaced
without exceeding the maximum allowable diameter (stamped into the drum), new ones will be
required (illustration 5.5). At the very least, if not resurfacing, remove the glaze with
sandpaper or emery cloth using a swirling motion.*

Install the brake drum on the axle flange. Mount the wheel, install the lug nuts, lower the
vehicle, tighten the lug nuts to the Chapter 1 Specifications torque. Make a number of
forward and reverse stops and operate the parking brake to adjust the brakes until
satisfactory pedal action is obtained. Check the operation of the brakes carefully before
driving in traffic.

## 6. Wheel cylinder - removal, overhaul and installation (PDF p.204-205) <a id="p204"></a>

*Note (as printed): if an overhaul is indicated (usually fluid leaks or sticky operation),
explore all options — new wheel cylinders are available, making the job easier. If
rebuilding, confirm a rebuild kit is available first. Never overhaul only one wheel cylinder
— always rebuild both at the same time.*

**Removal** (illustration 6.4): raise the rear and support securely on jackstands. Block the
front wheels. Remove the brake shoe assembly (Section 5). Remove dirt/foreign material from
around the wheel cylinder. Disconnect the brake line with a flare-nut wrench if available —
don't pull the brake line away from the wheel cylinder. Remove the wheel cylinder mounting
bolts. Detach the wheel cylinder from the backing plate and place it on a clean workbench —
immediately plug the brake line to prevent fluid loss/contamination.

**Overhaul** (illustration 6.7 exploded view): remove the bleeder screw, cups, pistons,
boots, and spring assembly from the wheel cylinder body.

> **WARNING** — do not, under any circumstances, use petroleum-based solvents to clean brake
> parts!

Clean the wheel cylinder with brake fluid, denatured alcohol, or brake system cleaner. Use
compressed air to dry it and blow out the passages. Check the bore for corrosion and score
marks — crocus cloth can remove light corrosion/stains, but replace the cylinder with a new
one if defects can't be removed easily or the bore is scored. Lubricate the new cups with
brake fluid. Assemble the components (illustration 6.7) — make sure the cup lips face in.

**Installation:** place the wheel cylinder in position and install the bolts — don't tighten
completely until after the brake line fitting is tightened. Connect the brake line and
install the brake shoe assembly. Bleed the brakes (Section 9). Check the operation of the
brakes carefully before driving.

## 7. Master cylinder - removal, overhaul and installation (PDF p.205-207) <a id="p205"></a>

*Note (as printed): before deciding to overhaul, check the availability/cost of a new or
factory-rebuilt unit and a rebuild kit.*

**Removal** (illustration 7.4): the master cylinder is on the power brake booster in the
engine compartment. Remove as much fluid as possible from the reservoir with a syringe.
Place rags under the fittings and prepare caps/plastic bags for the disconnected line ends.

> *Caution: brake fluid will damage paint. Cover all body parts and be careful not to spill
> fluid during this procedure.*

Loosen the fittings where the brake lines enter the master cylinder using a flare-nut wrench
(prevents rounding off the flats). Pull the brake lines away and plug the ends to prevent
contamination. Disconnect the electrical connector at the master cylinder, then remove the
nuts attaching it to the power booster (illustration 7.4). Pull the master cylinder off the
studs — careful not to spill fluid.

**Overhaul** (illustrations 7.8a, 7.8b, 7.9, 7.10, 7.11a-7.11c): obtain the proper rebuild
kit with necessary parts and model-specific instructions first. Remove the reservoir
retaining screw, pull off the reservoir, remove the grommets (illustrations 7.8a — on some
models the reservoir is retained by a screw; 7.8b — pull the grommets off after removing the
reservoir; replace them if hardened, damaged, or appear to have been leaking). Place the
cylinder in a vise and use a punch or Phillips screwdriver to depress the pistons until they
bottom against the other end — hold them there and remove the stopper bolt (illustration
7.9). Carefully remove the snap-ring at the end of the master cylinder (illustration 7.10).
The internal components can now be removed (illustration 7.11a exploded view, early models
shown: cap, strainer, reservoir, grommet, housing, sealing washer, piston stopper bolt,
reservoir retainer screw, secondary piston and spring, primary piston and spring, snap-ring,
boot — *later models slightly different*) — note the proper order for reinstallation. *Note:
the two springs are different — pay attention to their installed order.*

Carefully inspect the bore for deep score marks or other damage — a new master cylinder is
required if found. **DO NOT attempt to hone the bore.** Replace all parts included in the
rebuild kit per its instructions. Clean reused parts with new brake fluid, brake system
cleaner, or denatured alcohol — no petroleum-based solvents. During reassembly, lubricate
all parts liberally with clean brake fluid. Push the assembled components into the bore,
bottoming against the end, then install the stopper bolt. *Be sure to replace the copper
washer on the stopper bolt before installation.* Install the new snap-ring, seated properly
in the groove (illustration 7.11b — after the snap-ring is removed, the primary piston
assembly can be withdrawn). Install the reservoir grommets, reservoir, and screw.

Before installing, bench-bleed the master cylinder — mount it in a vise clamping the
mounting flange (you'll apply pressure to the piston while controlling flow from the brake
line outlets). Insert threaded plugs into the brake line outlet holes, snugged down enough to
prevent leakage but still easily loosened. Fill the reservoir with the recommended fluid type
(Chapter 1). Remove one plug and push the piston assembly into the bore to expel air — a
large Phillips screwdriver can push on the piston assembly. To prevent air being drawn back
in, the plug must be replaced and snugged before releasing pressure on the piston. Repeat
until only brake fluid is expelled from the outlet hole, then repeat at the other outlet and
plug — keep the reservoir filled to prevent air introduction.

*Alternative (since high pressure isn't involved in bench bleeding): instead of removing and
replacing the plugs each stroke, remove the plug once, then before releasing the piston, put
your finger tightly over the hole instead of replacing the plug — wait several seconds for
fluid to draw into the bore, then depress the piston again, removing your finger as fluid is
expelled. Put your finger back over the hole each time before releasing the piston, and once
bleeding at that outlet is complete, replace and tighten the plug before moving to the other
port.*

**Installation** (illustration 7.27): install the master cylinder over the studs on the
power booster and tighten the nuts only finger-tight for now. Thread the brake line fittings
in — the cylinder can move slightly since it's still loose; don't strip the threads. Tighten
the mounting nuts and brake line fittings. Fill the reservoir with fluid, then bleed the
master cylinder (only if not bench-bled) and the brake system per Section 9. To bleed the
master cylinder on the vehicle: have an assistant depress the brake pedal and hold it down;
loosen the fitting to let air/fluid escape; tighten the fitting, then let the assistant
return the pedal to rest. Repeat on both fittings until fluid is free of air bubbles
(illustration 7.27). Check the brake system's operation carefully before driving.

## 8. Brake hoses and lines - inspection and replacement (PDF p.208-209) <a id="p208"></a>

**Inspection:** about every six months, with the vehicle raised and supported securely,
inspect the rubber hoses connecting the steel brake lines to the front and rear brake
assemblies for cracks, chafing of the outer cover, leaks, blisters, and other damage — a
light and mirror help. Replace any hose showing these conditions.

**Replacement — front brake hose** (illustrations 8.3, 8.4): loosen the wheel lug nuts,
raise and support the vehicle, remove the wheel. At the frame bracket, hold the hose fitting
with an open-end wrench and unscrew the brake line fitting from the hose (illustration 8.3)
— use a flare-nut wrench to prevent rounding the corners. Remove the U-clip from the female
fitting at the bracket with pliers, then pass the hose through the bracket (illustration
8.4). At the caliper end, remove the banjo fitting bolt and separate the hose from the
caliper — note the two copper sealing washers on either side of the fitting, replace them
with new ones on installation. Remove the U-clip from the strut bracket, then feed the hose
through the bracket.

To install: pass the caliper fitting end through the strut bracket, connect the fitting to
the caliper with the banjo bolt and copper washers — make sure the locating lug on the
fitting engages the hole in the caliper, then tighten the bolt to the Specifications torque.
Push the metal support into the strut bracket and install the U-clip — make sure the hose
isn't twisted between the caliper and strut bracket. Route the hose into the frame bracket
(again, not twisted), connect the brake line fitting starting the threads by hand, install
the U-clip, tighten the fitting securely. Bleed the caliper (Section 9). Install the wheel
and lug nuts, lower the vehicle, tighten to the Chapter 1 Specifications torque.

**Rear brake hose:** perform Steps 2, 3, and 4 above, then repeat Steps 3 and 4 at the other
end of the hose. Bleed the wheel cylinder (or caliper) per Section 9.

**Metal brake lines:** use the correct parts when replacing — don't use copper tubing for
any brake system components; purchase steel brake lines from a dealer or auto parts store.
Prefabricated brake line, with ends already flared and fittings installed and bent to proper
shapes, is available at auto parts stores and dealers. Ensure the new line is securely
supported in the brackets with plenty of clearance from moving/hot components. After
installation, check the master cylinder fluid level and add fluid as necessary. Bleed the
brake system (Section 9) and test the brakes carefully before driving in traffic.

## 9. Brake hydraulic system - bleeding (PDF p.209-210) <a id="p209"></a>

> **WARNING** — wear eye protection when bleeding the brake system. If fluid contacts your
> eyes, immediately rinse with water and seek medical attention.

*Note: bleeding removes air that enters the system when it's opened during removal/
installation of a hose, line, caliper, or master cylinder.*

Bleed all four brakes if air entered due to low fluid level, or if the brake lines were
disconnected at the master cylinder. If a brake line was disconnected only at a wheel, just
that caliper or wheel cylinder needs bleeding. If a line is disconnected at a fitting between
the master cylinder and any brake, that part of the system served by the disconnected line
must be bled. Remove any residual vacuum from the power booster by applying the brake
several times with the engine off. Remove the master cylinder reservoir cover, fill with
brake fluid, reinstall the cover. *Note: check the fluid level often during bleeding and add
as necessary to prevent it from falling low enough to let air bubbles into the master
cylinder.*

Have an assistant on hand, plus a supply of new brake fluid, a clear plastic container
partially filled with clean brake fluid, a length of 3/16-inch plastic/rubber/vinyl tubing to
fit over the bleeder valve, and a wrench to open/close it. Beginning at the right rear wheel,
loosen the bleeder valve slightly, then tighten to a point where it's snug but can still be
loosened quickly and easily. Place one end of the tubing over the bleeder valve and submerge
the other end in brake fluid in the container (illustration 9.8). Have the assistant pump the
brakes slowly a few times to build pressure, then hold the pedal down firmly. While held down,
open the bleeder valve just enough to allow fluid flow — watch for air bubbles exiting the
submerged tube end. When flow slows after a couple of seconds, close the valve and have the
assistant release the pedal. Repeat until no more air is seen leaving the tube, then tighten
the bleeder valve and proceed to the left front, left rear, and right front wheels in that
order, performing the same procedure. Check the master cylinder reservoir fluid frequently.

> *Never use old brake fluid — it contains moisture that will deteriorate brake system
> components.*

Refill the master cylinder with fluid at the end of the operation. Check the operation of the
brakes — the pedal should feel solid when depressed, with no sponginess. If necessary, repeat
the entire process.

> **WARNING** — do not operate the vehicle if you're in doubt about the effectiveness of the
> brake system.

## 10. Power brake booster - check, removal and installation (PDF p.210-212) <a id="p210"></a>

**Operating check:** depress the brake pedal several times with the engine off and confirm
there's no change in the pedal reserve distance (distance between the pedal and the floor).
Depress the pedal and start the engine — if the pedal goes down slightly, operation is
normal.

**Airtightness check:** start the engine and turn it off after one or two minutes. Depress
the brake pedal slowly several times — if the pedal depresses less each time, the booster is
airtight. Depress the pedal while the engine is running, then stop the engine with the pedal
depressed — if there's no change in pedal reserve travel after holding for 30 seconds, the
booster is airtight.

**Removal** (illustration 10.7): *Note (as printed): power brake booster units shouldn't be
disassembled — they require special tools not normally found in most repair shops, are
fairly complex, and because of their critical relationship to brake performance, should be
replaced with a new or rebuilt one if faulty.* To remove: first remove the brake master
cylinder (Section 7). Locate the pushrod clevis connecting the booster to the brake pedal —
accessible from inside the vehicle, under the dash on the driver's side. Remove the clevis
pin retaining clip with pliers and pull out the pin. Holding the clevis with pliers, unscrew
the locknut with a wrench — the clevis is now loose. Disconnect the hose leading from the
engine to the booster — careful not to damage the hose when removing it from the booster
fitting. Remove the four nuts and washers holding the booster to the firewall (a light may
help see them). Slide the booster straight out from the firewall until the studs clear the
holes.

**Installation** (illustrations 10.14a, 10.14b): basically the reverse of removal. Tighten
the clevis locknut securely and the booster mounting nuts to the specified torque. If the
booster is being replaced, the clearance between the master cylinder piston and the pushrod
in the vacuum booster must be measured and, if necessary, adjusted: using a depth micrometer
or vernier calipers, measure the distance from the seat (recessed area) in the master
cylinder to the master cylinder mounting flange. Next, measure the distance from the end of
the vacuum booster pushrod to the mounting face of the booster (including gasket) where the
master cylinder mounting flange seats. Subtract the two measurements to get the clearance
(illustration 10.14a — *if there's interference between the two, the brakes may drag; if
there's too much clearance, excessive brake pedal travel will result*). If the clearance is
more or less than the Specifications value, turn the adjusting screw on the end of the
booster pushrod until it's within the specified limit (illustration 10.14b — hold the
serrated portion of the rod with pliers and turn the adjusting screw in or out as necessary).

After final installation of the master cylinder and brake hoses/lines, the brake pedal height
and freeplay must be adjusted and the system bled — see the appropriate Sections of this
Chapter.

## 11. Parking brake - adjustment (PDF p.212) <a id="p212"></a>

The parking brake lever, when properly adjusted, should travel five to eight clicks when a
moderate pulling force is applied. Fewer than five clicks risks the parking brake not
releasing completely and dragging on the drum. More than eight clicks risks the parking
brake not holding adequately on an incline, allowing the car to roll.

To gain access to the parking brake cable adjuster, remove the center console. Loosen the
locknut (upper nut) while holding the adjusting nut (lower nut) with a wrench (illustration
11.3). Tighten the adjusting nut until the desired travel is attained. Tighten the locknut.
Install the console.

## 12. Parking brake cables - replacement (PDF p.212-214) <a id="p212"></a>

**Equalizer-to-parking brake cable** (illustrations 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 12.7, 12.8):
loosen the rear wheel lug nuts, raise and support the vehicle securely on jackstands, block
the front wheels, remove the wheel. Confirm the parking brake is completely released, then
remove the brake drum (or disc). If the vehicle has rear drum brakes, remove the brake shoes
and disconnect the cable from the parking brake lever (Section 5), then unbolt the cable
housing from the backing plate (illustration 12.3 — the housing is bolted to the backing
plate on drum brake models). If the vehicle has rear disc brakes, detach the cable from the
parking brake levers on the undersides of the calipers (illustration 12.4 — remove the clevis
pin). Unbolt the cable bracket from the frame at the forward end of the strut rod.

Unbolt the cable bracket from the forward end of the fuel tank strap (illustration 12.6).
Follow the cable toward the front of the vehicle and locate the cable clamp (illustration
12.7) — loosen the clamp bolt and slide the cable housing out. Pry the cable and grommet out
of the guide just to the rear of the equalizer (illustration 12.8). Disconnect the cable end
from the equalizer by aligning the cable with the slot in the top of the equalizer and
sliding the cable end out of the hole.

To install: reverse the removal procedure. Adjust the parking brake lever per Section 11.

**Equalizer-to-brake lever cable** (illustration 12.13): remove the center console. With the
lever in the down position, remove the locknut and adjusting nut (Section 11) and detach the
cable from the lever. Working under the vehicle, pull the cable to the rear, turn it 90°, and
pass it through the center of the equalizer. Installation is the reverse of removal — apply a
light coat of grease to the portion of the cable end that contacts the equalizer. Adjust the
parking brake lever per Section 11.

## 13. Brake pedal - removal, installation and adjustment (PDF p.214-215) <a id="p214"></a>

**Removal and installation** (illustrations 13.6, 13.11, 13.13): remove the left side
under-dash panel. Disconnect the return spring from the outer groove in the pushrod clevis
pin. Remove the clip and extract the clevis pin. Unscrew the pivot bolt nut, withdraw the
bolt, and remove the pedal. Inspect the bushings for wear, replace if necessary. Install the
brake pedal in the reverse order of removal — lubricate the pivot bolt with grease.

**Adjustment:** pedal height is measured from the floor to the top of the pedal (illustration
13.6). Compare to this Chapter's Specifications. To adjust the pedal height: block the
wheels and release the parking brake lever. Loosen the locknut on the brake light switch and
unscrew it until it no longer contacts the brake pedal shaft. Depress the pedal a few times
to remove any vacuum in the system. Loosen the pushrod locknut and turn the rod in the
desired direction to set the pedal.

Check the brake pedal freeplay (illustration 13.11 — the distance the pedal moves before the
pushrod contacts the booster air valve): press on the pedal with your fingers until initial
resistance is felt, compare to Specifications. If the freeplay is incorrect, recheck the
pedal height and adjust accordingly.

Check brake pedal reserve travel (illustration 13.13): start the engine, depress the brake
pedal a few times, then press down hard and hold it. Pedal reserve travel is measured from
the floor to the top of the pedal while depressed — compare to Specifications. If reserve is
less than specified, have the brakes adjusted by a dealer or repair shop. Readjust the brake
light switch so it's actuated when the pedal is up, not depressed (Section 14).

## 14. Brake light switch - removal, installation and adjustment (PDF p.215) <a id="p215"></a>

**Removal and installation** (illustration 14.1: 1 brake light switch, 2 brake pedal): the
